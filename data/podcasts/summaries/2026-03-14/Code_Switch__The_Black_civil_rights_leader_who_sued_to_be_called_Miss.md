# Code Switch — "The Black civil rights leader who sued to be called 'Miss'"

**Host:** Gene Demby. **Guests:** Camila Domonoske (NPR reporter, original 2017 episode; discovered Mary Hamilton's story while writing an obituary for Sheila Michaels); Barbara McCaskill (English professor, University of Georgia; scholar of Black American narratives and civil rights movement literature; her own mother vividly remembered not being called "Miss"); Holly Wesley (Mary Hamilton's daughter); Sheila Michaels (via archival recordings — white civil rights activist, Mary Hamilton's roommate and lifelong friend, credited with launching the Ms. movement, recorded 80+ oral histories of civil rights activists before her death in 2017). **Original co-host (2017 episode):** Shireen Marisol-Moragi.

## TL;DR

- **Mary Hamilton, a Black civil rights activist, was jailed and fined $50 in 1963 for refusing to answer a white Alabama prosecutor unless he called her "Miss Hamilton"** — the case, *Hamilton v. Alabama*, went to the Supreme Court, which ruled unanimously without oral arguments that calling only Black witnesses by their first names was discriminatory.
- **The Supreme Court's ruling in *Hamilton v. Alabama* (1964) stands as binding precedent** — law students today are still taught to use honorifics for everyone in court as a direct result of the case.
- **Hamilton heard of her Supreme Court victory from a hospital bed** — years of beatings and violence had left her health broken; she was in the hospital recovering when the 1964 decision came down.
- **Sheila Michaels, Hamilton's white roommate and fellow CORE activist, launched the "Ms." movement after seeing the honorific on a piece of mail addressed to Hamilton** — a socialist Detroit magazine had addressed Hamilton as "Ms. Mary Hamilton" around 1961, and Michaels spent years pushing the title, eventually inspiring *Ms. Magazine*.
- **Hamilton refused to pass as white even when her family did** — some family members used an Italian surname to pass; Hamilton dropped the surname and "identified strongly with being black," per her daughter Holly Wesley.
- **"There would not have been a movement without the women"** — Hamilton told Michaels in oral history recordings, yet Black women's leadership roles were erased even contemporaneously; Hamilton was on the cover of *Jet Magazine* in her time but largely forgotten by her death in 2002.
- **Hamilton filed a sexual harassment complaint directly to Martin Luther King Jr. about a drunken SCLC member in her bed** — King's response was "Now, Bernard, you get out of that room and you leave that little girl alone"; King later also made advances on Hamilton at a Memphis conference.

**Mary Hamilton: Activist, Freedom Rider, and Reluctant Fighter**

Mary Hamilton was born and raised in Iowa and Colorado in the 1940s and '50s. She considered becoming a nun, then discovered socialism in college, dropped out, became a teacher, and joined the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) — one of the "big six" civil rights organizations alongside the NAACP, SNCC, National Urban League, and the SCLC — after hearing about the burgeoning Southern movement. She became a Freedom Rider. The training, she recalled in oral history recordings made with Sheila Michaels in the late 1990s, was "getting into a fetal position" — learning how to be beaten without striking back. Other activists called her "red," not for her hair but for her temper. "We had to practice nonviolence because if we didn't, we'd get the shit kicked out of us," she told Michaels. After her Freedom Rider work, she drove a white Plymouth Valiant through the Deep South, registering voters, fundraising, and building new CORE chapters from scratch.

**Violence, Assault, and Defiance in Southern Jails**

Hamilton was jailed repeatedly and beaten. She described being thrown into an elevator and run up and down while jailers beat her, screaming "scream, scream, so nobody can hear you." She refused. "I wouldn't give them the satisfaction. I didn't scream at all." Jailers subjected her and other women activists to demands for vaginal and anal examinations, and Hamilton stared down guards she believed were planning to rape her: "I said, you have to kill me first. I will not be raped." She also faced sexism within the movement — men making unwanted advances, a drunken SCLC member who would not leave her bed, and rumors spread about her sexual orientation after she rebuffed a colleague.

**The Meaning of "Miss" Under Segregation**

Barbara McCaskill, who studies Black American narratives at the University of Georgia, explains that withholding honorifics from Black people was a deliberate instrument of racial hierarchy. "Segregation was in the details as much as it was in the bold strokes," McCaskill says. "The idea was to remind African Americans and people of color in general, in every possible way, that we were inferior." Gene Demby notes that after the Civil War, some Black families gave their children names that were themselves honorifics — "Mr.," "Colonel," "Master" — so white people addressing them would be forced to use the title. Generations later, Hamilton was fighting the same battle: white Southerners in authority addressed grown Black men as "boy" and Black women as "girl," or called them by first name regardless of the formality of the context, even as white women were addressed as "Miss" or "Mrs."

**The Courtroom Confrontation and *Hamilton v. Alabama***

In June 1963, Hamilton was arrested in Gadsden, Alabama during a protest. When the state's attorney opened by saying "Mary, what are you here for?" she responded: "I will not answer you until you call me Miss Hamilton." The judge — whom Hamilton described as "a little man" in a big chair, his foot up on a hand-carved mahogany desk — ordered her to apologize. Her attorney quietly told her she did not have to. She didn't. She was sentenced to several days in jail for contempt of court and fined $50. Her lawyers argued the prosecution had violated her constitutional rights by treating her differently than white witnesses. The case, *Hamilton v. Alabama*, was appealed repeatedly and reached the Supreme Court, which ruled unanimously in 1964 — without even calling for oral arguments — that Alabama was wrong. Calling only Black witnesses by their first names in court was discriminatory. The precedent remains in force; law students today are taught to use honorifics for everyone in court.

**The "Ms." Connection: Two Women, Two Parallel Fights**

Around 1961, when Hamilton and Sheila Michaels were roommates in New York and both working with CORE, a socialist magazine from Detroit sent mail addressed to "Ms. Mary Hamilton." The title existed as an obscure business-correspondence option for when the marital status of a woman was unknown, but women did not use it to describe themselves. Michaels saw it and thought it could be a way for women to avoid being defined by their marital status. Hamilton was unimpressed — "Sheila, we have much more important things to be working on" — focused as she was on the civil rights struggle. After the movement wound down, Michaels transitioned into feminist activism, a path she credited directly to her civil rights work: "For me, that was the seeds of feminism. If I hadn't been in SNCC, I don't know that I would have been ready to just jump in." She used her feminist platform to champion the "Ms." title, which eventually inspired *Ms. Magazine*.

**Erasure: Who Gets Written Into History**

When Sheila Michaels died in 2017, her obituaries focused almost entirely on her role in popularizing "Ms." — Mary Hamilton appeared as a footnote, "the roommate who got the mail," her name misspelled in some accounts. Domonoske discovered the Hamilton story only while fact-checking Hamilton's name for a Michaels obituary, eventually connecting with Andrew Yeager, an NPR reporter in Alabama. Hamilton had been famous in her day — covered in speeches, her story in the press, her face on the cover of *Jet Magazine* — but by her death in 2002 from ovarian cancer, she had been largely forgotten. Even in her final illness, she was reportedly chastising hospital workers in her oncology ward for not being unionized. Sheila Michaels had recorded more than 80 oral histories of civil rights activists specifically to prevent this kind of erasure. The irony, as Domonoske notes, is that Sheila's story is widely remembered while Mary's — the story that directly inspired Sheila's most famous act — almost wasn't. Hamilton had put it plainly: "There would not have been a movement without the women. And yet and still, the women are ignored when the kudos are."
