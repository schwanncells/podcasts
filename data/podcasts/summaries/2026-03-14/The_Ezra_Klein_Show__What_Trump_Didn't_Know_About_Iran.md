# The Ezra Klein Show — "What Trump Didn't Know About Iran"

**Host:** Ezra Klein. **Guest:** Ali Vaez (Iran Project Director, International Crisis Group; nuclear scientist; involved in negotiations that led to the 2015 JCPOA; co-author of *How Sanctions Work: Iran and the Impact of Economic Warfare*).

## TL;DR

- **Trump's war with Iran appears to lack any plan at all** — Klein describes "an almost absence of planning or information," noting the administration simultaneously believed Iranians would rise up AND that senior regime figures might cut a deal, while Trump himself said the leading candidates for negotiation "were killed in the initial attacks."
- **Iran's nuclear breakout time collapsed from 12+ months under the JCPOA to six days when Trump took office in January 2025** — maximum pressure not only failed to curb the program but accelerated it to levels "we could not even imagine in the past," including enrichment to 60% and advanced centrifuges.
- **The JCPOA worked while it lasted** — Iran shipped out 97% of its nuclear stockpile, dismantled most centrifuges, and accepted inspections "no other country in the world has ever accepted"; 15 consecutive IAEA reports confirmed full compliance until Iran began rolling back commitments a year after the U.S. withdrew.
- **Iran's proxy strategy was born from "strategic solitude"** — the 1980–88 Iran-Iraq war, in which Iraq was backed by the West and Gulf states while Iran fought alone, created the doctrine of maintaining proxies far from Iran's borders as a deterrent.
- **Khamenei became supreme leader as an underdog** — he wasn't even an Ayatollah and "they had to overnight make him an Ayatollah"; kingmaker Rafsanjani installed him believing he'd remain controllable, but Khamenei "managed to outwit and outwait everybody else" by allying with the Revolutionary Guards.
- **The Kushner-Witkoff negotiations were "always doomed to fail"** — Vaez says negotiator Steve Witkoff "was surprised that the Iranians were able to manufacture their own centrifuges" and described an IR-6 centrifuge as "probably the most powerful centrifuge in the world, which is not true."
- **Iran's current strategy is to outlast, not outfight** — Iran believes it has "a higher threshold for pain," noting a thousand Iranian casualties in the 12-day war were portrayed as victory, while equivalent American or Israeli casualties could not be.
- **If the regime survives, it will almost certainly pursue nuclear weapons** — Vaez says "the religious edict that Ayatollah Khamenei had against nuclear weapons probably died with him" and a younger Khamenei successor would see every historical precedent (Libya, Ukraine, Iraq) pointing toward the bomb as "the only way to create a shield."

---

**The 1979 Revolution, the Hostage Crisis, and the Roots of Enmity**

Vaez, who grew up in Iran during the Iran-Iraq war, explains that pre-revolution Iran was prosperous with strong international relations — "the Shah really didn't have any serious enemies" — but lacked political freedom. Ayatollah Khomeini positioned himself as a transitional figure, promising women's rights and banning clerics from politics, which allowed leftists, Maoists, conservatives, and religious groups to coalesce behind him. Once three million people welcomed him in Tehran, "he realized that his power is basically unchallenged" and began purging the coalition.

The 1979 embassy hostage crisis created a "rupture in Iran-U.S. relationship that has not been healed in the past 47 years." But it traces back to the 1953 CIA-backed coup against Prime Minister Mossadegh — "every schoolchild in Iran has heard of this event." Khomeini used the crisis to eliminate moderate forces: "the entire government resigned, and he could bring his own people to power."

**The Iran-Iraq War and the Birth of the Proxy Doctrine**

Saddam Hussein invaded Iran in 1980, backed by Gulf monarchies, world powers, and tacitly the United States, while Iran fought alone — a calculation Vaez compares to "Putin's calculation in the Ukraine war in 2022, that this would be a quick win." This "sense of strategic solitude" birthed Iran's proxy strategy and ballistic missile program. The war also created the Revolutionary Guards, whose very name excludes "Iran" because it was "designed to safeguard the revolution." The eight-year trench war, featuring chemical weapons, consolidated the regime's power and created "real hard men with very fixed views."

**Khamenei's Rise and the Militarization of Iranian Politics**

After Khomeini's death in 1989, Rafsanjani installed Khamenei as a puppet successor — he was so junior "they had to overnight make him an Ayatollah." But Khamenei allied with the Revolutionary Guards to compensate for his lack of religious credentials, "militarizing Iranian politics in ways that Khomeini had actually banned." The 1997 election of reformist President Khatami raised hopes, but the deep state crushed reform. Vaez voted in that election — "the first election that I voted in and the last election I voted in." Khatami's failure was "the beginning of a lot of people losing hope in this regime's ability to change course."

**Iran, Israel, and the Palestinian Cause as Geopolitical Instrument**

Iran adopted the Palestinian cause as a strategic tool to "transcend all of these inherent limitations" as a Shia, Persian nation surrounded by Sunnis and Arabs. Vaez argues the ideological veneer masked geopolitical calculation: Iran "was willing to fight Israel to the last Palestinian or the last Arab, but they really did not care much about the Palestinian cause." Israel only reoriented its threat perception from Iraq to Iran after the first Gulf War neutralized Saddam — before that, Israel had actually sold weapons to Iran during the Iran-Iraq war.

**The Post-9/11 Missed Opportunity and the Axis of Evil**

Qasem Soleimani — the same commander Trump assassinated in 2020 — "was the first man to arrive in Afghanistan to prepare it for U.S. fighter jets to land" after 9/11. Iran cooperated with the U.S. on intelligence and military operations against the Taliban, believing it would begin "a new chapter." Bush's 2002 "Axis of Evil" speech shut that door. North Korea responded by going nuclear; Iraq, which had no nuclear weapons, was invaded; Libya gave up its program and Gaddafi "died in a ditch." These lessons shaped Iranian nuclear thinking for decades.

**The JCPOA: Theory, Achievement, and Destruction**

The nuclear deal was "a 159-page, very complex document" that "really boils down to a very simple bargain: nuclear restrictions and transparency measures in return for economic incentives." Iran shipped out 97% of its stockpile, dismantled most centrifuges, and accepted unprecedented inspections. Vaez's theory of change: 5% economic growth over 10 years would expand Iran's pro-Western middle class from 65% to 80-85%, coinciding with the original revolutionary elite dying off naturally — "these two lines will cross one another."

Trump withdrew from the deal and imposed maximum pressure, which "completely changed the dynamics and weakened our best allies and strengthened our worst adversaries in that system." Iran's nuclear breakout time went from over 12 months to six days. The Revolutionary Guards became "even more powerful than before."

**The Chain of Miscalculations Leading to War**

Iran tested its "Ring of Fire" concept — four simultaneous fronts against Israel — in April 2023 and concluded "they're not ready," but failed to communicate this to Hamas's Sinwar. Soleimani's assassination in 2020 had removed the one leader with the "charisma and the authority" to restrain proxy freelancing. Khamenei then compounded errors: endorsing October 7th while trying to distance Iran from it, failing to restrain Hezbollah, and eventually firing hundreds of missiles at Israel in April 2024 — crossing a red line while "telegraphing it in advance" in a way that "didn't really signal strength."

**Iran's Endgame: Outlasting, Not Outfighting**

Iran's current strategy is attritional: spreading pain to Gulf states, disrupting energy exports (oil could go "above $200 a barrel"), and depleting interceptor arsenals. Vaez notes Iran lost about a thousand citizens in the 12-day war and "portrayed that war as a victory because it survived." The regime believes "they can outlast Israel and the United States." If it survives, Russia and China may go "all in" supporting Iran as a shield against U.S. dominance in the Middle East, turning the country into "an arena of great power competition."

**The Absence of an American Plan**

Vaez concludes that the U.S. "followed Israel into this and was hoping that the day after would arrive very quickly and would magically work." Israel is comfortable with any outcome — weakened Iran, collapsed Iran, or restored monarchy — but the U.S. has not considered the long tail. Trump's Venezuela model requires pre-negotiated transitions, but he has "burned the Iranians three times now." The most likely outcome resembles Iraq post-1991: a defeated but surviving regime, a shredded social fabric, and containment that "comes back to haunt you down the road." Vaez's final assessment: "hope is not a strategy."
