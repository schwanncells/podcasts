# The Indicator from Planet Money — "Can anything save the news biz?"

**Host:** Adrian Ma, Waylon Wong. **Guest:** Ken Docter (media analyst; founder of Lookout Santa Cruz; entrepreneur rebuilding local journalism business models).

## TL;DR

- **More than 3,000 local newspapers have closed in the past couple decades** — Google and Yahoo siphoned advertising revenue; social media and streaming fractured consumer attention; the dual-revenue model supporting journalism for centuries collapsed.
- **Lookout Santa Cruz raised $2.5 million to build a full newsroom in a 260,000-person county** — Ken Docter's 10-person startup grew to reach about half the adults in Santa Cruz County monthly; won a Pulitzer Prize for flood coverage in 2024.
- **Earned revenue strategy: subscriptions, ads, events, and commissioned "promoted content"** — majority of Lookout's revenue is now earned rather than philanthropic; diversified streams reduce reliance on donor funding.
- **Deep South Today operates three nonprofit newsrooms (Mississippi, Louisiana) without paywalls** — their investigative reporting freed a man from prison and exposed a governor's misuse of welfare funds; planning to expand to Arkansas.
- **Economies of scale power nonprofit expansion** — shared HR, tech, and sales functions lower per-unit journalism costs; more newsrooms attract more readers, advertisers, and donors to a single network.

---

**The Structural Collapse of the Traditional Journalism Business**

Docter witnessed the transformation firsthand as a journalist and media analyst. Newspapers thrived on two revenue streams for centuries: readers and advertisers. The Internet decimated both. Search engines and social platforms captured advertising; streaming and digital entertainment captured consumer attention. The Santa Cruz Sentinel, once the region's primary news source, became "a shell of its former self" under hedge fund ownership by the time Docter arrived in 2019. Thousands of journalists have been laid off; the void has filled with misinformation, paid influencers, and AI-generated content. Yet Docter remained convinced the core business model could work if restructured correctly.

**Lookout Santa Cruz: Capital and Diversified Revenue**

Docter launched Lookout in 2020 with 10 reporters, banking on the hypothesis that readers and advertisers would support quality local journalism if given the chance. Philanthropic funders initially balked at his $2.5 million target for a county of 260,000 residents, but Docter argued that replacing a daily newspaper as "a source of truth in the community" required sufficient reporting depth across city, county, education, school boards, universities, public health, arts, food, and sports. The gamble paid off: Lookout now reaches approximately half the county's adults each month and secured a Pulitzer Prize for its flood coverage. Expansion to Eugene, Oregon followed, with three additional newsrooms planned.

Revenue comes from multiple sources: subscriptions, display advertising, hosted events, and "promoted content" (clearly labeled advertiser-commissioned articles). Docter stresses the majority of revenue is now earned rather than philanthropic-dependent, with ongoing growth anticipated. "It is no longer in any of our minds an experiment," Docter stated, adding "there is enough money, if you execute correctly, to serve these communities."

**Deep South Today: Nonprofit Scale and Free Access**

Deep South Today operates three newsrooms—Mississippi Today, Verite News (New Orleans), and a Louisiana-based outlet—publishing all content without paywalls. CEO Warwick Saban emphasizes reaching underserved populations who lack access to city council meetings, school board coverage, or accountability reporting. The network's impact includes a 2023 Pulitzer Prize for reporting on a former governor's theft of welfare funds and a 2025 investigation (with ProPublica) that freed a wrongly convicted man by exposing junk science in his trial. A fourth newsroom in Arkansas is planned.

The model relies on network effects: scaling newsrooms lowers the per-unit cost of journalism while attracting more readers, advertisers, and donors to a shared revenue pool. HR, tech, and sales functions are centralized. Saban expects "per unit cost of doing the journalism obviously decreases" as the network grows, creating sustainability without paywalls.

**A Path Forward for Local News**

Both Lookout and Deep South Today demonstrate that independent, accountability-focused local journalism can survive and grow if structured around earned revenue, diversified funding streams, or nonprofit networks. Partnerships with larger outlets like the New York Times and Associated Press, as well as local public radio stations, extend reach without proportional cost increases. These models suggest that the decline of legacy media need not be the final chapter for fact-based local reporting.
