# The Economics of Everyday Things — "22. Cadavers - Part 1"

**Host:** Zachary Crockett. **Guests:** Susan Lawrence (historian, University of Tennessee; head of history department; studies cadaver history); Kaylin Goodwin (vice president of marketing, ScienceCare; world's largest for-profit body donation program).

## TL;DR

- **Medical schools place more than 45,000 anatomical specimens annually across 50 countries** — serving research, training, and education in a lucrative for-profit cadaver industry that emerged after legal body donation became possible in 1968.
- **Grave robbing was the main supply in the 1700s–1800s** — gangs unearthed executed criminals and the poor, selling bodies for around $1,000 (in today's money); two Scottish killers murdered ~16 people over 10 months to provide fresh specimens.
- **American medical schools used enslaved and poor African American bodies without much political outcry** — public uproar only erupted when white bodies were robbed, prompting states to grant schools access to unclaimed bodies from prisons, hospitals, and streets.
- **Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (1968) legalized whole-body donation** — unintentionally enabled for-profit companies to collect and sell cadavers with minimal oversight, unlike strictly regulated live organ donation.
- **ScienceCare has 257,000+ registered donors and generates $5,000–$10,000 per cadaver** — body parts (forearms, torsos, heads, knees) are sold separately to medical device manufacturers, researchers, and surgical training programs; free cremation offered as incentive.
- **Donors often don't know their bodies will be parted out** — for-profit companies operate under a "service, not product" framing; in 2022, a box of human heads was stolen from a ScienceCare cargo truck in Denver.
- **Unlike live organ donation, cadaver sales are largely unregulated** — no laws prohibit selling bodies; ScienceCare voluntarily sought accreditation from the American Association of Tissue Banks but it is not a legal requirement.
- **Historian Susan Lawrence fears the social contract around body donation could break** — concerns about scandals and abuse of body parts in an unregulated market could undermine the future of voluntary donation programs.

**From Taboo to Commodity: The Medieval and Early Modern History of Cadaver Supply**

Modern anatomy began in the 14th century, when humans first had legal access to human corpses for dissection. Susan Lawrence explains that in the late Middle Ages, being dissected was considered the ultimate punishment, reserved for executed murderers and other felons. Public dissections were spectacles intended to terrorize criminals and deter others; bodies offered little educational value. But as anatomical knowledge grew, scholars realized that demonstration was insufficient — hands-on training required actual cadaver labs where students could practice dissection. The supply of executed criminals quickly became inadequate for the number of students seeking to learn anatomy.

**The Grave Robbing Era and Its Victims**

The shortage of legal bodies spawned a lucrative grave-robbing industry. Lawrence notes that robbers targeted the graves of the poor, who had minimal burial guards. A single body could be extracted in less than an hour and sold to universities for approximately $1,000 in today's money. Some doctors and medical students dug graves themselves and often escaped detection. Most people either remained unaware of the practice or turned a blind eye. However, some cadaver suppliers went further: in Scotland, two grave robbers decided that unearthing graves was too much work, so they murdered approximately 16 poor, vagrant, and otherwise vulnerable people over a period of 10 months, assuming no one would miss them.

When American medical schools began teaching anatomy for the first time, they too obtained cadavers through nefarious means. They commonly used the bodies of enslaved people and poor, free African Americans, which raised little political objection. Only when grave robbers began targeting the graves of wealthy white citizens did the practice become a politically contentious issue. The result was a series of state laws that granted medical schools access to unclaimed bodies from prisons, hospitals, and city streets — a decision driven by the economics of burial: if someone died on the street or in a public institution, taxpayer money covered disposal, so medical schools had to pay for both the body and subsequent cremation.

**The Rise of Voluntary Donation and Legal Framework**

Cadavers were sourced from unclaimed bodies for roughly a century. By the 1950s, American attitudes shifted. The public became less superstitious and more supportive of medicine following breakthroughs like the first human organ transplants. Simultaneously, distrust of the modern funeral industry — which had made dying expensive — grew. Americans began to support the idea of willingly donating their bodies to science. However, it was technically illegal to donate one's own body. In response, legislators enacted the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act in 1968, a federal framework designed primarily to enable organ donation. The act inadvertently included whole-body donation, allowing Americans to donate their bodies for research and education. Nearly every state has since enacted versions of this law, which generally define the human body as property and grant Americans the right to donate that property to an organization of their choice.

**The Unintended Consequence: For-Profit Cadaver Companies**

The legalization of body donation had an unforeseen side effect: the emergence of a new generation of for-profit companies that could obtain and sell cadavers with very little regulatory oversight. Medical schools maintained their own not-for-profit donation programs. Georgetown University, for example, offers donors a card to keep in their wallet; when death occurs, the body is frozen and used within 30 months. Georgetown receives approximately 120 donated cadavers annually and has 4,500 living donors registered. But for those seeking an alternative, for-profit body donation companies emerged to collect bodies and sell them to third parties.

ScienceCare, founded 23 years ago by a funeral insurance salesman who saw an opportunity to act as a cadaver middleman, has become the world's largest for-profit body donation program. The firm grew to approximately $30 million in annual sales before being acquired by a private equity firm in 2016. ScienceCare now operates seven donation facilities across the country and accepts bodies in most states. Vice president of marketing Kaylin Goodwin states that the company has "more than 257,000 people registered with us that intend to donate their body to science" (as of the time of the episode).

**The Business Model: Marketing Altruism While Parting Out Bodies**

ScienceCare markets body donation as an altruistic act. Goodwin describes a campaign titled "There's a Hero in Us All," designed to help people understand that body donation expands knowledge for future generations. The company offers material incentives as well: all accepted donors receive free cremation at the end of the program, with any uncollected parts cremated and returned to designated recipients. According to Susan Lawrence, companies like ScienceCare frame themselves as providing a service, not a product — claiming they are not "buying and selling body parts" but rather charging "reimbursement cost for the difficulty of securing the body, preparing the body, transporting the body." However, this framing obscures a critical practice: the body is typically parceled into parts (forearms, torsos, heads, knees) and sold to multiple entities. Body parts from a single donor can collectively generate $5,000 to $10,000.

Cadaver workshops, known as "wet labs," are a particularly lucrative market segment. When medical device manufacturers design new tools or surgeons develop new procedures, testing often requires body parts in bulk. ScienceCare collaborates with medical device manufacturers, research organizations, medical professional associations, pharmaceutical companies, and medical teaching institutions worldwide. Donors have contributed to cancer research, Alzheimer's research, drug development, and biomechanical testing that has led to new medical devices. Yet many donors are unaware their bodies will be parted out, and companies never disclose exactly where those parts go.

**The Lack of Regulation and Recent Scandals**

Unlike live organ donation, which faces strict federal oversight, bodies donated for education or research are largely unregulated. There are no laws against selling them. Sometimes bodies are sold fully intact to medical schools with cadaver shortages, either in the United States or abroad. But more often, bodies are dissected into parts for multiple buyers. In 2022, concerns surfaced when a box full of human heads from ScienceCare was stolen from a cargo truck in Denver. Despite this incident, ScienceCare remains one of the most trusted body donation firms in the country and is one of only a handful accredited by the American Association of Tissue Banks (AATB), the same organization that regulates live organ donation. However, accreditation is not a legal requirement to operate; ScienceCare pursued it voluntarily, as Goodwin states, "solely to hold ourselves to that higher standard of regulatory compliance."

**Concerns About the Social Contract**

Susan Lawrence is considering donating her own body to science, but she prefers to give it to an educational institution rather than a for-profit company. She is considering a program at the University of Tennessee that runs a body farm where forensic anthropologists study human decomposition — a use that "doesn't bother me at all" to imagine. However, Lawrence worries about the future of voluntary body donation. She describes it as "a delicate social contract that I think could be easily broken if there were enough scandals and abuse of body parts." In a market as unregulated as the human cadaver trade, that concern has merit.
