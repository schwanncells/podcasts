# Unchained — "Bits + Bips: Bitcoin Finally Acted Like a Hedge. Will It Last?"

**Host:** Steve Ehrlich. **Guest:** Andy Baer (Managing Director of Asset Management, GSR; former head of CoinDesk Indices; equity volatility specialist with 20+ years in finance).

## TL;DR

- **Bitcoin up 4% since conflict started February 28th** — traded steadily in $65K–$71K range, acting as safe haven while oil surged above $100/barrel and equities declined 1.5%.
- **VIX at 26 signals "yellow" not "red"** — implies ~1.6% daily equity moves; far below the 32–50 levels seen in acute crises, suggesting market pricing in moderate rather than severe contagion.
- **$1.2 billion in notional volume on Hyperliquid oil contracts in one day** — demonstrates crypto's capacity for 24/7 price discovery on geopolitical events when traditional markets closed.
- **Stablecoins hit record $317–$320 billion, with Ethereum holding ~50%** — signals institutional demand for tokenized yield and DeFi lending revival.
- **Perps unlikely to replace traditional futures for institutional traders** — perpetual contracts feel more like options than delta-one derivatives; leverage liquidation carries less stigma, making them more retail-compatible.
- **Q2 2026 likely to be pivotal** — DeFi borrowing rates and funding rates will signal appetite for leverage; commodity prices, Fed policy clarity, and geopolitical resolution will drive the next trend.
- **Inflation expectations rising to 3–3.5% if oil stays above $100** — threatens rate-cut hopes; central banks contemplating hikes rather than cuts, constraining monetary stimulus.

**Bitcoin's Unexpected Role as a Safe Haven**

The conflict that began February 28th created unusual market dynamics. While oil climbed above $100 per barrel despite a 400-million-barrel IEA release, and equities declined roughly 1.5%, Bitcoin rose approximately 4%. Baer, with decades of volatility expertise, notes this marks a shift in perception. "I always look at the VIX first," he explains, observing that at 26, the index sits in yellow-alert territory rather than the red crisis levels of 32–50. A VIX of 26 implies roughly 1.6% daily moves in the S&P 500; the market is pricing in moderate risk contagion, not systemic collapse. This suggests investors are not yet flooding into traditional havens like U.S. Treasuries or gold, which have already rallied substantially.

**Market Structure and Crypto's 24/7 Advantage**

Unlike traditional equities and futures markets, crypto operates seven days a week and 365 days a year. This became evident when Hyperliquid processed approximately $1.2 billion in notional volume on oil derivative contracts in a single day—a massive figure for the platform. Baer cautions against assuming this represents true market-wide hedging. "It can only be the market's view when the entire market can participate," he states. While Bitcoin has regulatory clarity and deep options and perpetuals markets, lower-tier altcoins remain accessible primarily to crypto-native traders, not institutional hedgers. Perpetual futures, though innovative, differ meaningfully from traditional futures contracts. They function more like leveraged options for retail traders, who face liquidation risk without career-ending stigma—a stark contrast to institutional futures trading, where losses carry reputational weight.

**Stablecoins and the Tokenization Frontier**

A significant structural development underlies the broader narrative: stablecoins have reached record levels of $317–$320 billion in circulating supply, with approximately $160 billion on Ethereum and Solana gaining market share. This concentration reflects institutional appetite for yield-bearing positions. Baer emphasizes that tokenization and stablecoin adoption will be essential to the next phase of crypto growth, but demand cannot be artificially manufactured. "Good liquidity costs money," he notes, hinting that secondary liquidity—where buyers can exit positions—remains the real constraint on tokenized equity adoption. He remains skeptical that all equity trading will migrate to tokenized venues despite pilots from NYSE, NASDAQ, and ICE.

**DeFi's Return and the Signal of Borrowing Rates**

Another overlooked metric is the borrowing rate for stablecoins like USDC on platforms such as Aave. In low-energy markets, these rates fall sharply because leverage appetite collapses. Baer, who built indices at CoinDesk, recommends watching these rates closely: rising rates signal renewed demand for borrowed capital, a harbinger of fresh momentum. He also stresses the importance of portfolio breadth beyond Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Solana. DeFi vaults and yield protocols appear poised for growth, particularly as institutional interest in trustable, non-custodial yield expands.

**Macro Headwinds and the Fed Path**

February's inflation data showed a rare month-to-month flat reading and 2.4% year-over-year growth, suggesting modest price pressure. However, that snapshot became obsolete within days as geopolitical tensions erupted. Current forecasts suggest sustained oil prices above $100 per barrel could push inflation to 3–3.5% for an extended period. This threatens the base case for interest-rate cuts in 2026, particularly under a potential new Federal Reserve Chair. Baer observes that traditional safe havens have inverted: U.S. Treasury yields are rising despite geopolitical turmoil, the dollar remains strong (DXY up), and gold, while elevated, has already priced in most of the rally. In such an environment, he recommends dry powder—waiting for clearer catalysts rather than chasing into flat, low-conviction markets.

**Looking Ahead to Q2 2026**

The quarter carries multiple potential pivots. The geopolitical situation could resolve; the deleveraging from the October 2024 flash crash and February 5th IBIT-related sell-off could fully digest; and the Federal Reserve could shift communication under new leadership, potentially supporting "staging the house" for midterm elections. Baer's guidance is to watch for trend formation and rising volatility in crypto, particularly elevated funding rates and DeFi borrowing costs. "The attention span of the market moves in quarters," he observes. The current quarter has been sour and low-energy; Q2 will likely bring either relief or fresh capitulation. His contrarian take: many builders are constructing tokenized equity infrastructure speculatively, and it remains unproven whether demand will materialize once products launch.
