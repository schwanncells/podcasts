**Two Farmer's Almanacs, Hundreds of Years of Publishing**

The Old Farmer's Almanac dates back to the late 1700s, while the Farmer's Almanac was founded in 1818. Both publications have persisted through massive technological and media shifts. Dean Regas reports that the Farmer's Almanac faced closure when the family running it decided to exit the business, but a new company took over earlier in 2026. Regas contributes astronomy articles on events like eclipses and meteor showers, initially skeptical about "farming and astrology" but discovering strong reader engagement, particularly online where the publication has over a million social media followers.

**Are Modern Farmers Using Almanacs?**

Liz Grazik, an organic farmer in Missouri, treats the Farmer's Almanac as entertainment and cultural reading—learning "old wives tales"—rather than farming guidance. She does not know modern farmers who use them as intended, though she notes her 70-plus-year-old neighbors "look at the world a little differently" and base decisions on what she calls old wives' tales, and "they're not often wrong." However, Grazik thinks about things "more scientifically." She relies on weather forecasts instead, explaining she checks "10 day, 30 day forecasts like many, many times a day." Climate change has made the Farmer's Almanac essentially obsolete for her farm; she has invested in high tunnels and growing under cover to maintain crop control. A livestock farmer from Iowa wrote to the show saying she glances at the Farmer's Almanac only once a year in stores and does not refer to it because "climate change has made them more obsolete."

**Astronomy Content and Forecast Accuracy**

Regas notes that Farmer's Almanac weather forecasts claim they are "right more than half the time," though he admits uncertainty about such predictions. As an amateur weather observer tracking forecasts for the Ohio Valley region, he finds precision difficult. For astronomy, he has worked to bring "certainty" to the content, allowing readers to reliably look up sky events.

**Zebra Finches and Color Preference**

Lauren Gillette has studied zebra finches for 10 to 15 years, discovering they have individual color preferences for nest-building materials. In laboratory experiments, male zebra finches were presented with blue and yellow string and video-recorded for four hours while researchers measured how much time each bird spent interacting with each color. This interaction time revealed both preference (blue or yellow) and preference strength, expressed as a proportion. A bird spending the entire time with blue had a preference strength of 100; equal time split gave 50/50. Across tests using various colors—blue, yellow, pink, orange, and purple—birds displayed different individual preferences rather than all preferring the same color.

**Social Conformity and Personal Preference in Nest Building**

The critical experiment placed a male finch and his partner in a small colony where other pairs had already built nests. Sometimes the colony nests matched the focal male's initial color preference; sometimes they contradicted it. After observing for a few days, the pair was given blue and yellow string to build their own nest. Results showed that preference strength determined conformity: birds with weak initial preferences (50/50) were more likely to conform to the majority and build with the color everyone else used, while birds with strong preferences stuck with their original choice. As Gillette explained, "strong opinion matters, even in birds," paralleling how humans filter information through pre-existing biases and form cultural patterns.

**Female Finch Influence and Future Research**

While male zebra finches select and deposit nest materials, researchers have only recently begun studying female preferences. Preliminary data suggests female color preferences show up in the final nest composition, though the mechanism remains unclear. Gillette notes females may communicate preference by "throwing material out of the nest when she's not happy," but other communication methods may exist. The team is analyzing video to understand how females influence males without directly selecting materials.

**Avian Color Vision Beyond Human Perception**

Zebra finches, like most birds, are tetrachromats and can see blue, green, red, and ultraviolet light—a broader spectrum than humans, who are trichromats. This gives birds superior color vision and likely influences how they perceive and select nesting materials.
