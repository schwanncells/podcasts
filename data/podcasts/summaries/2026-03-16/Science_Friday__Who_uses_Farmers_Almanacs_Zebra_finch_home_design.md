**Farmers Almanacs in Modern Agricultural Life**

Farmer's Almanacs have survived for over 200 years, with two rival publications dominating the market: the Farmer's Almanac (founded 1818, orange cover) and the Old Farmer's Almanac (late 1700s). The Farmer's Almanac nearly ceased publication in 2026 when the family operating it decided to exit the business, but another company acquired it before total shutdown. Both publications feature robust astronomy sections and claim to provide weather forecasts and planting guidance based on historical patterns.

However, modern farmers rarely depend on these almanacs for operational decisions. Liz Grazik, an organic farmer in Missouri, reads them primarily for entertainment and curiosity about historical agricultural practices and folklore. She consults weather forecasts multiple times daily (10-day and 30-day outlooks) and maintains detailed records of her own farm's performance alongside neighbors' records. Climate change has made traditional almanac guidance less reliable; Grazik has invested heavily in high tunnels and covered growing systems to maintain control over crop timing and yields despite unpredictable seasonal patterns. Older farmers (70+ years old) still consult Farmer's Almanacs and make decisions based on what younger farmers call "old wives tales," though interestingly they are often accurate. Dean Regas notes that the Farmer's Almanac itself claims to be "right more than half the time" regarding weather predictions, though verification is difficult. Regas contributes astronomy content to the publication, writing about eclipses and meteor showers; this content has resonated widely online, with the Farmer's Almanac boasting over 1 million social media followers.

**Individual Preferences and Conformity in Zebra Finch Nest-Building**

Zebra finches—small Australian songbirds—exhibit individual color preferences when selecting nest-building materials, and these preferences predict whether they conform to social pressure. Researchers, led by Lauren Gillette of the University of Alberta, spent 10–15 years studying the species' color preferences and recently published findings on how individual bias interacts with population-level trends.

In laboratory experiments, male and female zebra finches were placed together in cages with colored string (blue and yellow) tied down so birds could interact with but not remove the material. Researchers scored video recordings (4 hours per test) to measure preference strength—the proportion of interaction time spent with each color. A bird spending all time with blue would score 100; equal time yields 50. Results showed birds have distinct individual color preferences; not all finches prefer the same color. When researchers then introduced focal males and females into small colonies containing nests already built by other pairs, they observed striking behavior: birds with weak initial preferences (around 50/50) conformed to the majority color in the colony, even when it contradicted their initial slight bias. Conversely, birds with strong preferences (near 100) resisted social pressure and built nests matching their original choice.

**Female Influence and Bird Color Vision**

While males physically select and deposit nest material (making them the primary builders), preliminary research suggests females significantly influence final nest composition. Gillette's team has begun measuring female color preferences and testing how they manifest in completed nests. Early data shows female preferences do appear in the finished structure, despite females not handling material. The mechanism remains unclear—females may communicate preferences by removing disliked material from the nest, or through other subtle social signals.

Zebra finches perceive color differently than humans. Humans are trichromats with three color receptors (blue, green, red); birds are tetrachromats, possessing all three human receptors plus ultraviolet sensitivity. This gives finches superior color vision, allowing them to perceive wavelengths invisible to human eyes. The individual variation in color preference, combined with the social conformity findings, suggests that birds filter environmental information through their pre-existing biases—a pattern that mirrors human cognition and cultural transmission. The research demonstrates that "strong opinion matters, even in birds," and that culture and information spread through bird populations similarly to human societies, challenging the notion that such sophistication is uniquely human.
