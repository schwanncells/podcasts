# Business History — "Bowie, McCartney & Michael Jackson: How Songwriters Learned to Play Hardball"

**Hosts:** Robert Smith and Jacob Goldstein. **Guests:** None (live piano demonstrations by Ben Heffrey).

## TL;DR

- **ASCAP founded in 1914 after Victor Herbert sued Shanley's restaurant** — the Supreme Court ruled in 1917 that playing copyrighted music in a for-profit establishment requires payment to composers, even if no admission is charged.
- **By 1939, ASCAP controlled roughly 90% of U.S. music copyrights** — charging radio stations 5% of advertising revenue, with president Gene Buck described by *The New Yorker* as "the most powerful private citizen on earth."
- **Buck's attempt to double rates to 10% backfired spectacularly** — broadcasters formed rival cartel BMI in 1940, signing country, blues, R&B, and Latin artists ASCAP had excluded; when the deadline hit on December 31, 1940, listeners didn't notice the difference, and ASCAP ultimately settled for 2.75% — barely half what they'd been paid before.
- **Michael Jackson bought the Beatles catalog in 1985 for $47.5 million** — outbidding Richard Branson after Paul McCartney had jokingly suggested the idea; Jackson later doubled his money selling the rights to Sony, establishing that song catalogs were dramatically undervalued assets.
- **David Bowie securitized 287 songs in 1997 into "Bowie Bonds" paying 7.9% interest**, raising $55 million to buy back the half of his catalog owned by a manager he despised — pioneering the use of royalty income as collateral without surrendering ownership.
- **Songs are now Wall Street assets** — as of the episode's reporting, Blackstone, Carlisle, and Michigan's state pension fund had raised at least $4.4 billion packaging royalties from Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon/Paul McCartney into securities.
- **The irony of the full circle** — most working musicians are back to where they were before ASCAP; real money now comes from touring and merchandise, because live performance hasn't yet been financialized.

**Victor Herbert, Shanley's Restaurant, and the Birth of ASCAP**

In 1913, composer Victor Herbert was eating at Shanley's, a fashionable Times Square restaurant, when he heard the band play a song from his Broadway musical *Sweethearts* — without his permission or payment. When Herbert demanded compensation, owner Shanley refused, arguing the music was incidental background entertainment, not a performance. Herbert sued. The hotel and restaurant industry rallied behind Shanley, and Herbert assembled eight figures from the music publishing world to found the American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers — ASCAP — in 1914. The lawsuit wound its way to the Supreme Court, where Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote the decision in 1917: "If the music did not pay, it would be given up. If it pays, it pays out of the public pocketbook. Whether it pays or not, the purpose of employing it is profit and that is enough." The ruling established the legal foundation for performance royalties in any commercial setting.

**ASCAP's Enforcement Model: Music Cops and Blanket Licenses**

ASCAP's strategy had two parts. First, it sent agents to major venues to document which songs were played, then sued the owners — a threat everyone knew they would lose. Second, it offered an easy out: pay a small percentage of revenues and gain unlimited access to ASCAP's entire catalog. Smith describes this as solving a genuine friction problem for both sides; venues weren't in the "counting songs" business, and blanket licensing made compliance effortless. ASCAP distributed royalties back to members using a weighted formula: more popular songwriters with more hits received larger shares. What ASCAP did not offer was inclusivity — membership required being able to read music and having the right pedigree, effectively excluding jazz, blues, and country musicians, who were referred to at the time as "hillbilly" performers.

**Radio, the FCC, and ASCAP's Expanding Empire**

Radio's arrival in November 1921 — beginning with KDKA in Pittsburgh — created ASCAP's biggest new revenue stream. Radio stations initially argued they weren't giving a public performance since no audience was physically present, that they made artists famous so artists should pay them, and, most inventively, that radio didn't broadcast music but rather "emanated electrical energy." All three arguments failed. The FCC ruled in 1924 that radio constituted a public performance subject to copyright. ASCAP extended its licensing regime to include broadcast revenue, and by 1935 had negotiated a deal requiring radio stations to pay 5% of their advertising revenue annually — worth millions of dollars to ASCAP members.

**Gene Buck, the Cartel Overreach, and the ASCAP Ultimatum**

ASCAP's new president Gene Buck — known as "Mr. Broadway" and described by *The New Yorker* as capable of stopping jazz from the air with a single word — ran the organization like a monopolist with no interest in moderation. He was a driving force behind ASCAP's exclusion of Black musicians and country acts, and he expanded enforcement aggressively enough that a Montana justice of the peace issued a warrant for his arrest on extortion charges. Buck was arrested while visiting Phoenix on a holiday weekend, and had to scramble to post a $10,000 bail because the sheriff had never heard of a certified check or Chase National Bank. Rather than reading these legal challenges as warnings, Buck announced in the run-up to the contract expiration at the end of 1940 that ASCAP would double its rate from 5% to 10%. When broadcasters sought to negotiate, Buck stood them up to attend the Bohemian Grove retreat.

**BMI, the Counter-Cartel, and ASCAP's Collapse**

Faced with Buck's ultimatum, broadcasters formed Broadcast Music Incorporated — BMI — in February 1940, giving themselves ten months before the ASCAP contract expired. Their strategic insight: ASCAP had left enormous talent on the table. BMI signed country, blues, R&B, gospel, and Latin artists. Station relations manager Carl Haverlin — whose instinct for popular taste had previously kept Amelia Earhart awake across the Pacific with a live rendition of "Red River Valley" — traveled the country signing up stations to BMI licenses. When midnight struck on December 31, 1940, radio stayed on. Listeners expecting silence heard music and assumed ASCAP had negotiated a deal. It was BMI music, and, as Smith puts it, "nobody really noticed or even really cared." ASCAP caved within months, settling for 2.75% of radio station revenues — barely half what they'd been charging before their gambit. Buck was eventually removed as president. Goldstein notes the lesson: "If you form a cartel, you better make sure there's not a substitute for what you're selling."

**Songs Become Assets: Copyright Extension and Financialization**

As ASCAP and BMI matured, the two organizations pushed for copyright terms to be extended to the songwriter's lifetime plus fifty years — eventually reaching lifetime plus seventy years — meaning a successful song could generate revenue for well over a hundred years. Smith draws an explicit analogy: "A great song is essentially like a long-term bond." With radio, television, films, records, eight-tracks, cassettes, CDs, and oldies radio all generating separate royalty streams, songs stopped being products and became cash-flow-generating assets. This attracted Wall Street's attention.

**Michael Jackson and the Beatles Catalog**

In 1981, Michael Jackson was at Paul McCartney's estate outside London when McCartney — who had lost control of some early Beatles songs and was eager to reclaim them — showed Jackson his own publishing acquisitions, including the Buddy Holly catalog and Broadway tunes. Jackson half-jokingly suggested he might buy McCartney's music someday. McCartney didn't take it seriously. Four years later, with $9 million in cash and a growing appetite for music rights, Jackson had already bought the Sly and the Family Stone catalog for $500,000 and rights to classic fifties hits. When Australian media tycoon Holmes à Court put the Beatles catalog up for sale in 1984, Jackson bid $40 million — an amount industry insiders thought absurd. A bidding war with Richard Branson followed, and Jackson prevailed in August 1985 at $47.5 million plus an appearance at a Perth telethon fundraiser. One song was carved out by the seller as a gift to his daughter; hoping she would choose "Yesterday," she instead chose "Penny Lane." McCartney was furious — Jackson had not only bought the catalog McCartney wanted, but had greatly inflated the cost of ever buying it back. Jackson soon licensed Beatles songs to Nike, including "Revolution," over McCartney's objections. Jackson later sold the rights to Sony, doubling his money. The episode's lesson for the financial world: music catalogs had been dramatically underpriced.

**Bowie Bonds and the Securitization of Music**

In the 1990s, David Bowie owned 50% of his catalog; the other 50% belonged to a manager he despised. Every time one of his songs played on the radio, half the royalty went to someone he hated. His financial advisers devised a solution: package 287 of his compositions — from "Life on Mars" to "Ashes to Ashes" — into a special-purpose securitization vehicle, sell slices of the future royalty revenue to investors, and use the proceeds to buy out the hated manager's stake. The instrument, immediately dubbed "Bowie Bonds," paid 7.9% annual interest and raised $55 million, mostly purchased by insurance companies seeking long-dated assets to match long-dated liabilities. Crucially, Bowie retained ownership of the songs; he had only pledged the revenue stream. The timing proved unfortunate for investors: the deal was struck near peak CD-era revenues, Napster arrived shortly after, music industry revenues collapsed, and the bonds were eventually rated junk. But the template had been drawn, and Wall Street had learned that music royalties could be packaged and sold like any other income-producing asset. Investors including Blackstone, Carlisle, and Michigan's state pension fund have since raised at least $4.4 billion securitizing the catalogs of Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon and Paul McCartney.

**The Full Circle: Financialization and the Working Musician**

Smith closes with a pointed observation about where the money actually flows. Financialization has made superstar catalogs even more valuable — the entities that own music rights have strong financial incentives to continue promoting established artists, increasing the value of their securities. But for most working musicians, the situation echoes the era before ASCAP: they earn a modest trickle from recorded music, and real income requires performing in venues — the modern equivalent of Shanley's lobster restaurant. Live performance and merchandise haven't been financialized yet, which is why that's where the money still is.
