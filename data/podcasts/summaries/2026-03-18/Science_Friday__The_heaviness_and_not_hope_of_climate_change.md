# Science Friday — "The heaviness and (not) hope of climate change"

**Host:** Flora Lichtman. **Guest:** Elizabeth Kolbert (staff writer, *The New Yorker*; Pulitzer Prize winner, 2015; author of *Life on a Little-Known Planet: Dispatches from a Changing World*, the current Science Friday Book Club selection).

## TL;DR

- **Insect decline is a "much bigger story" than it gets** — entomologist Dave Wagner, described as "one of the world's leading caterpillar experts," is compiling a four-volume encyclopedia of Western North American caterpillars, a work that underscores how little is still known about insects that form the base of most food chains.
- **Samso island, Denmark went carbon neutral ~20 years ago** — a community of ordinary potato and strawberry farmers used wind turbines and national-level policy support to eliminate their net carbon footprint; nearly two decades on, Kolbert is struck by "how few communities have followed that example."
- **Kolbert rejects "hope" as a journalistic default** — when asked what gives her hope at a recent event, she cited James Hansen's answer: "I hope you're listening." She argues that mandating hopeful endings has become "almost a cliché."
- **Solar, wind, and battery costs have dropped dramatically** since Kolbert began covering climate, but the current U.S. administration is "doing its darndest to promote the use of fossil fuels" — a contradiction she calls "a very, very strange moment."
- **Humanity is "sleepwalking into the unknown"** — modern humans have never experienced a climate warmer than the current interglacial, and current emissions are pushing beyond any planetary analog of at least several million years.
- **The Great Barrier Reef (size of Italy) has suffered multiple global coral bleaching events** in recent years; researchers at the University of Miami are achieving early success breeding corals with heat-tolerant algal symbionts, though scaling to reef-wide intervention remains a fundamental challenge.
- **Kolbert explicitly avoids saying "solve climate change"** — "climate change is something that doesn't go away. Even once you stop emitting carbon, you don't get the climate back that you had. We really need to stabilize the climate."

**Caterpillars, Beating Sheets, and the Insect Apocalypse**

Kolbert opens by describing a reporting trip to Texas with Dave Wagner, whom she calls one of the world's leading caterpillar experts. She learned the standard fieldwork technique: a "beating sheet" — a kite-like frame placed under a plant — is held below while the plant is struck with a pole, sending caterpillars raining down to be sorted and identified. The exercise illuminated how underdocumented caterpillars remain. Wagner is working on a four-volume encyclopedia of Western North American caterpillars, a project Kolbert likens to a very slow treasure hunt because the subject matter is so poorly catalogued that no comparable work currently exists.

Kolbert describes caterpillars as "hugely important to ecosystems" — they transfer vast amounts of energy from photosynthesis up the food chain, serving as a primary food source for birds in particular. Yet insect declines are severe and nearly universal in places where researchers have looked. She argues the scale of the loss should register as a much bigger story than mainstream coverage has allowed, since insects constitute the majority of species on Earth and, as she puts it, are "what makes the world run" — pollinating, dispersing seeds, and decomposing organic matter.

**Samso Island and the Limits of Inspiring Precedent**

In 2008 Kolbert profiled Samso, a small Danish island of farmers who achieved carbon neutrality through community organizing and favorable national energy policy. The island's windy geography made it well-suited for turbines, and Danish policies made it economically sensible for farmers to install them on their own land; significant offshore wind followed. Kolbert climbed one of the turbines as part of her reporting. The key dynamic, she explains, was that energy reduction became a kind of competitive community sport — residents who had not identified as "energy ideologues" began tracking consumption and competing to reduce it.

What has surprised her in the nearly two decades since is not the story itself but its limited replication. She uses the Samso example to demonstrate that transitions are achievable with the right mix of collective attention and supportive policy — and implicitly to highlight the structural barriers that have prevented the same logic from spreading more widely.

**Hope as Journalistic Trap**

Lichtman presses Kolbert on the emotional and narrative framework she uses to process decades of covering accelerating environmental loss. Kolbert is candid that the current political moment — with the U.S. government actively promoting fossil fuel use — makes it genuinely difficult not to be "dispirited." She describes "ending on a hopeful note" as having hardened into almost a genre cliché, with a theory behind it: bad news without hope supposedly demotivates audiences. She understands the logic but resists the formula.

When Lichtman recounts asking Kolbert at a recent public event what gives her hope, Kolbert's answer was James Hansen's: "I hope you're listening." The exchange captures her view that the more honest posture is urgency rather than reassurance. She explicitly refuses the phrase "solve climate change," insisting the correct goal is climate stabilization — because emissions reductions arrest further damage without restoring what was lost.

**Technology vs. Policy: The Current Contradiction**

Kolbert acknowledges that the technological picture has genuinely improved since she began covering climate. Solar, wind, and battery storage have all fallen sharply in price, placing the necessary tools within economic reach. This, she says, represents a "huge boon" and demonstrates that the toolbox exists. The contradiction she keeps returning to is that the United States is simultaneously positioned to use those tools at scale and actively trying — in her phrasing — "to strangle them," through an administration oriented toward fossil fuel expansion. She describes the resulting situation as "very, very strange."

**Coral Bleaching and the Scale Problem**

Kolbert describes the Great Barrier Reef, which she visited while living with researchers on a tiny coral island barely above the waterline, as a transformative experience — a "kaleidoscope of life" that recalibrates one's sense of what biological abundance can look like. She contrasts it with the impoverished ecosystems most people in temperate regions grow up in.

The current threat is severe. Under sustained heat stress, corals expel their algal symbionts — the phenomenon known as coral bleaching — and multiple global bleaching events have occurred in recent years. Researchers at the University of Miami are experimenting with breeding corals that take up more heat-tolerant symbionts, and are achieving early success. But the Great Barrier Reef alone is the size of Italy, and Kolbert notes the fundamental problem of scaling any laboratory-level intervention to that magnitude.

**Sleepwalking Into the Unknown**

Kolbert closes by situating the current moment in deep time. Modern humans are relatively young as a species — a few hundred thousand years — and for most of that period the climate was significantly colder than today, punctuated by interglacial warm periods roughly comparable to preindustrial conditions. Current emissions are pushing beyond even those warmer interglacials into a heat regime that has no analog in millions of years of planetary history. She describes humanity as "sleepwalking into the unknown" — a situation made more dangerous by the absence of historical precedents to reason from.

On the question of whether something in human nature might yet rescue the situation, Kolbert is equivocal. She acknowledges humans have survived past crises, possibly including population bottlenecks following large volcanic eruptions, and that the species' capacity for foresight and creativity is real. Whether that capacity will prove sufficient through the rest of the 21st century, she leaves as the open question.
