# Business History — "Bowie, McCartney & Michael Jackson: How Songwriters Learned to Play Hardball"

**Hosts:** Robert Smith and Jacob Goldstein (co-hosts, *Business History*, Pushkin).

## TL;DR

- **Victor Herbert sued Shanley's Times Square restaurant in 1913** for playing his song without payment; Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes's 1917 Supreme Court ruling established that any commercial use of music — even as background — requires compensating copyright holders.
- **ASCAP, founded in 1914**, assembled composers including Irving Berlin, John Philip Sousa, and lyricist Gene Buck into a licensing cartel that by 1939 controlled ~90% of U.S. music copyrights and collected 5% of radio stations' advertising revenue — while explicitly excluding jazz, blues, R&B, and country musicians.
- **Gene Buck's 1940 ultimatum** — demanding radio stations double payments to 10% of ad revenue — triggered the creation of BMI by broadcasters, which signed the excluded artists; when ASCAP music went dark January 1, 1941, nobody noticed, and ASCAP eventually capitulated at 2.75% — less than the original 5%.
- **Michael Jackson bought the Beatles catalog in August 1985 for $47.5 million** (outbidding Richard Branson), licensed "Revolution" to Nike, then sold to Sony for roughly double — demonstrating that song catalogs were dramatically undervalued tradeable assets.
- **David Bowie securitized 287 songs in 1997** into "Bowie bonds" paying 7.9% interest, raising $55 million to buy back his despised manager's 50% stake — pioneering music-backed securities that later attracted Blackstone, Carlisle, and pension funds.
- **Copyright term extensions** lobbied for by ASCAP and BMI stretched protection to songwriter's lifetime plus 70 years; investors including Blackstone and Michigan's State pension fund have since raised at least **$4.4 billion** packaging songs by Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon and Paul McCartney into securities.
- **Full circle**: despite a century of rights infrastructure, most working musicians today still earn primarily from live performance — touring and merchandise — much as they did before Victor Herbert walked into Shanley's.

**Victor Herbert, Shanley's Restaurant, and the Birth of Performance Rights**

In 1913, composer Victor Herbert — author of the 1903 musical *Babes in Toyland* — was dining at Shanley's restaurant in Times Square when he heard the house band playing one of his own songs. Herbert demanded payment; owner Shanley refused, arguing that background music played without a cover charge was not a "public performance." Herbert sued. Shanley had his own collective action problem: rather than negotiate individually with every composer, he rallied the National Association of Hotel and Restaurant Owners to fund a joint legal defense seeking a court ruling that incidental music required no copyright payment.

Herbert assembled eight music publishing heavyweights at Luchow's restaurant downtown, and in 1914 this group founded the American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers — ASCAP — recruiting the composers of Tin Pan Alley. The case wound its way to the Supreme Court, where in 1917 Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote the majority opinion: "If the music did not pay, it would be given up. If it pays, it pays out of the public pocketbook. Whether it pays or not, the purpose of employing it is profit and that is enough." The ruling confirmed that any commercial use of copyrighted music — in a restaurant, dance hall, or anywhere audiences paid to be — required compensation to its creators.

**ASCAP's Licensing Model and Its Exclusions**

ASCAP's enforcement strategy was two-pronged. "Music cops" visited establishments and documented every copyrighted song played, then threatened lawsuits. The practical alternative was a blanket license: pay a small percentage of revenues and play anything in ASCAP's catalog without further accounting. ASCAP distributed royalties to members, weighting payments toward songwriters with greater name recognition and more hits, splitting proceeds with publishing houses. By 1921 the organization was profitable — enforcement costs were below royalty income.

ASCAP's benefits, however, were not distributed equally. Membership required demonstrated music literacy and the right pedigree, effectively excluding jazz, blues, R&B, and country ("hillbilly") musicians. The organization was, as Jacob Goldstein summarized, "New York thinking it was better than everybody else."

**Radio Arrives and ASCAP Wins the Airwaves**

The first commercial radio broadcast aired November 2, 1921, from KDKA in Pittsburgh (Westinghouse Electric and Manufacturing Company). Radio stations quickly built their model around live music, and ASCAP asserted that broadcasting constituted a public performance triggering copyright payment. Stations cycled through familiar defenses: that an unseen studio audience meant no "public performance"; that they made songwriters famous and should be paid rather than owe payment; that radio "emanates electrical energy" rather than broadcasting music. All three arguments failed. In 1924, the FCC ruled that radio constituted a public performance and copyright protections applied. ASCAP now collected a share of the era's dominant technology.

**Gene Buck's Overreach and the Creation of BMI**

ASCAP's power grew rapidly. By 1939 the organization controlled roughly 90% of U.S. music copyrights and held a 5% share of radio advertising revenues. Gene Buck — the lyricist known as "Mister Broadway," whose Long Island estate was rumored to have inspired *The Great Gatsby* — ran ASCAP with the style The New Yorker described as that of "the most powerful private citizen on earth," someone who "can stop the broadcasting of popular music if he says stop." Buck continued and deepened the exclusion of Black and country musicians. In late 1940 he announced the 5% rate would double to 10%, effective January 1, 1941. When radio executives gathered at the Ritz Tower to negotiate, Buck skipped the meeting entirely, traveling instead to the Bohemian Grove. The confrontation turned personal: a Montana justice of the peace issued a warrant for Buck's arrest on extortion charges, and while Buck was visiting Phoenix on a holiday weekend he was arrested under the warrant. A friend's certified check from Chase National Bank failed to satisfy the sheriff, who "don't know what a certified check is" and had "never heard of this Chase National Bank."

The broadcasters' response was decisive. They convened a war meeting and founded Broadcast Music Incorporated (BMI) in late 1939, giving themselves ten months to build a competitive catalog. BMI's signing strategy targeted exactly the artists ASCAP had refused: country acts including the Carter Family and Jimmie Rodgers, blues and R&B performers, and Latin artists. BMI's station relations manager, Carl Haverlin, traveled the country signing radio stations to BMI licenses — his instinct, shaped by years in the industry, was that audiences outside New York wanted music that ASCAP didn't offer.

**January 1, 1941: ASCAP's Bluff Called**

When the contract expired at midnight December 31, 1940, ASCAP music went silent on American radio. Listeners waited to see whether there would be any music at all. What they heard was BMI music — and, as Robert Smith noted, "nobody really noticed or even really cared." Audiences danced into the night on country, blues, and whatever else BMI had assembled. ASCAP had gambled that the public would demand Gershwin and Irving Berlin; instead they were content yodeling. Ten months later ASCAP capitulated, accepting 2.75% of radio revenues — well below the original 5%, let alone the 10% Buck had demanded. Buck was removed as ASCAP president; Haverlin became BMI's president. As Jacob Goldstein observed, "if you form a cartel, you better make sure that there's not a substitute for what you're selling."

**Songs as Long-Term Financial Assets**

ASCAP and BMI jointly lobbied for copyright term extensions, ultimately pushing protection to a songwriter's lifetime plus 50 years, later extended to 70 years — meaning a hit song could generate revenue for more than a century. Combined with expanding revenue streams (radio, television, film, vinyl, eight-tracks, cassettes, CDs, streaming), a successful song became what Robert Smith described as "essentially like a long-term bond." A great catalog generates predictable cash flows for a hundred years, and Wall Street eventually took notice.

**Michael Jackson Buys the Beatles**

In 1981, Michael Jackson was visiting Paul McCartney at his English estate. McCartney, who with John Lennon had lost control of some of the Beatles' early songs, counseled Jackson on the importance of owning your rights and showed off a small catalog he had acquired — including Buddy Holly's songs and some Broadway material. Jackson joked that he would someday buy McCartney's music. McCartney later said he thought it was a joke.

Jackson entered the catalog business methodically. He purchased the Sly & the Family Stone catalog for $500,000 and recouped the investment as cover royalties came in. When Australian media tycoon Holmes à Court put the Beatles catalog up for sale in 1984, Jackson made his move. A bidding war with Richard Branson of Virgin Records ensued; in August 1985 Jackson won the catalog for $47.5 million — plus an appearance at a Perth telethon — the most expensive publishing purchase ever by an individual. As part of the deal, Holmes à Court was permitted to gift his daughter one song; hoping she would pick "Yesterday," he let her choose, and she selected "Penny Lane," which she owns to this day.

McCartney felt personally betrayed, particularly when Jackson licensed "Revolution" to Nike. Jackson's response — "Paul, it's just business" — encapsulated the new logic of song ownership. Jackson later sold the rights to Sony for roughly double his purchase price. The transaction demonstrated that song catalogs were dramatically undervalued assets capable of generating substantial returns.

**David Bowie and the Invention of Music-Backed Securities**

In the 1990s, David Bowie owned 50% of his catalog; the other half was controlled by a manager he despised. Rather than sell songs outright to raise buyout funds, his financial advisors devised a securitization. In 1997, Bowie packaged 287 songs — from "Life on Mars" to "Ashes to Ashes" — into a special purpose vehicle and sold slices of the future royalty stream as securities. The "Bowie bonds" paid 7.9% interest and were purchased primarily by an insurance company whose long-term liabilities made long-term royalty assets an attractive match. Bowie raised $55 million and used the proceeds to buy out his manager, regaining full ownership of his catalog.

The timing proved difficult: the bonds were issued near the peak of CD-era revenues, and when internet piracy via Napster and LimeWire caused industry revenues to collapse, the royalty streams fell short of projections and the bonds were eventually downgraded to junk. Nonetheless, the instrument introduced the concept of music-backed securities to institutional investors and drew lasting Wall Street attention to music as a distinct asset class.

**The Financialization of Music and Its Limits**

Following the Bowie bond precedent, institutional capital moved aggressively into music rights. Merck Mercuriadis founded Hipgnosis Songs Fund, which became publicly traded and spent more than two billion dollars acquiring tens of thousands of songs before being acquired by Blackstone. By recent years, investors including Blackstone, Carlisle, and Michigan's State pension fund had raised at least $4.4 billion packaging songs by Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon and Paul McCartney into securities. A side effect of catalog financialization is concentration: it makes investment in already-proven superstars more profitable still, widening the gap between catalog holders and working musicians.

The episode closes with an irony. Despite a century of infrastructure built to monetize recorded and broadcast music — from ASCAP's music cops to Bowie bonds to Blackstone's portfolio — most working musicians today depend primarily on live performance, touring, and merchandise for income. The financialized music economy benefits institutional investors and established catalog holders far more than working songwriters, returning the industry to roughly where it stood before Victor Herbert ordered lobster at Shanley's.
