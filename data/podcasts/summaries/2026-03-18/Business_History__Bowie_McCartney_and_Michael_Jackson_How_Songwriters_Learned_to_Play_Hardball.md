# Business History — "Bowie, McCartney & Michael Jackson: How Songwriters Learned to Play Hardball"

**Host:** Robert Smith. **Guest:** Jacob Goldstein (co-host, Business History).

## TL;DR

- **ASCAP formed in 1914 to enforce songwriter copyrights after Victor Herbert sued a restaurant** — Herbert heard his copyrighted song "Sweethearts" played at Shanley's restaurant in Times Square without payment; the Supreme Court ruled in 1917 that background music performances require royalty payments.
- **ASCAP grew into a powerful cartel controlling 90% of copyrights by 1939** — the organization collected 5% of radio station advertising revenue; president Gene Buck aggressively enforced rights but became so aggressive that several states passed laws against his "mafia-like" tactics.
- **Broadcasters formed rival cartel BMI in 1939 to sign excluded genres like country, blues, and R&B** — when Gene Buck refused to negotiate and announced a rate increase to 10%, radio stations created a competitor; at midnight December 31, 1940, ASCAP music vanished from radios and was replaced with BMI music without public notice.
- **ASCAP lost its monopoly when American audiences didn't miss Broadway composers** — Gene Buck expected national outrage for missing Irving Berlin and Gershwin; instead, listeners danced to yodeling and country music all night; ASCAP ultimately accepted 2.75% of revenues (down from 5%) and lost cultural dominance to rock and roll.
- **Michael Jackson bought the Beatles catalog for $47.5 million in 1985, transforming songs into financial assets** — Paul McCartney had warned Jackson to own his music; Jackson assembled a portfolio of music rights, doubled his money when selling to Sony, and demonstrated that classic songs were severely underpriced as long-term revenue-generating assets.
- **David Bowie issued $55 million in securities backed by his song royalties in 1997** — Bowie packaged 287 songs into a "Bowie Bond" securitization, allowing him to own his music while borrowing against future royalties; insurance companies bought these bonds expecting stable, long-term returns matching their liabilities.
- **Wall Street now packages musician catalogs into billion-dollar securities** — as of 2026, investors including Blackstone, Carlyle, and Michigan's pension fund have raised $4.4+ billion in securities backed by catalogs of Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, John Lennon, and Paul McCartney; financialization has made superstars vastly richer but pushed ordinary musicians back to live performance revenue.

**Victor Herbert's Lawsuit and the Birth of ASCAP**

In 1913, composer Victor Herbert heard his song "Sweethearts" (from a Broadway show) played by a band at Shanley's restaurant in Times Square while he ate lobster. Herbert demanded payment; the restaurant owner refused, claiming background music in a restaurant wasn't a "public performance" under copyright law and that since the restaurant charged no admission, it didn't owe royalties. The dispute revealed a fundamental problem: as urbanization and leisure time grew in the early 20th century, restaurants, dance halls, and hotels increasingly played copyrighted music without compensating composers—and it was economically impossible for any individual composer to monitor and sue each venue.

Herbert assembled eight music publishers at a restaurant called Lucho's and founded ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers) in 1914, recruiting songwriters from Tin Pan Alley including Irving Berlin and John Philip Sousa. When Herbert v. Shanley reached the Supreme Court in 1917, Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes rejected the "incidental music" argument, ruling that if music generates profit for a business (even a restaurant), the songwriter must be paid. ASCAP's strategy was elegant: send "music cops" to document songs played at venues, sue owners (who would certainly lose), then offer a simple licensing deal—pay a small percentage of revenues and play anything from ASCAP's collective without worry.

By 1921, ASCAP's enforcement costs were covered by licensing fees; the system worked. However, ASCAP was selective about membership, excluding jazz, blues, and "hillbilly" musicians, creating a cultural monopoly favoring Broadway and New York composers.

**Gene Buck's Aggressive Monopoly and Radio's Challenge**

Gene Buck, a lyricist nicknamed "Mr. Broadway," became ASCAP president and transformed the organization into what members described as "the Godfather." When radio emerged in 1921 as the dominant music distribution medium, ASCAP demanded payment; the FCC ruled in 1924 that radio was a public performance and copyright applied. By 1935, Buck negotiated a deal requiring radio stations to pay ASCAP 5% of advertising revenue—millions of dollars annually.

Buck's refusal to compromise provoked a backlash. Radio stations appealed to state legislatures; Montana, Nebraska, Louisiana, and Tennessee passed laws against ASCAP's "extortion," with Montana even issuing a warrant for Buck's arrest on extortion charges while he visited Phoenix (bail: $10,000). Unintimidated, Buck announced in 1940 that he would raise rates to 10% effective December 31, 1940—or ASCAP music would vanish from radio.

**The BMI Coup: Competition, Country Music, and Yodeling**

Rather than capitulate, radio broadcasters formed Broadcast Music Incorporated (BMI) in February 1940 and hired Carl Haviland to sign alternative music catalogs. BMI recruited the Carter Family, Jimmy Rodgers, and country artists ASCAP had excluded. On December 31, 1940, ASCAP's ultimatum deadline, radio stations switched entirely to BMI music at midnight. Gene Buck expected outrage—listeners demanding Gershwin and Berlin. Instead, listeners heard yodeling and country songs and, according to the transcript, "danced all night long" without noticing the difference. Nobody demanded the "real" songwriters back.

The psychological impact was devastating for Buck: Americans didn't care enough about Broadway monopoly to fight for it. ASCAP capitulated, accepting 2.75% of station revenues (down from 5%) and, crucially, losing cultural dominance as BMI signed Elvis Presley and rock-and-roll artists. ASCAP later signed Bob Dylan and adapted to electric rock to remain relevant. Carl Haviland became BMI president.

**The Cartels' Unintended Consequence: Songs as Financial Assets**

The ASCAP-BMI duopoly created a stable, profitable market for songwriting. Crucially, it transformed songs from ephemeral performances into legally protected, long-term revenue streams. Copyright protections were extended repeatedly throughout the 20th century—first to the songwriter's lifetime plus 50 years, later to 70 years. A hit song could now generate royalties for over a century across radio, television, movies, records, cassettes, CDs, and satellite radio. Hotels, shopping malls, and streaming services all paid fees for repeated playback.

This financialization attracted Wall Street. Michael Jackson, sitting on $9 million in 1981, learned from Paul McCartney that owning song rights was more valuable than being a performer. Jackson bought the Sly and the Family Stone catalog for $500,000, then acquired the Beatles catalog for $47.5 million in 1985 (later increased to $47.5 million through a bidding war with Richard Branson). The purchase shocked the music industry—Beatles songs seemed priceless and unquantifiable, yet Jackson's valuation proved accurate. When he later sold the catalog to Sony at roughly double, the music industry recognized that classic songs were undervalued revenue-generating assets.

**Securitization and the Billionaire Musician**

David Bowie's innovation was structural. In 1997, Bowie owned 50% of his catalog but despised his manager, who owned the other 50%. Bowie's financial advisors devised a securitization: package 287 Bowie songs (Life on Mars, Ashes to Ashes, etc.) into a special-purpose vehicle and issue securities backed by future royalties. Bowie issued $55 million in "Bowie Bonds" paying 7.9% interest, bought primarily by insurance companies seeking long-term, stable cash flows matching their long-term liabilities. Bowie kept ownership of his songs while using the bond proceeds to buy back his manager's 50% stake. The strategy worked brilliantly until internet piracy (Napster, LimeWire) devastated music industry revenues; the bonds were eventually rated junk, but they proved the concept worked.

Today, Wall Street actively packages musician catalogs into securities. As of 2026, firms including Blackstone, Carlyle, and Michigan's state pension fund have raised at least $4.4 billion in securities backed by catalogs of Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and the Beatles. This financialization has made superstar musicians vastly richer—they access capital markets like major corporations—but it has also inverted the original ASCAP problem. One hundred years ago, songwriters couldn't get paid for their work; today, superstar catalogs are Wall Street assets, while ordinary musicians must return to live performance (the only revenue stream not yet financialized) to earn a living. The pie grew, as one host noted, but the slice distribution became more unequal.
