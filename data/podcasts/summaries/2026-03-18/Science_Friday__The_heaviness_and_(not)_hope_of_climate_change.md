# Science Friday — "The heaviness and (not) hope of climate change"

**Host:** Flora Lichtman. **Guests:** Elizabeth Colbert (staff writer, The New Yorker; Pulitzer Prize winner 2015; author of *Life on a Little-Known Planet: Dispatches from a Changing World*).

## TL;DR

- **Caterpillars power entire ecosystems** — they transfer enormous amounts of energy from plants to animals and are a critical food source for birds, yet scientists don't fully understand which food plants different caterpillar species need, making conservation of moths and butterflies difficult.
- **The insect apocalypse is real and underreported** — insect populations are declining "just about everywhere that people look," and since insects make up the majority of Earth's species and provide pollination, seed dispersal, and decomposition, their loss threatens planetary function.
- **Samso Island achieved carbon neutrality through community engagement and smart policy** — the Danish farming community transitioned entirely to renewable energy (wind turbines on farms and offshore) by turning energy conservation into a competitive "sport" coupled with national policies that made it economically sensible.
- **Climate change has pushed humans into unknown territory** — modern humans evolved in climates colder than today; current warming pushes beyond the envelope of natural history ("an analog for what we're doing to the climate right now" doesn't exist even millions of years back), and we're "sleepwalking into the unknown."
- **Coral reefs face functional extinction from bleaching** — global coral bleaching events over recent years have created "big dead patches"; scientists are exploring heat-tolerant symbiont breeding, but scaling efforts to reef sizes like the Great Barrier (the size of Italy) is extraordinarily difficult.
- **Hope is a cliche that obscures harder truths** — Colbert argues against forcing hopeful endings on climate stories; instead, the focus should be on showing "paths forward" and relying on humans' ability to foresee futures, worry, take action, and be creative—capacities that have gotten the species through hard times.
- **The technology to stabilize climate exists and is affordable** — solar, wind, and battery costs have dropped dramatically since Colbert started covering climate, creating a real toolbox to address the crisis, yet the current U.S. administration is working to promote fossil fuels instead.

**Caterpillars: Understudied Keystone Species**

Colbert describes her work with entomologist Dave Wagner, one of the world's leading caterpillar experts, where they searched for caterpillars using a beating sheet—a kite-like tool placed under plants that are then whacked with a pole, causing caterpillars and debris to fall into the sheet. Wagner is working on a four-volume encyclopedic guide to Western North American caterpillars, a work that Colbert compares to "Gravity's Rainbow" for its ambitious scope and the uncertainty that one lifetime will ever complete it. This reflects how little is known about caterpillars despite their critical ecological role.

Caterpillars are the larval stage of moths and butterflies and are "hugely important to ecosystems" because they transfer enormous energy from plant matter (photosynthesis) into the animal kingdom, serving as a massive food source for birds and other creatures. Yet conservation efforts are hampered because scientists don't fully understand what food plants different caterpillar species require. This knowledge gap means "the conservation of a lot of species of moths and butterflies depends on knowing that entire life cycle." Wagner's encyclopedia is a response to this gap, though the number of caterpillar species—likely more than Wagner will ever catalog—testifies to how vast the unknown remains.

**The Insect Apocalypse and Planetary Consequences**

Colbert stresses that the decline in insect populations should "really be a much bigger story than it has been." Insects comprise the majority of species on Earth—"to a first approximation, all species on earth are insects"—and they make the world function: they disperse seeds, pollinate, decompose, and serve as a foundational food source for other groups. When insect numbers fall, "you are really doing something very serious to the planet." The news is uniformly bad: "just about everywhere that people look, they find very serious declines in insect numbers."

**Samso: A Carbon-Neutral Community and the Hope Trap**

In contrast, Colbert's 2008 story about Samso, a small Danish island community of farmers (potato and strawberry growers) and tourists, showed a hopeful example. The community achieved carbon neutrality through a combination of community engagement and smart national policies. Energy use became a competitive "game" where residents focused on conservation and emissions reduction, turning what could have been a burden into an engaging challenge. Coupling this social dynamic were policies that economically incentivized farmers to install wind turbines on their land, and the island also built significant offshore wind capacity.

However, Colbert expresses surprise and disappointment that "how few communities have followed that example" in the nearly 20 years since. While Samso proves "what can be done when people put their minds to it," the limited replication suggests that the translation from example to widespread practice remains elusive.

**Humans Beyond Their Climate Envelope**

Colbert describes a troubling scientific reality: climate scientists confirm that modern humans are "basically leaving the climate envelope under which humanity evolved." Modern humans are only a few hundred thousand years old, and for most of that period the climate was "a good deal colder" than today. The warmest interglacial periods in that history were "roughly as warm as we are now," but current warming is pushing beyond that into a hotter world that "even our distant ancestors haven't experienced." The problem is that we lack an analog: "in the whole history of the planet, basically, or certainly for many, many millions of years," there's no precedent for what humanity is doing to the climate. As a result, we are "sleepwalking into the unknown," a "dangerous situation to be in."

**The Great Barrier Reef: Beauty and Bleaching**

Colbert describes living on a tiny coral island formed from reef fragments, where she snorkeled in an "extraordinary profusion of life"—a "kaleidoscope" of sharks, sea cucumbers, and brilliantly colored fish that cannot be found on land. This experience "really tilts your worldview" for those raised in impoverished temperate ecosystems. Yet this beauty is under extreme threat. Coral reefs are a "very threatened ecosystem" facing potential "functional extinction." Bleaching occurs when corals expel their algal symbionts (which provide much of their nutrition), turning white. Recent years have seen "really serious coral bleaching events, global coral bleaching events, several of them." The result: large dead patches in reefs.

Scientists are pursuing interventions—breeding heat-tolerant symbionts or encouraging corals to take up more resilient algal partners—and some facilities, like the University of Miami, are achieving "some success." But the scale problem is staggering: the Great Barrier Reef alone is "the size of Italy," making any intervention at the necessary scale "really difficult." Still, researchers are working to scale up these efforts to help corals "get through the next century."

**Rejecting the Hope Cliche and Embracing Action**

Colbert pushes back against the pressure to end climate stories on a hopeful note. She acknowledges the theory: if you give people bad news without hope, they won't act. But she believes this misses the point. Instead of forcing hope, she works to show "paths forward," and she emphasizes that taking action "couldn't be more urgent." The critical distinction is between hope as narrative cliche and the deeper human capacities that actually matter.

Humans can "foresee the future," "worry," "take action," and "be very creative"—capacities that have "gotten us through pretty hard times in the past." Perhaps, she reflects, there is "something deep in our wiring" that preserves us and will "get us to where we need to go through the rest of the 21st century." But that's an open question, not a guarantee, and framing it as inevitable hope risks complacency.

**The Reality of Climate Solutions**

Colbert emphasizes an often-overlooked fact: the technologies needed to stabilize climate (not "solve" it—climate change, once started, persists even after carbon emissions stop) already exist and have become affordable. Solar, wind, and various battery systems have "all come down tremendously in price since I started working on this issue." This is "a huge boon" and proof that "there are tools" and a real "toolbox to address this." The tools are there. The problem is political: "the current administration is doing its darndest to promote the use of fossil fuels, which is exactly what we shouldn't be doing." The result is a contradiction—we have the affordable tools to make a real difference, yet the U.S. is working to "strangle them" in favor of fossil fuel expansion.
