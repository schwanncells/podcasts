# The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart — "The Real Election Threat with Casey Newton and Renée DiResta"

**Host:** Jon Stewart. **Guests:** Renée DiResta (associate research professor, Georgetown McCourt School of Public Policy; author of *Invisible Rulers: The People Who Turn Lies into Reality*; former lead researcher, Stanford Internet Observatory); Casey Newton (editor, Platformer; co-host of the New York Times' *Hard Fork* podcast).

## TL;DR

- **Non-citizen voting is statistically nonexistent**, yet roughly 50% of Americans believe it is a massive problem — even Cato Institute and Heritage Foundation studies find it "infinitesimally small," and only about 1,500 documented cases have occurred since 1982.
- **The SAVE Act would disenfranchise an estimated 69 million women** who took their spouse's name and lack a birth certificate matching their current legal name, disproportionately hitting Democratic-leaning voters.
- **Election rumors spread through a three-part engine** — influencers seed content, algorithms amplify high-engagement posts, and online crowds carry stories across platforms; bots inflate like and retweet counts to trip algorithmic thresholds before real users ever see the content.
- **Stanford's Election Integrity Partnership tracked 22 million tweets** across the top 10 most viral 2020 election rumors; platforms acted on only ~40% of the 3,000 URLs the project flagged — 60% received no action, 30% got a label, 10% were removed.
- **Jim Jordan's Weaponization Committee misrepresented the 22 million figure as proof of censorship**, despite it being a post-election data pull published in March 2021, long after it could have affected the outcome; Stephen Miller subsequently sued DiResta in a hand-picked Louisiana court using the same theory.
- **Elon Musk interacted with non-citizen voting stories roughly 1,300 times** on X, and an independent study found his algorithmic feed moved users "significantly" to the right compared to a chronological control group — a far greater democratic distortion than any mythical undocumented voter.
- **Musk rewrote X's algorithm to boost his own tweets** after a Biden Super Bowl post outperformed his, and has banned journalists, censored the Turkish opposition at government request, and defended his AI's ability to generate non-consensual nude images as protected speech — all while claiming free speech absolutism.
- **The industry's political realignment is transactional, not ideological**: Musk donated $250+ million to Trump and gained approximately $234 billion in net worth; Bezos paid ~$80 million in Melania-related content and advertising and is up roughly $15 billion; these men were "essentially good liberals" until Democratic administrations tried to regulate them.
- **The Supreme Court rebuked the censorship narrative in *Murthy v. Missouri***: Amy Coney Barrett wrote that the lower court had "clearly erroneous findings," dismissed the case for lack of standing, and noted that not a single White House email so much as mentioned plaintiff Jay Bhattacharya.
- **The proposed antidote is a "slow food" media diet** — long-form podcasts, newsletters, and direct counter-speech rather than suppression; DiResta argues institutions must fight openly rather than retreat, because silence only makes them easier targets.

**The SAVE Act and the Myth of the Non-Citizen Voter**

Stewart frames the episode around the Senate's push to pass the SAVE Act — the Safeguard American Voter Eligibility Act — which would require documentary proof of citizenship to register to vote. He notes that roughly 70% of Americans support the act in principle, since the idea that voting should be restricted to citizens is broadly uncontroversial. The problem, he argues, is that the legislation treats a near-nonexistent problem as a crisis. DiResta and Newton both confirm that elections are decided by American citizens; even the Cato Institute and Heritage Foundation — libertarian and right-wing, respectively — find voter fraud "infinitesimally small." A figure Stewart cites puts documented cases at roughly 1,500 total since 1982.

Newton raises the practical harm: he cites a study estimating that as many as 69 million women who took their spouse's name lack a birth certificate matching their current legal name, potentially disqualifying them from registering. Trans voters would face similar barriers. The demographic pattern tracks closely with groups that lean Democratic — suggesting the policy's effect, whatever its stated intent, would be to reduce turnout among the opposing party's base.

**How Election Rumors Spread**

DiResta explains that election fraud claims are what researchers call "tropes" — recurring, emotionally resonant stories with a traceable lineage that predates social media. She uses the word "rumor" deliberately rather than "lie," because a rumor does not require intent to deceive; it only needs to feel plausible and to spread person to person. The three-part propagation engine consists of influencers who seed the content, algorithms that amplify high-engagement posts, and online crowds that carry stories across platforms. Bots serve primarily as amplifiers, inflating like and retweet counts to trip the algorithmic threshold that then serves content to real users. The actual narrative creation is done by ideologically motivated influencers — some paid, some purely true believers who have built financial identities around their content.

Newton adds that the demand side matters as much as the supply side. In 2020, when Trump had clearly lost, there was enormous right-wing demand for an alternative reality; in 2024, after he won, that demand evaporated and fraud claims largely disappeared. The algorithms are built to give people what they want to feel, so when the emotional demand for a narrative is high, the ecosystem furnishes it.

**Why Facts Can't Win**

DiResta describes the structural disadvantage facing accurate information. Election officials — often regular citizens working a second job — typically have small social followings. By the time one posts a careful correction, the rumor has already been amplified by influencers with millions of followers and has reached a deeply distrustful crowd that has been told for years its vote is being stolen. Suppression creates a second-order problem: attempts to remove viral content are interpreted as proof that authorities are hiding the truth — what she calls the Streisand effect. She describes what platforms were doing until recently as "upranking good information," an active editorial effort to surface authoritative content in search results; that effort has since been largely abandoned. Unless platforms actively surface good information, she says, it simply doesn't reach people.

**The Stanford Election Integrity Partnership and Its Aftermath**

DiResta details the project she ran at Stanford during the 2020 election. Four research centers and roughly 120 students used a JIRA-style ticketing system to trace viral rumors in real time, flagging them to platforms and election officials. The project sent approximately 3,000 URLs to social media companies during the election period. Platforms acted on about 40% of them: roughly 30% received a label ("this content is disputed"), roughly 10% were removed, and 60% received no action — evidence, DiResta argues, that the researchers did not pressure platforms into compliance. The project also worked with state and local election officials, including flagging an Iranian influence operation in which foreign accounts impersonated the Proud Boys.

After the election, DiResta's team published a post-election data pull showing that the top 10 most viral election rumors — Dominion machines flipping votes, Italian space laser theories, Sharpie markers in Arizona — totaled 22 million tweets. Two years later, Jim Jordan's Weaponization Committee reframed that number as evidence that the researchers had *censored* 22 million tweets as part of a Biden administration plot. Stewart notes the agencies the project communicated with were run by Trump appointees. Newton says the hearings were fundamentally about "creating a spectacle and manufacturing this sense of grievance" to justify further steps to disenfranchise voters — not about any principled concern for speech.

**Murthy v. Missouri and What the Courts Found**

The case originated with the attorneys general of Missouri and Louisiana — both election deniers — who alleged a Biden censorship regime, despite the fact that the relevant government communications during 2020 involved Trump appointees. To sidestep that inconvenience, the plaintiffs pivoted to COVID-era communications and invoked the "deep state" — an unfalsifiable claim that could absorb any inconvenient fact. They deposed Fauci, FBI agents, and DHS officials. Their strongest piece of evidence was a series of emails from White House Digital Director Rob Flaherty — many of which, DiResta says, turned out to concern the White House's own Instagram account rather than any suppression order. The Twitter Files suffered the same problem: she says they were literally "taking emails and cutting them in half and pretending the top half said something it didn't."

The case was first heard by a hand-picked Louisiana judge who declared it "the biggest censorship effort the world has ever seen" and issued a sweeping injunction. The Fifth Circuit — itself quite conservative — partially walked that back. When it reached the Supreme Court, Amy Coney Barrett wrote the majority opinion, finding "clearly erroneous findings" by the lower court and dismissing the case for lack of standing: not a single White House email so much as mentioned plaintiff Jay Bhattacharya, now the CDC director. DiResta notes that Stephen Miller subsequently filed a similar lawsuit against her in the same Louisiana court — alleging she acted as a de facto government agent — which she describes as litigation designed to drain resources and silence critics for years rather than to win on the merits.

**Elon Musk and the X Algorithm as Political Infrastructure**

Newton and DiResta walk through the evidence that X is not a neutral information platform but an actively engineered political instrument. Newton reports that after Musk bought Twitter, Musk grew unhappy that a Biden Super Bowl tweet outperformed his own, and instructed engineers to re-engineer the algorithm to give his content disproportionate prominence regardless of whether users follow him. An independent study comparing X's ranked algorithmic feed to a chronological feed found that people on the algorithmic feed moved measurably further to the right over time. Musk has interacted with non-citizen voting stories roughly 1,300 times, Stewart notes.

Newton catalogs other inconsistencies in Musk's free speech absolutism: he banned journalists for including their Instagram bio in their Twitter profile, removed the Turkish opposition at government request, and defended his AI's ability to generate non-consensual nude images as protected speech — a claim DiResta flags as the moment the concept of censorship "just lost all meaning." She frames this as Musk "borrowing the moral weight of the word censorship while emptying it of moral content." Both speakers note that the First Amendment right on a private platform legally belongs to the platform, not the user — and that conservatives have consistently argued exactly that when it benefited them.

**Big Tech's Transactional Politics**

Stewart draws attention to the financial logic underlying the industry's political realignment. Musk donated more than $250 million to Trump and Republicans and gained approximately $234 billion in net worth. Bezos paid around $40 million for a Melania documentary and further advertising deals, cut the Washington Post, and is up roughly $15 billion. Zuckerberg's $400 million in pandemic-era election infrastructure spending was weaponized as a 2020 fraud narrative, yet he was reportedly heard asking Trump how much he should say he was donating.

Newton argues that most of these figures were "essentially good liberals" in the 2010s — their content policies were rooted in human rights frameworks — but found that Democratic administrations came with regulatory headaches: Biden pursued antitrust actions against Meta and Amazon, created regulatory friction for Musk, and appointed Lena Khan to the FTC. Trump, by contrast, operates transactionally: "agree to build part of his ballroom and he gives you whatever you want." DiResta agrees, adding that controlling X specifically is about owning "an incredibly potent infrastructure" for activation and reach — a tool with no functional equivalent on the fragmented left, where audience has dispersed across Bluesky, Threads, and other platforms without coalescing into a comparable organizing force.

**Free Speech vs. Algorithmic Speech**

Stewart articulates a distinction he considers foundational: free speech is not the same as algorithmic speech, which he calls "ultra-processed" — "Twitter speech is free speech in the way that Doritos are food." DiResta traces the phrase "freedom of speech, not freedom of reach" to an argument she and Asa Raskin developed in 2018, intended to articulate that platforms bear responsibility for what they amplify, not merely what they permit to exist. She argues that during COVID, platforms made reasonable choices to surface CDC information in search results, and that while they probably over-removed some content — she singles out the lab leak hypothesis policy as "a very dumb policy" — that policy was Meta-only, lasted only three months, and was not the industry-wide multi-year suppression it has been portrayed as.

**What Might Help**

Newton proposes a "slow food" media movement as the structural alternative: long-form podcasts, newsletters, and other sustained-attention formats that surface nuance and reasoning. He argues that trying to make Instagram or X marginally better is a losing game, and that restricting children's access to social media — a policy now spreading across many countries, with bans for users under 16 — may ultimately shift adult norms in the same way that restricting children from smoking contributed to broader cultural change. DiResta is more skeptical that corporate self-reform will move the needle, noting that a decade of academic engagement and policy feedback has not produced meaningful results. Her piece written after Stanford shut down the Internet Observatory argued that institutions must fight publicly — "you have to fight in the arena" — rather than go quiet under legal and reputational pressure, because retreat only validates the attacks and makes future ones cheaper to mount.
