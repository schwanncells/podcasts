# Business History — "Bowie, McCartney & Michael Jackson: How Songwriters Learned to Play Hardball"

**Host:** Robert Smith and Jacob Goldstein.

## TL;DR

- **Victor Herbert v. Shanley (1917):** Supreme Court Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes ruled that restaurants must pay composers for copyrighted music played in the background, even if no admission was charged—establishing the principle that public profit-generating uses require royalty payments.
- **ASCAP formed in 1914:** Composers banded together to enforce copyright through collective action, hiring "music cops" to monitor establishments and sue violators, then offering licenses in exchange for 5% of radio station advertising revenue.
- **Gene Buck's overreach:** As ASCAP president, Buck announced a rate increase from 5% to 10% of radio revenues effective December 31, 1940, threatening to pull all music from the air if stations refused, sparking industry revolt.
- **BMI created in 1939:** Radio broadcasters launched Broadcast Music Incorporated as a rival collective to sign excluded artists—country, blues, R&B, gospel, and Latin musicians that ASCAP had rejected—recruiting Carl Havilin to sign 750 stations in ten months.
- **Midnight of December 31, 1940:** When ASCAP's ultimatum took effect, radio stations seamlessly switched to BMI music; audiences didn't notice or care, listeners "yodeled all night long," and Gene Buck's cartel power collapsed.
- **ASCAP settled for 2.75% of revenues:** Down from the original 5% pre-demand, demonstrating that competition broke the cartel and expanded the music market for superstars and new genres.
- **Michael Jackson bought Beatles catalog in 1985 for $47.5 million:** He monetized it through commercial licensing (Nike's "Revolution" ad) and later sold it to Sony for double the purchase price, proving music rights were underpriced financial assets.
- **David Bowie issued securitized Bowie Bonds in 1997:** He bundled 287 songs into a special purpose vehicle, sold securities paying 7.9% interest to insurance companies, and raised $55 million to buy back the half of his catalog he didn't own—inventing a template for financializing music royalties that became a multi-billion-dollar asset class.

**The Birth of Songwriter Power: Herbert v. Shanley**

In 1913, composer Victor Herbert walked into a New York restaurant called Shanley's and heard his own song from the Broadway musical *Sweethearts* being performed by the restaurant's orchestra. Herbert owned the copyright and demanded payment; Shanley refused, arguing the music was merely incidental background ambiance in a restaurant without paid admission. Herbert sued. The case reached the Supreme Court in 1917, where Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes rejected Shanley's argument. Holmes wrote that "if the music did not pay, it would be given up. If it pays, the purpose of employing it is profit, and that is enough." The ruling established that any public performance where the owner made money—restaurants, dance halls, and soon radio stations—required payment to composers.

**ASCAP as Cartel and Enforcer**

Reacting to Herbert's struggle, composers in Tin Pan Alley formed the American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers (ASCAP) in 1914. ASCAP employed "music cops" to identify and document copyrighted music in restaurants and other establishments, then sued violators. The organization offered an exit: pay a small percentage of revenues to ASCAP, and you could play any song in the collective's catalog without worrying about individual lawsuits. Radio broadcasters agreed to pay 5% of their advertising revenue. This system worked smoothly through the 1920s and 1930s until Gene Buck, a prolific songwriter and Tin Pan Alley insider, took over as ASCAP president. Buck was ruthless. He saw radio as the future and moved to double the rate to 10%, set to take effect December 31, 1940. Buck did not show up to negotiate; he was instead sailing to the Bohemian Grove to hobnob with the elite. Radio stations, facing sudden extinction of music, decided to create a rival cartel.

**BMI as Competitive Counterweight**

In February 1940, radio broadcasters launched Broadcast Music Incorporated (BMI) as an alternative to ASCAP. Unlike ASCAP, which had rejected country, blues, R&B, gospel, and Latin musicians as insufficiently refined, BMI welcomed the genres and artists ASCAP excluded. A charismatic station relations manager named Carl Havilin traveled the country signing radio stations to BMI licenses in ten months—a remarkable feat given the deadline. He understood something Gene Buck did not: Americans outside New York wanted to hear music beyond Broadway standards. Havilin had once arranged for Amelia Earhart to hear her favorite country song on the radio during her solo flight attempt; he knew local and regional music had value.

**The New Year's Eve Gamble and BMI's Unexpected Victory**

On December 31, 1940, at midnight, ASCAP's ultimatum took effect. Listeners across America tuned their radios expecting silence. Instead, they heard music—BMI music. Stations played songs from their new BMI roster, including yodeling country acts that ASCAP had scorned. Audiences didn't notice the switch; to them, music was music. Gene Buck, who had expected Americans to riot in the streets demanding Gershwin and Irving Berlin, was devastated. Ten months later, ASCAP capitulated. The cartel agreed to accept 2.75% of radio revenues—nearly half the 5% it had previously extracted. More importantly, the existence of BMI forced ASCAP to reckon with rock and roll, blues, and country music, eventually signing Elvis Presley and later accommodating Bob Dylan's electric turn. The lesson: when a monopoly overreaches, competition breaks the cartel and expands the pie for everyone.

**Songs as Financial Assets: Michael Jackson's Beatles Bet**

In 1981, Michael Jackson and Paul McCartney met at McCartney's estate in London. McCartney warned Jackson about the importance of owning your own songs, noting that he and John Lennon had lost some of their rights early in their career. Jackson, sitting on $9 million in earnings, took the hint and began buying music catalogs. In 1985, Australian media tycoon Holmes à Court put the Beatles catalog up for sale. Jackson made an audacious bid: $47.5 million for the rights to their entire catalog—a massive valuation that industry observers thought was insane. He won. Jackson did not merely hold the catalog; he monetized it aggressively, licensing Beatles music to Nike for a "Revolution" ad that made hippies cringe but made Jackson money. He later sold the rights to Sony for double his purchase price. The Jackson lesson penetrated Wall Street: songs were not sentimental artifacts—they were underpriced financial assets with predictable, century-long cash flows.

**Bowie Bonds and the Financialization of Music**

In the 1990s, David Bowie owned only 50% of his own songs; the other half was owned by a manager he despised. Bowie wanted to buy back the rights but needed $50+ million. His financial advisors invented a solution: securitization. In 1997, Bowie bundled 287 of his songs—from "Life on Mars" to "Ashes to Ashes"—into a special purpose vehicle and sold slices of future royalties as securities paying 7.9% interest. Insurance companies, needing long-term assets to match long-term liabilities, bought the bonds. Bowie raised $55 million and used it to buy back his catalog from the manager he hated. The Bowie Bond worked as intended, though it later suffered when internet piracy plummeted music revenues. More importantly, the structure opened the door to a multi-billion-dollar asset class. In recent years, investors including Blackstone and private equity firms have raised at least $4.4 billion buying up music rights from superstar catalogs and packaging them into securities. Songs now live forever in spreadsheets as revenue-generating machines, with ownership fragmented across investment firms, record labels, and publishers. A hundred years after Victor Herbert fought for the right to be paid for his music, songwriters—especially superstars—have become part of the financialized global economy, even as working musicians struggle to earn money in an era of streaming and free internet music.
