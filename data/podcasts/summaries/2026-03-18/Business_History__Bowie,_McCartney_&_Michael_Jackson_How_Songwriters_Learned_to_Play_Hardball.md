# Business History — "Bowie, McCartney & Michael Jackson: How Songwriters Learned to Play Hardball"

**Hosts:** Robert Smith and Jacob Goldstein (co-hosts, Business History, Pushkin Industries).

## TL;DR

- **Victor Herbert founded ASCAP in 1914** after suing Shanley's restaurant in Times Square for playing his music without payment; the Supreme Court ruled in 1917 that background music in for-profit venues requires licensing, even if no admission is charged.
- **ASCAP controlled roughly 90% of music copyrights by 1939** and, under president Gene Buck, demanded a doubling of radio royalties from 5% to 10% of advertising revenue starting January 1941 — or they would pull all music from the air.
- **Broadcasters formed rival organization BMI in February 1940** and signed the artists ASCAP had excluded — country, blues, R&B, gospel, and Latin — giving radio stations a substitute; when midnight arrived on New Year's 1941, listeners heard BMI music and barely noticed.
- **ASCAP caved entirely**, accepting 2.75% — barely more than half of the 5% they charged before escalating — and Gene Buck was ousted as president.
- **Copyright extension and the royalty stack transformed songs into long-term financial assets**, generating predictable cash flows for the songwriter's lifetime plus up to 70 years across radio, TV, film, records, eight-tracks, cassettes, and CDs.
- **Michael Jackson paid $47.5 million for the Beatles catalog in 1985** — then the most expensive publishing purchase ever by an individual — after Paul McCartney himself had casually suggested Jackson look into buying songs as investments; Jackson later doubled his money selling to Sony.
- **David Bowie securitized 287 songs in 1997 into "Bowie bonds"** paying 7.9% interest, raising $55 million to buy back his rights from a manager he despised; the bonds were later downgraded to junk after Napster gutted royalty revenues.
- **Modern investors including Blackstone, Carlyle, and Michigan's state pension fund have raised at least $4.4 billion** packaging songs by Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon into securities — while most working musicians are back to making their real money on tour.

**Victor Herbert, a Restaurant, and the Copyright Problem**

In 1913, composer Victor Herbert walked into Shanley's, a fashionable Times Square restaurant known for its lobster and its house band. While eating, he heard the band playing a song he had written — from his Broadway show *Sweethearts* — and realized he was not receiving a cent. When he called the owner and demanded payment, Shanley refused, arguing that since the restaurant charged no admission, the music was merely incidental atmosphere and not a "performance" subject to copyright.

Smith frames the incident as a crystallization of a much larger problem: the United States was urbanizing, leisure spending was growing, and hit songs existed for the first time — distributed via Broadway and restaurants rather than radio, which barely existed yet. Every piano in every dining room and dance hall was playing copyrighted music, and none of the composers were being paid. Herbert sued, and the National Association of Hotel and Restaurant Owners funded Shanley's defense, hoping a court would declare that background music was legally irrelevant.

**ASCAP Forms, Wins, and Expands to Radio**

Herbert assembled eight music-publishing heavyweights at a downtown German restaurant in 1914 and founded the American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers — ASCAP. Its roster quickly included Irving Berlin, John Philip Sousa, and a young lyricist named Gene Buck. When *Herbert v. Shanley* reached the Supreme Court in 1917, Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. wrote the ruling: "If the music did not pay, it would be given up. If it pays, it pays out of the public pocketbook." The decision established that any commercial use of copyrighted music — even background music in a for-profit establishment — requires a license.

ASCAP's enforcement model was straightforward: send music inspectors to restaurants, hotels, and burlesque halls to record what was played, then threaten lawsuits. Because everyone knew they would lose, most venues quickly took ASCAP's alternative offer — pay a small percentage of revenues and play anything in the catalog. When radio arrived and began broadcasting live orchestras in the early 1920s, the same logic applied. Radio stations tried arguing that studio broadcasts were "private performances," that they promoted songwriters for free and so should owe nothing, and even that they did not broadcast music but merely "emanated electrical energy." All of these arguments failed. By 1924, the FCC ruled that radio constituted a public performance subject to copyright. By 1921, ASCAP's enforcement costs were already being outpaced by royalty income.

**Gene Buck's Overreach and the War with Broadcasters**

ASCAP's power invited overreach. Gene Buck, elected president of ASCAP and celebrated by Time magazine as the "Generalissimo of the clangorous army of Tin Pan Alleymen," ran the organization with contempt for anyone outside Broadway's orbit. ASCAP excluded jazz, blues, R&B, hillbilly (country), and Latin musicians — Buck showed little interest in Black artists or regional music. By 1939, ASCAP controlled approximately 90% of music copyrights and was extracting 5% of radio advertising revenues. Buck then announced that the rate would double to 10% when contracts expired on December 31, 1940.

When the heads of the major radio networks arrived at the Ritz Tower to negotiate, Buck simply didn't show — he was on a train to the Bohemian Grove to mingle with the powerful elite. The radio executives called a war meeting and resolved to form a rival organization. Broadcast Music Incorporated — BMI — was announced to a standing ovation. Their insight was that ASCAP's exclusivity was its vulnerability: there was an enormous body of music ASCAP had never captured, and it happened to be exactly what most Americans outside New York actually wanted to hear.

**BMI Signs Country and the Midnight That Wasn't**

BMI recruited Carl Haverlin as its station relations manager, a radio veteran who understood working-class American tastes. While ASCAP's members were staging elaborate Broadway productions, Haverlin knew that audiences in bars and honky-tonks wanted the Carter Family, Jimmy Rodgers, and the country and blues artists ASCAP had dismissed as low-class. BMI gave itself ten months — from February 1940 to December 31 — to sign enough stations and artists to survive the ASCAP blackout.

When the clock struck midnight and the ASCAP contracts expired, listeners tuning in across the country heard music continue uninterrupted. It was BMI music — and nobody cared. They partied on. Smith notes that Buck had apparently believed Americans would take to the streets demanding Gershwin and Irving Berlin; instead, they yodeled along all night. Within months, ASCAP caved. The final agreement set rates at 2.75% of radio revenues — barely more than half of the 5% they had charged before escalating. Buck was removed as president. Carl Haverlin became president of BMI.

**Songs Become Assets — The Financial Logic of Royalties**

With two competing licensing organizations creating a stable, well-enforced market, songs transformed from cultural objects into financial instruments. ASCAP and BMI lobbied successfully to extend copyright protection to a songwriter's lifetime plus 50 years — eventually extended to 70 years — meaning a single hit song could generate royalties for well over a century. The revenue streams multiplied: radio gave way to television, film licensing, records, eight-tracks, cassettes, and CDs. A song like "Hotel California," Smith observes, is played somewhere every single day in perpetuity.

Goldstein articulates the financial logic: a major hit is essentially a long-term bond. It produces a predictable cash flow, is legally protected, and matures over a timeframe comparable to insurance liabilities. Wall Street noticed.

**Michael Jackson and the Beatles Catalog**

In 1981, Michael Jackson and Paul McCartney were socializing at McCartney's estate outside London, discussing a potential songwriting collaboration. McCartney warned Jackson about the importance of owning publishing rights — he and Lennon had lost significant control of their early catalog and McCartney dreamed of buying it back. He showed Jackson his own modest collection of purchased catalogs, including Buddy Holly's songs and some Broadway titles. Jackson laughed that he should buy all of McCartney's music someday. McCartney thought it was a joke.

It was not a joke. Jackson had roughly $9 million in cash and a growing interest in song acquisition. He bought Sly and the Family Stone's entire catalog for half a million dollars, recouping the investment quickly as covers of those songs generated licensing fees. When an Australian media tycoon named Holmes à Court put the Beatles catalog up for sale in 1984, Jackson made his move — $40 million, which industry insiders considered absurdly high. A bidding war developed, with Richard Branson of Virgin Records joining the competition. In August 1985, Jackson won at $47.5 million — plus an appearance at a telethon fundraiser in Perth, Australia. As a condition of the deal, Holmes à Court was allowed to gift his daughter one Beatles song; hoping she'd choose the most valuable track, *Yesterday*, she instead picked *Penny Lane*.

McCartney was furious. Jackson promptly began licensing Beatles songs to advertisers — including *Revolution* to Nike — something McCartney said he would never do. Jackson later sold the rights to Sony, doubling his money. The episode confirmed that published song rights were systematically undervalued as assets.

**David Bowie Invents the Bowie Bond**

By the 1990s, David Bowie owned 50% of his catalog — unusually high for a rock star — but the other 50% was held by his manager, a man Bowie despised. Every time a Bowie song played on the radio, half the royalty went to someone he hated. He wanted to buy his rights back, but he needed cash to do it. His financial advisors devised a solution by adapting a financial instrument that would later become notorious in a very different context: the securitization pool.

In 1997, Bowie packaged 287 songs — from "Life on Mars" to "Ashes to Ashes" — into a special purpose vehicle and sold slices of the future royalty stream as securities. The "Bowie bonds" paid 7.9% interest and were purchased primarily by an insurance company looking for long-dated assets to match its long-dated liabilities. Bowie raised $55 million, used it to buy out his manager's stake, and retained ownership of his songs. The structure was elegant: unlike an outright sale, Bowie kept his catalog while monetizing its future income.

The bonds did not fare well for investors. They were issued at the peak of the CD era, with valuations premised on royalty revenues continuing indefinitely. Then came Napster, LimeWire, and the collapse of music industry revenues. The Bowie bonds were eventually downgraded to junk. But the transaction had introduced music publishing to a new class of institutional investor, and the broader concept took hold.

**Financialization, Superstars, and the Return to the Stage**

The logic of the Bowie bond scaled up. Companies like Hipgnosis (later acquired by Blackstone) spent over $2 billion buying tens of thousands of songs, assembling them into publicly traded funds. Blackstone, Carlyle, and Michigan's state pension fund are among the investors who have raised at least $4.4 billion packaging catalogs of Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, and John Lennon and Paul McCartney into securities.

Smith notes the irony of where this leaves the average working musician. A century after Victor Herbert sued Shanley's so that songwriters would finally get paid, the financial machinery that grew from that victory now mostly enriches institutional investors and a thin tier of superstars — the artists whose songs underpin billion-dollar securities. A side effect, he points out, is that it makes financial sense for these companies to pour money into promoting their asset-holdings, which makes the famous more famous and the rich richer. For most musicians, the real money is still what it always was: performing live. The beer halls, restaurants, and concert stages remain the last unfinancial frontier.
