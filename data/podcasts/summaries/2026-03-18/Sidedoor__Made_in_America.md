# Sidedoor — "Made in America"

**Host:** Lizzie Peabody (Sidedoor, a Smithsonian podcast produced with PRX). **Guests:** Rachel Slade (author, *Making It in America*); Christopher Payne (architectural and industrial photographer, 15 years documenting American factories); Susan Brown (Associate Curator and Acting Head of Textiles, Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum).

## TL;DR

- **Benjamin Franklin appeared at Versailles in "homespun" clothing in late 1776** — deliberately wearing a handmade colonial suit and beaver fur cap as theatrical marketing to win French military aid, which Slade calls "a hundred percent marketing."
- **Francis Cabot Lowell spent 5 years inside British textile factories** — memorizing power loom schematics entirely in his head after British officers searched his bags and shoes on the return voyage; he then founded the Boston Manufacturing Company, launching American industrial textile production.
- **The 1973 oil crisis was the first major domino in manufacturing's decline** — energy costs made domestic production uncompetitive, triggering a half-century exodus of factories overseas.
- **Manufacturing employment dropped from more than 30% of the U.S. workforce post-WWII to less than 10% today** — a contraction Slade dates to approximately 1972.
- **At the WWII-era peak, 1 in 3 American workers carried a union card** — labor unions drove double-digit wage increases and established healthcare, retirement, and paid leave as standard benefits.
- **The Berry Amendment required the military and Department of Defense to buy American whenever possible** — preserving aerospace as one of the last major domestic manufacturing sectors.
- **Microchip fabrication facilities ("fabs") cost a decade to build and house machines priced at upward of $250 million apiece** — operated in environments described as "something like a thousand times cleaner than an operating room."
- **Losing one manufacturing sector triggers a ripple collapse across related industries** — and Payne estimates reversing the trend would take "another several decades."

**Franklin's Homespun Diplomacy at Versailles**

By the winter of 1775, Britain had blockaded colonial trade, leaving revolutionary forces without adequate clothing or weapons. Slade notes that many casualties resulted not from combat but from cold and disease: soldiers "had no coats, they had no hats." With no domestic mass manufacturing capacity, the colonies depended on French intervention. In late 1776, Benjamin Franklin traveled to Paris — already famous in France for his scientific discoveries — and secured an audience with Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette. Rather than dressing to match the gilded court, Franklin appeared in a lumpy brown homespun suit and a beaver fur cap, a choice Slade describes as deliberate stagecraft: showing the French monarchy that Americans were "really bootstrapping" made the appeal for guns and clothing viscerally legible. The French king agreed to provide military support.

**Cabot Lowell's Industrial Espionage and the Birth of American Textiles**

After independence, the British closely guarded their manufacturing secrets — refusing to export equipment or allow outsiders into factories. Founding father Francis Cabot Lowell traveled to England in the early 19th century and presented himself as an ignorant American tourist, which Slade says "played really well into the British idea of Americans." The ruse earned him five years of unhindered access to British power loom facilities. He memorized all the schematics, writing nothing down. A British frigate intercepted his ship mid-Atlantic and officers searched his bags and shoes — and found nothing. Back in America, Lowell worked with immigrant engineers to draw up the plans and founded the Boston Manufacturing Company, a water-powered textile mill, sparking the spread of power looms across New England and the South. Thomas Jefferson, previously an anti-manufacturing Virginian, acknowledged the national security implications by 1812: "Manufactures are now as necessary to our independence as to our comfort."

**The "Made in America" Exhibition and Christopher Payne's Photography**

Cooper Hewitt's Susan Brown is curating an exhibition of Payne's factory photographs, also titled "Made in America." Payne has spent 15 years gaining access to factories — John Deere, Kohler, Whirlpool, Boeing, GE, and many others — producing images that are never improvisational. His stated goal is capturing what he calls the "a-ha moment": "So that's how they do it." Brown notes that viewers are consistently surprised: "Would I have imagined that a person laid the core inside my pencil by hand? Never in a million years." The General Pencil Company, in operation since 1860, is one example — a worker places each lead core by hand, and Brown observes that the worker "changes her manicure every week to match the pastel color that they're running." Payne's photo of Bartlett Yarns in Harmony, Maine — an 1821 mill still running vintage New England machinery — captures a "spinning mule" that traverses the full length of the building, drawing and twisting yarn: "your grandmother's attic, but also the old workshop."

**The Gilded Age: Industrial Growth and Its Human Cost**

Coal power electrified American factories in the 1880s, enabling the Industrial Revolution and mass migration from farms to cities. But Slade describes the Gilded Age as "horrific" for workers: injuries were legally treated as the employee's fault, and workers could even be charged for damage done to equipment — "it ate your hand," she says, and "you might even have to pay for the equipment." The industrial revolution transformed America into a global manufacturing giant, but the wealth was concentrated, not distributed.

**WWI, Infrastructure, and the Assembly Line**

World War I served as what Slade calls "phase two of American industrialization" — the need to supply food, clothing, ships, and munitions to Europe forced investment in roads, rails, and ports, which allowed factories to spread beyond major cities. Henry Ford's assembly line formalized the efficiency principle: a single worker builds one part rather than an entire product. Curator Brown notes that the resulting repetition — identical pencils, identical neon golf balls in metal molds — became a defining visual feature of industrial photography and of mass production itself.

**WWII Bonanza and the Labor Movement's Peak**

After World War II, with European and Asian manufacturing capacity destroyed, the United States dominated global production. Slade calls it "a bonanza for an entire generation." Unlike the Gilded Age, this prosperity was more broadly shared: labor unions had organized 1 in 3 American workers, and the labor movement drove wages up by double digits while making healthcare, retirement, and paid leave standard. This era represents the high-water mark of American manufacturing's social contract.

**The 1973 Oil Crisis and the Long Decline**

Slade dates the beginning of manufacturing's decline to approximately 1972. The immediate trigger was the 1973 oil crisis: the United States was already consuming more oil than it produced, and when Middle East producers cut off shipments, energy costs spiked. "Manufacturing is an intensely energy-expensive endeavor," Slade notes, and the price shock shuttered factories across the country. Companies did the math and found it cheaper to build overseas, where labor and energy costs were lower. The resulting "half-century march of factories leaving America" reduced manufacturing from more than 30% of the U.S. workforce to less than 10%.

**Modern American Factories: Aerospace, Peeps, and Microchips**

What remains of domestic manufacturing is anchored partly by the Berry Amendment, which requires the military and DoD to buy American — protecting companies like Boeing, which holds military aircraft and satellite contracts and manufactures commercial planes in facilities Payne describes as making visitors "feel like you've entered a land of giants." The fuselages are stored in bright green primer — a color invisible to passengers — before final paint is applied.

At the consumer end, Just Born Quality Confections has made Peeps in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, for 100 years, with the current marshmallow-chick process in place for about 70 years. Payne photographed the process: uniform dollops of white marshmallow emerge on a conveyor belt, pass over a bed of yellow-gold sugar, then — contrary to what Brown expected — the sugar comes from below. "They vibrate it to such an extent that the sugar sort of jumps up," she explains, coating the chicks from underneath.

At the high-tech end, microchip fabrication presents a different photographic challenge: the manufacturing process is invisible, proprietary, and conducted inside machines costing upward of $250 million apiece, housed in "fabs" that take a decade to build and are "something like a thousand times cleaner than an operating room." Payne found his entry point in the basement infrastructure: the wiring, piping, cables, water, and electrical systems needed to keep the machines running — "that wonderful balance of complexity and chaos and order."

**The Irreversibility of Deindustrialization and the Human Core**

Slade argues that when one manufacturing sector disappears, the loss cascades: removing apparel manufacturing, for instance, collapses the adjacent industries that supplied yarn, fabric, fasteners, and logistics. "When you remove one piece of an industry, everything else kind of crumbles." Payne echoes the point from a practical angle: "You can't just restart that. It takes decades." Despite widespread political consensus that manufacturing jobs should return, Slade and Payne describe the structural barriers as generational in scale. What both emphasize, however, is that humans remain at the center of every production process — including those run by sophisticated power looms where a single operator may spend up to a month loading the loom by hand. Payne concludes that factory floors, where "a machine doesn't care what side of the political spectrum you're on," represent one of the few remaining spaces where Americans from all backgrounds work side by side.
