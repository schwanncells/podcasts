# The Indicator from Planet Money — "How much is the Iran war costing us?"

**Hosts:** Ricky Mulvey, Darian Woods. **Guests:** Mark Gantzian (retired U.S. Marine colonel; former Department of Defense budgeting official; helped develop the initial $900 million/day cost estimate for Operation Epic Fury); Linda Bilmes (public finance professor, Harvard Kennedy School; studied costs of post-9/11 wars); Nita Crawford (co-founder, Costs of War Project at Brown University).

## TL;DR

- **Daily cost of Operation Epic Fury has already surpassed initial estimates** — CSIS first estimated ~$900 million/day; the New York Times reported Pentagon officials now put it at more than $1.9 billion/day, with Gantzian citing a richer munitions mix and higher base damage than projected.
- **Bombs dropped in wartime fall outside the Pentagon's existing budget** — the DoD treats munitions used in combat as off-budget, requiring the military to return to Congress for supplemental funding regardless of war duration.
- **The Bush administration estimated the Iraq war at ~$50 billion; Crawford's Costs of War Project put the real figure at ~$3 trillion** — a pattern Bilmes says dates back thousands of years, where war initiators consistently underestimate duration and expense.
- **Accrued disability benefits already promised to U.S. veterans total $7.3 trillion** — not including healthcare costs; Bilmes describes veterans' care as among the largest long-term financial obligations of any war.
- **The DoD's 2024 military emissions were 47 million metric tons CO2 equivalent** — larger than many countries' annual emissions; Crawford flags additional environmental damage from strikes on oil facilities in Iran and a Saudi oil refinery attack attributed to Iran.
- **All post-9/11 wars have been financed almost entirely through debt** — unlike prior conflicts, which used a mix of debt, tax increases, and budget cuts; Bilmes argues this shifts the burden to future generations and reduces public accountability.
- **Trump requested a $1.5 trillion military spending budget, more than 50% above current levels** — Bilmes warns the war's backdrop will help him secure a large permanent base increase, and that once added, Pentagon budget levels "stay there pretty much forever."

**The Gap Between Daily Cost Estimates and Reality**

Mark Gantzian, who helped develop the Center for Strategic and International Studies' initial estimate, notes that the $900 million/day figure has already proven too low. The munitions mix used in strikes on Iran has been richer than projected, and damage to U.S. bases in the Gulf has exceeded early assessments. The Pentagon's internal estimate, reported by the New York Times, now exceeds $1.9 billion per day. Gantzian does expect the daily run rate to fall now that the U.S. has established air superiority — cheaper bombs dropped from aircraft can replace the long-range precision-guided weapons used in the opening phase of the conflict.

A structural feature of Pentagon budgeting compounds the cost problem: munitions expended in combat are treated as off-budget. Once a bomb is dropped, the military classifies the expense outside its regular appropriation and must return to Congress for supplemental funding. This is true regardless of how long Operation Epic Fury lasts.

**How Post-9/11 Wars Consistently Exceeded Early Estimates**

Linda Bilmes places the Iran war within a pattern she traces across millennia: those who initiate wars tend to be optimistic about duration and dramatically underestimate costs. The Iraq war is the clearest modern example. The Bush administration's initial public estimate was approximately $50 billion. Nita Crawford's Costs of War Project at Brown University put the final figure for Iraq and Syria at roughly $3 trillion — roughly 60 times the original projection.

Crawford and Bilmes argue the visible daily costs — munitions, fuel, logistical operations — are the tip of an iceberg. The deeper expenses accumulate over decades after fighting ends.

**Veterans' Care as the Largest Long-Term Liability**

Both Bilmes and Crawford identify veterans' healthcare and disability benefits as the most consequential long-term cost of any armed conflict. Bilmes notes that the U.S. is still paying billions of dollars annually to care for veterans of the Gulf War, a relatively brief conflict. The total accrued disability benefits already promised to veterans across U.S. wars — excluding healthcare — stands at $7.3 trillion. Tens of thousands of service members across the Army, Air Force, Navy, and other branches are involved in the current Iran war, implying a long tail of future obligations.

**Environmental Costs of Wartime Operations**

Crawford raises a category of war cost that rarely appears in budget discussions: environmental damage. The Department of Defense's military emissions in 2024 reached 47 million metric tons of CO2 equivalent — greater than many nations' entire annual output — driven by large aircraft and ship movements and supersonic fighter jet operations. The Iran war adds to this baseline, and strikes on oil infrastructure compound it further. Iran has reportedly attacked a Saudi oil refinery (though the Iranian government denies responsibility), and U.S.-Israel joint strikes have targeted oil facilities inside Iran. Bombing oil infrastructure, Crawford argues, creates long-term environmental harm beyond what any emissions figure captures.

**How Wars Are Financed — and Why It Matters**

With limited exceptions dating to the Revolutionary War, the United States historically financed conflicts through a combination of debt, tax increases, and internal budget cuts. That approach ended after September 11, 2001. Every post-9/11 war has been financed almost entirely through borrowed money, with no corresponding tax increases or offsetting spending reductions. Interest on that debt becomes an additional long-term cost of the conflict.

Gantzian characterizes the choice between debt financing and tax financing as economically neutral, invoking the concept of Ricardian equivalence — the theory that raising taxes and running deficits have similar aggregate economic effects. Bilmes directly disagrees. She cites behavioral economics research showing that people experience the cost of paying taxes differently than the cost of incurring debt, arguing that tax-financed wars create public accountability that debt-financed wars do not. She points to President Truman, who delivered 206 speeches making the case that a war important enough to fight is important enough to pay for. Financing through debt, Bilmes argues, obscures the true cost, makes it easier to sustain prolonged conflicts, and pushes the financial burden onto future generations.

**The War's Effect on the Pentagon's Permanent Budget Baseline**

President Trump has asked Congress to approve a military spending budget of $1.5 trillion — more than 50% above current levels. While Congress has shown tepid enthusiasm, Bilmes argues that the backdrop of active combat operations and public discourse about munitions depletion and troop support will give the administration leverage to secure a substantial permanent increase. More significantly, she warns that once a higher spending level is incorporated into the Pentagon's baseline, it rarely retreats: "Once that gets added into the Pentagon's annual budget, it stays there pretty much forever so that every year you have a higher base that you start from." The Iran war, in her view, is not just a near-term expense — it is likely to ratchet up the structural floor of U.S. defense spending for years to come.
