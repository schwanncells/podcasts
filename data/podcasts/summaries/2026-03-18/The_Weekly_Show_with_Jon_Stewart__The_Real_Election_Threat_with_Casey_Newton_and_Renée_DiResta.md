# The Weekly Show with Jon Stewart — "The Real Election Threat with Casey Newton and Renée DiResta"

**Host:** Jon Stewart. **Guests:** Renée DiResta (associate research professor, Georgetown McCourt School of Public Policy; former Stanford Internet Observatory researcher; author of *Invisible Rulers: The People Who Turn Lies into Reality*); Casey Newton (editor, Platformer newsletter; co-host, *Hard Fork* podcast, New York Times).

## TL;DR

- **Non-citizen voting is statistically negligible** — studies from libertarian Cato Institute, right-wing Heritage Foundation, and the Brennan Center all conclude voter fraud is "infinitesimally small"; since 1982, approximately 1,500 documented cases in total across all elections.
- **The SAVE Act targets a near-nonexistent problem while ignoring algorithmic threats** — roughly 70% of Americans support it and ~50% believe undocumented voting is rampant, a perception driven by recurring "election rumor tropes" rather than evidence.
- **A 2020 Stanford research project (Election Integrity Partnership) tracked 22 million viral tweets** across the top 10 election rumors — Dominion machine-flipping, Italian space lasers, Sharpie markers in Arizona — and submitted ~3,000 URLs to platforms; platforms ignored 60% of flagged content, labeled ~30%, and removed only ~10%.
- **The SAVE Act's real disenfranchisement effect** — one study estimates up to 69 million women who took their spouse's name may lack a birth certificate matching their current legal name, disproportionately affecting groups likely to vote Democratic, including married women and trans voters.
- **Elon Musk rewired X's algorithm to boost his own tweets** — after his posts underperformed Joe Biden's during the Super Bowl, Musk directed engineers to re-engineer the algorithm for his own prominence; a separate study found users on the ranked algorithmic feed moved "further to the right" by "a very significant measure" compared to a chronological feed control group.
- **Jim Jordan's "Weaponization Committee" accused DiResta's group of censoring the 22 million tweets** — a figure DiResta's team published in March 2021 (after January 6) as a measure of rumor *spread*, not suppression; the agencies involved were Trump appointees, not Biden officials.
- **Murthy v. Missouri reached the Supreme Court** — Amy Coney Barrett's majority opinion found "clearly erroneous findings" by the lower court and dismissed for standing, noting there was "not a single email in which the White House so much as mentions" plaintiff Jay Bhattacharya (now CDC director).
- **Stephen Miller filed a lawsuit against DiResta** using a plaintiff in a specific Louisiana district to get a favorable judge; DiResta describes the strategy as "to tie you up in legal bills for three years and to shut you up."

**How Election Rumors Propagate: Tropes, Algorithms, and the Crowd**

DiResta describes election fraud narratives as "rumors" — a word she uses deliberately, because a rumor is "something where it sounds like it could be true" and spreads person-to-person through emotional resonance rather than evidence. The same tropes recur across election cycles: the bused-in voter, the person voting under a maiden name, the undocumented immigrant casting a ballot. These stories persist not because they are verified but because they activate a specific fear — that your vote is being stolen — which DiResta calls the reason "it lands so hard emotionally."

Newton describes the mechanics: an "aggrieved user" posts footage of something that looks suspicious (he cites the Georgia election worker pulling a container from under a table), adds an inflammatory caption, and the algorithm responds to early engagement by amplifying the post to far larger audiences. DiResta adds a third layer beyond algorithms and influencers: the crowd — "the online community that surrounds the influencer, that believes it, that amplifies it, and that moves it from platform to platform." People are the connective tissue between platforms, bypassing any single platform's moderation policy. Attempts to suppress a rumor often trigger the Streisand effect, amplifying the conspiracy by making audiences feel their access to truth is being blocked.

**Why Counter-Speech Fails: Asymmetry Between Officials and Influencers**

Election officials are poorly positioned to fight misinformation at scale. They have small followings, they are running elections, and they are "putting out facts" into a media environment where "facts do not land against an emotional story." By the time a corrections-oriented account with 200 followers responds to a viral claim, the rumor has already been boosted by a chain of influencers. DiResta notes that platforms were "actively trying to uprank good information" in 2020 and have since stopped; without that intentional surfacing of authoritative sources, accurate rebuttals simply do not reach the audiences who have already seen the original claim.

**Supply and Demand for Disinformation**

Stewart frames the economics: supply-side disinformation involves actors deliberately seeding plausible theories — not necessarily knowing they are false — to "cast doubt on the integrity of the election." Bot networks serve as amplifiers, artificially inflating engagement metrics to trigger algorithms to push content to real users. The actual content creators want legitimacy, so ideologically motivated accounts (Stewart invokes "the cat turds of the world") provide authentic-seeming voices, while automation handles the like and retweet volume needed to cross algorithmic thresholds.

Newton emphasizes the demand side: "In a moment in 2020, when Donald Trump had clearly lost, there was a huge demand on the right for that not to be true." Once that demand evaporated after the 2024 election, the disenfranchisement narrative largely disappeared. Newton argues this demand-side dynamic is at least as important as algorithmic mechanics — people "can just kind of go out and pick their own reality based on what they want."

Foreign actors exist in this ecosystem but DiResta notes their influence was "not particularly major or significant in 2020 or in 2024, far less than what we saw in 2016." The dominant source of delegitimizing election content in 2020 came from domestic actors, including the sitting president.

**The Election Integrity Partnership and Jim Jordan's Weaponization Committee**

DiResta describes the Election Integrity Partnership (EIP) — a four-center research collaboration including Stanford, involving approximately 120 undergraduate and graduate analysts — that operated during the 2020 election using a JIRA-style ticketing system to track viral rumors in real time and route them to platforms or election officials for response. The project sent tip-line invitations to the RNC, DNC, NAACP, AARP, and other civil society groups. When rumors flagged to platforms were not actioned, the team documented the outcome: platforms ignored 60% of 3,000 submitted URLs, applied labels to about 30%, and removed approximately 10%.

After the election, DiResta's team published a report that included a data pull showing the top 10 most viral election rumors — Dominion vote-flipping, "Italian space laser" theories, and the Sharpie ballot claims in Arizona — had collectively generated 22 million tweets. That number, published publicly in March 2021, was subsequently cited by Jim Jordan's House Judiciary Subcommittee on the Weaponization of the Federal Government as evidence that EIP had *censored* 22 million tweets. DiResta characterizes the accusation as a deliberate inversion: the figure measured the scale of viral misinformation the team was studying and flagging, not content they suppressed. The agencies EIP communicated with were run by Trump appointees, not Biden officials.

Casey Newton says the committee's goal was not principled free-speech advocacy but "creating a spectacle and manufacturing this sense of grievance that will then enable Republicans to take further steps to disenfranchise American voters." The practical outcome: Stanford's Internet Observatory was effectively shut down, and lawsuits were filed against undergraduate analysts who studied the 2020 election.

**Murthy v. Missouri and the Limits of the "Censorship Regime" Theory**

The case originated with Missouri and Louisiana attorneys general — both DiResta identifies as election deniers, one of whom (Eric Schmitt) became a U.S. senator — who filed suit alleging a "Biden censorship regime." Because the relevant agencies were run by Trump appointees in 2020, the complaint shifted to allege a "deep state" operating inside the administration. Evidence centered on emails from White House Digital Director Rob Flaherty using "strong language" with platforms, but DiResta notes that many of the most prominent Flaherty emails involved questions about the White House's *own* Instagram account.

The case was initially decided by a district judge in Louisiana who called it "the biggest censorship effort the world has ever seen" and issued a sweeping injunction. The conservative Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals partially walked that back. At the Supreme Court, Amy Coney Barrett wrote that lower courts had made "clearly erroneous findings" and dismissed the case for lack of standing, noting that not a single White House communication mentioned plaintiff Jay Bhattacharya — now NIH and CDC leadership — by name.

Stewart notes the ongoing irony: the same administration now uses similar government-to-platform communication channels, including complaining about moderation of ICE-related content, and Trump threatened Mark Zuckerberg with jail if platforms acted against him.

**Elon Musk, Algorithmic Capture, and the Free Speech Contradiction**

Newton reports that Platformer broke the story of Musk directing engineers to re-engineer X's algorithm to boost his own posts' prominence after they underperformed Biden's during the Super Bowl. The result: X users are exposed to Musk's views regardless of whether they follow him. A separate study found that users on X's ranked algorithmic feed moved significantly further to the right compared to a chronological feed control group.

DiResta argues Musk "borrows the moral weight of the word censorship while emptying it of moral content." She and Newton were on Kara Swisher's podcast together when Musk argued that restricting his AI model's ability to generate non-consensual nude images of children constituted censorship. Simultaneously, Musk complied with Turkish government demands to remove opposition content. Newton catalogs the contradictions: Musk banned journalists for listing an Instagram bio in their Twitter profile, rewrote algorithms to privilege his own speech, and banned users who published the location of his private jet.

DiResta draws a legal distinction: on private platforms, the First Amendment right belongs to the platform, not the user. This is why conservative "must-carry" law challenges have consistently failed. Platforms can amplify, suppress, or restructure content — all of it constitutes their protected editorial discretion.

**Algorithmic Speech vs. Free Speech**

Stewart introduces a distinction between free speech and "algorithmic speech," describing the latter as "ultra processed" — "Twitter speech is free speech in the way that Doritos are food." The algorithm does not neutrally reflect what users say; it selects for content that maximizes engagement through conflict, outrage, and emotional provocation, then monetizes that attention. DiResta elaborates: the phrase "freedom of speech, not freedom of reach" — which she and Asa Raskin developed in 2018 and which now appears in X's content moderation policy — was originally intended as an argument *for* content that stays up while limiting algorithmic amplification of the most harmful material. Musk has since stripped that principle of its original meaning.

**Tech Oligarchs, Citizens United, and Transactional Politics**

Stewart tallies post-2024 returns: Musk donated $250-plus million and gained an estimated $234 billion in net worth; Bezos paid $40 million for a Melania documentary plus advertising commitments and gained approximately $15 billion; Zuckerberg, whose $400 million "Zuckerbucks" election administration spending became a right-wing grievance target, asked Trump directly how much he should publicly pledge.

Newton argues the underlying ideology is capitalism, not conservatism: "These guys give a lot of money to democratic presidents and democratic causes. And in the end, they just don't get that much for it." Biden attempted to break up Meta, Amazon, and created regulatory friction for Musk. Trump, by contrast, is transactional in ways tech billionaires find familiar — "you can just agree to build part of his ballroom and he gives you whatever you want." DiResta frames this as a question of infrastructure: Twitter/X is "very, very good at activation" and represents "an incredibly potent infrastructure" for reach and persuasion, which is why controlling it has political value.

**What Might Actually Help: Slow Media, Regulation, and Institutional Courage**

Newton proposes a "slow food movement for the media" rather than trying to make algorithmic feeds safer from the inside. He identifies podcasts and newsletters as pillars of a richer, more contextual information environment. DiResta is less optimistic about reforming platforms from outside, noting a decade of failed attempts. Both agree that the current regulatory vacuum is anomalous: prior to roughly 2020, social media was not thought of in the same category as regulated goods like alcohol or tobacco; now multiple countries are banning minors from social media entirely. Casey speculates that banning children — as several countries have done — might, as banning minors from smoking did, eventually shift adult behavior.

DiResta flags content labeling (community notes), phone grayscale settings from the Center for Humane Technology, and Surgeon General-style warning labels as partial measures. On institutional response, she argues against crisis-communications silence — "making yourself a tasteless pablum so inoffensive that it serves no one" — in favor of active engagement. She notes that after Stanford's Internet Observatory was effectively shut down in response to political pressure, she wrote explicitly that "you have to fight." The precedent of fighting the PACT Act's detractors ("you would think, who's going to be against that") on social media terms, rather than attempting to suppress misinformation, is offered as a model.

Newton closes: "When you see something online that makes you feel a very strong emotion, that is the moment that I'm trying to be the most skeptical."
