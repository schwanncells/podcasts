# Steph Infection: The Podcast — "Butter Butt with MOLLY KEARNEY"

**Host:** Stephanie Lynn Tolev. **Guest:** Molly Kearney (comedian, actor, SNL cast member, DMV actor).

- **Molly Kearney recently completed top surgery and is navigating post-op recovery, including managing two drainage blood bags (emptied twice daily) and tracking the return of sensation** — the surgery removed an estimated 2.8 pounds and 2.5 pounds, and she attended O'Mary's final show with blood bags in her back pockets.

**Top Surgery, Recovery, and Body Autonomy**

Molly underwent top surgery approximately one year prior (around March 2025) at NYU's specialist surgery program. The procedure involved removing breast tissue, relocating and resizing the nipples to masculine proportions (using a cookie-cutter approach, positioning them according to the male clavicle line), and managing post-operative fluid buildup with two drainage tubes connected to blood bags. The bags, sewn in place below small sutures, required emptying twice daily—morning and night—by Molly's mother, Nurse Margaret, who managed the process meticulously enough that Molly was able to have the drains removed early (typically they stay in 10–14 days).

Molly described the drain removal as painless: the doctor simply removed two stitches and pulled out the tubing in one smooth motion at each site, then applied a band-aid. A year post-op, the horizontal scars below the chest have mostly faded due to silicone treatment. Sensation is returning unevenly—some areas are recovering, others tingling intermittently; she's not worried and remains happy with the results. She noted that running on a treadmill post-op felt revelatory ("I am going to run marathons"). The surgery also improved her posture, since she no longer needs to compensate for large breast weight and can focus on back strengthening.

**SNL Anxiety and Competitive Desperation**

Molly was cast on *Saturday Night Live* and served two years there, despite never receiving a live audition—a fact that still rankles her. At SNL, she wrote sketches (more as outlines than full scripts), pitched ideas, performed in the live show, and tried to attend open mics at the Comedy Cellar between rehearsals, though exhaustion often prevented this. The hours were brutal: late-night rewrites, studio sessions until 6 a.m., celebrity encounters that blended together, and the constant undercurrent of insecurity ("am I good enough?").

After SNL, she went on the road, but touring creates its own anxiety—specifically, the pressure to sell tickets. She's developed a bold spot from stress and has reached a breaking point where she no longer wants to promote herself or perform because the promotional burden burns her out. She has lost TikTok followers by publicly snapping at people who claim she never comes to their city, even though she does tour those areas; they simply don't check the tour schedule. She envisions her ideal touring schedule as one week per month in a specific region (e.g., West Coast), allowing her to stay rooted and write from lived experience rather than airport and hotel jokes.

**Competing for Roles and the Weight of Typecasting**

Molly and Stephanie discovered they've auditioned for the same roles multiple times, competing for parts that fit their shared "type"—masculine energy, deep voice, sketch-improv-stand-up background—making them distinct from other actresses like Lisa Gilroy. Molly booked *DMV* (the lead, Barb), a CBS workplace comedy about the Department of Motor Vehicles, after having a pre-audition meeting with Netflix executives who specifically said they had a role they thought she'd be perfect for. She filmed 20 episodes in Montreal from July to December, experiencing every season and discovering that Montreal's French-speaking crew (while skilled) could be dismissive of English speakers. The show allows some improvisation, though the plot necessitates sticking to logistics in Barb's manager role.

Molly's earlier acting experience included partial roles on shows like *Twisted Metal* (the motorized Cardinal Tetra-sized role she didn't get). She's committed to acting as her primary goal and will tour once monthly to supplement income, but television is her obsession.

**Hemorrhoids, Infections, and Bodily Indignities**

Molly has had a hemorrhoid (which she names "Glenn") since age 30; she's now 41. Recently, she experienced a rash so severe around her buttocks during a Puerto Rico vacation with 30 cousins that she felt the itch throughout her entire body and couldn't walk. With her aunt (a doctor) and mother (an ER nurse) present, they went to a random pharmacy in San Juan and bought over-the-counter cream that stung but worked. Molly now takes eight gummies of Metamucil daily (increased from six after a week without them caused her hemorrhoid to return); she describes the fiber regimen as essential to maintaining comfort.

Regarding her hemorrhoid specifically, it's external and deflated—not currently bleeding—so Molly is dismissive of it. However, Stephanie, who has hemorrhoids herself, offered extensive expertise: tucks pads work well, witch hazel and coconut oil are helpful, and the real secret is adequate fiber intake. Stephanie mentioned that many people don't realize diarrhea often causes hemorrhoids, not just straining from constipation.

**Infection Management and Cuticle Anxiety**

Molly has a habit of compulsively picking at the cuticles around her fingernails. Recently, she developed a cuticle infection so severe that her finger became red and painful. While in (she believes) Seattle, she sterilized a safety pin, cleaned the area, and lanced the infected cuticle, releasing thick yellow pus. She then soaked the finger in hot salt water from packets she retrieved from a café downstairs. The wound healed without Neosporin or medical intervention, though the experience did reduce her picking habit somewhat—she now picks less frequently, though not completely.

**Body Modifications and Self-Confidence**

Molly noted that she avoided looking at her chest before surgery, finding the sight distressing. Now she catches her reflection and thinks "fuck yeah," occasionally tearing up at how right her body feels. She attributes this to finally aligning her physical form with her internal sense of self. She also mentioned her girlfriend sometimes photographs her post-surgery results to send to her mother and aunt (her mom being an ER nurse), and one occasion her aunt remarked that a post-surgical bump "kind of looks like a clitoris," leading Molly to decide she'd just buy cream at CVS instead.

Stephanie mentioned she's undergoing CoolSculpting (fat freezing) to increase confidence in her stomach and has completed one round with another to come. She looked in the mirror recently and nearly cried, then sent her sister a photo saying "I like my stomach"—something she's never said before. The two agreed that body modification for personal confidence is valid, and neither should be judged for choosing what makes them feel good in their own skin.

**Family Medical Expertise and Irish Catholic Closeness**

Molly comes from an Irish Catholic family where medical expertise runs deep: her aunt is a primary care doctor (now retired) who treated the entire family; her uncle was the family dentist; and her parents informally served as therapists. Growing up, family members freely consulted the aunt about health issues, creating a tight-knit medical ecosystem. When Molly needed SSRIs for depression, the family arranged it without formal insurance. This level of medical intimacy among family members is unusual but was normalized in her upbringing.

**Sleep Issues and Apnea Screening**

Molly is snoring and experiencing episodes where she stops breathing, suggesting possible sleep apnea. She wore a finger pulse oximeter for three nights to begin screening and anticipates a sleep study in a specialized lab with full monitoring equipment. She's also sleeping heavily and has difficulty with the anxiety of wearing monitoring devices (she joked that she "needs her mommy" to fall asleep while wearing them). During a recent Puerto Rico trip, she shared a bed with her mother, who uses a white noise machine. Molly apparently thrashed, moved her arms and neck like an antenna, and made noise so disruptive that her mother couldn't sleep despite cranking the noise machine to maximum. The next morning, Molly's mother was recounting the snoring incident to the extended family like a stand-up routine, describing how she had to manually adjust Molly's limbs to silence her.

**Comedy, Touring, and Professional Despair**

Both Molly and Stephanie commiserate about the unsustainability of touring and the constant anxiety of selling tickets, managing followers, and promoting themselves online. Stephanie emphasized that having appeared on SNL should be credential enough—it shouldn't be necessary to also maintain a viral social media presence. Yet the industry demands both. Stephanie has reached such a breaking point with promoting herself that she's considering not performing at all. Molly echoes this sentiment, finding that being on the road prevents her from writing new material because she's exhausted and has no lived experiences beyond airports and hotels.
