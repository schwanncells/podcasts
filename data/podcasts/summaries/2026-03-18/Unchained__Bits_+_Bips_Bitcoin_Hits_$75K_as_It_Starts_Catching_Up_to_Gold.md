# Unchained — "Bits + Bips: Bitcoin Hits $75K as It Starts Catching Up to Gold"

**Host:** Austin Campbell (Zero Knowledge Group). **Guests:** Ram Alawalia (Lumina), Chris Perkins (CoinFund).

**Prediction Markets: Legal Framework and Valuation Bubble**

Chris Perkins attended FIA Boca, the annual derivatives conference, where crypto participation surged significantly. Prediction markets like Kalshi and Polymarket were described as "the pretty girls in the room" attracting institutional interest alongside perpetuals and 24/7 markets. Perkins notes that the CFTC released guidance stating insider trading is illegal and will be prosecuted—a message directed at firms attempting to profit from non-public information.

The regulatory picture is fractured. Federal judges have issued conflicting rulings on whether Kalshi's contracts fall under state gaming regulation or federal CFTC jurisdiction. This split creates the fastest path to Supreme Court resolution, since it's untenable for a product to be "federally legal in New York but federally illegal in Arizona for the exact same conduct." Austin and Chris both flag this as a battleground issue: gaming definitions will determine which products are permissible.

However, Ram Alawalia is bearish on enterprise value creation in prediction markets. DraftKings is down 30% in three months; Genius Sports is down 50% with a 60x price-to-earnings multiple. Private prediction market companies are even more expensive than public comps. Meanwhile, NASDAQ is entering the space, and traditional gambling companies are defending their turf. Ram forecasts that prediction market companies will face intensive competition and may end up destroying shareholder value.

**Iran Conflict: Strategic Objectives and Duration**

The conflict entered week three with Israel conducting limited ground operations in southern Lebanon. Dubai International Airport, the world's busiest, suspended flights after a drone strike on fuel depots—the third such incident since the war began. Missiles are still striking from Iran but at 90% lower frequency than earlier; initial targets have shifted to logistics and supply chains.

Austin and Chris debate the true strategic objectives. Chris emphasizes tactical U.S. dominance—excellent military performance, targeting degradation of Iranian logistics. Austin argues Trump's core objective is controlling Iran's uranium, not just immediate military success, based on Trump's State of the Union statements about Iranian nuclear ambitions. Ram counters that oil control is equally critical for maintaining U.S. dollar hegemony against China and preventing Chinese access to oil supplies.

The hosts question how long the conflict will last. Austin's reading of Trump's public comments suggests it will last longer than anticipated. Oil remains elevated—gasoline in California has hit $8.50 per gallon, and extended high oil prices create inflationary pressure that hurts both consumer spending and stock valuations. The Federal Reserve faces a dilemma: rate cuts cannot solve a supply shock, yet letting inflation run risks overheating the economy. The hosts note that job market strength has been resilient, but if that begins to crater, the Fed calculus shifts.

**Deleveraging, Market Volatility, and Dealer Pinning**

The markets have experienced four down days in the S&P with a lackluster relief rally today. Ram describes consistent VWAP (volume-weighted average price) selling through last week—the systematic, orderly deleveraging characteristic of forced position exits. Hedge funds are deleveraging across private credit, quant strategies, and fundamental long-short books. Only the hyperscalers—Meta, Amazon, Oracle—are re-leveraging, issuing "boatloads of debt" to purchase GPU compute and data centers from NVIDIA, which expects $1 trillion in revenue through end of 2027.

The backdrop of deleveraging raises the question: are funds voluntarily de-risking or being forced out of positions after whipsaw losses on oil trades? Rumors suggest several major hedge funds are down billions on oil trades. After a 90% decline in Iranian missile strikes, markets are exhibiting dealer pinning effects ahead of options expiration. Valuations remain extended—industrials like Caterpillar and John Deere trade at 30-35x earnings. Ram advocates for waiting for a real capitulation event rather than buying into extended valuations. The S&P is only five points from all-time highs despite the accumulated geopolitical and inflationary shocks, which Ram finds remarkable and worrying.

**Bitcoin's Rally: STRC Flows and the Gold Catch-Up Trade**

Bitcoin is at $74K, hitting key resistance levels. Austin and Ram debate the driver. Austin believes Micro Strategies' STRC issuance is sustaining the bid—record volumes last Friday, strong marketing attracting new flows. When that marketing process slows, the Bitcoin bid will slow; Austin would take chips off the table at current levels.

Ram and Austin both point to a shift in the Bitcoin-to-gold ratio. After sovereigns bought gold (which spiked upward) while Bitcoin stalled, the narrative broke down. The "digital gold" thesis appeared challenged. Now, as markets settle and the BTC-to-gold ratio bottoms and begins recovering, the narrative may be healing—Bitcoin catching up to the gold move. Chris notes that fundamentals around institutional crypto adoption are improving: Ethereum as a tech company paying yield ("ultrasound money"), and institutional recognition that crypto is both infrastructure and a store of value.

However, Ram flags a countervailing force: non-U.S. perspectives. If geopolitical shocks (Iran, Russia, China) accelerate, international investors should be rotating into Bitcoin as a non-system-dependent store of value. Tether supply expansion would be a signal to watch. That said, U.S. dollar strength and flight-to-safety toward U.S. assets could suppress digital asset demand. The outcome hinges on facts on the ground in Iran and Trump's stated intentions.

**Ethereum Foundation and the RWA Trilemma**

The Ethereum Foundation published a 38-page document outlining its principles under the PROPS framework: permissionless, censorship-resistant, open source, private, and secure. The foundation describes itself as Ethereum's "first steward" and claims its explicit goal is to become unnecessary over time as the network matures.

Austin objects to this framing as internally contradictory. He argues there exists a "trilemma" in blockchain design: you can have decentralization and permissionlessness, smart contracts, or real-world assets (RWAs), but not all three simultaneously. His concern centers on composability risk. If a major stablecoin like USDP (with $2 billion in reserves but $20 billion minted after a smart contract hack) is embedded across Aave, Uniswap, and Morpho, then the smart contract compromise cascades into liquidations across all protocols. Without network-level controls—the ability to freeze or roll back—there is no responsible party who can fix the damage.

Chris counters that Ethereum's core differentiator is precisely that decentralization and censorship resistance; the burden should lie on RWA issuers to implement robust security and on protocols to design smart contracts defensibly. Chris argues that organizations choosing to enable chain rollbacks (like some permissioned chains) can do so commercially, but Ethereum's validator set is too diverse (approaching a million validators) to coordinate such rollbacks without sacrificing decentralization.

Austin proposes alternative models for RWAs: public permissioned chains (open access but with more entry/exit controls), like Avalanche subnets, Stellar with tiered validators, Canto, and Tempo. He also floats the concept of a "global DTCC"—a chain run by the 1,000 largest financial institutions globally, which would be surprisingly decentralized given their geographic and philosophical diversity. Chris acknowledges these models but emphasizes Ethereum's strength as a fully decentralized settlement layer and warns that institutional adoption of Ethereum-based RWAs is hindered by the lack of a clear counterparty to negotiate with, unlike with alternatives.

Austin concludes with a prediction: the chain that ultimately hosts the majority of RWAs has probably not been created yet, or if it exists today, it will look vastly different in the future—comparable to how we now view 1990s tech investments.
