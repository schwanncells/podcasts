# Unchained — "Bits + Bips: Bitcoin Hits $75K as It Starts Catching Up to Gold"

**Hosts:** Austin Campbell (Zero Knowledge Group); Ram Ahlawalia (Lumida Wealth); Chris Perkins (CoinFund). **Guests:** None — co-host panel only.

## TL;DR

- **Bitcoin hit ~$74–75K amid a BTC-to-gold catch-up trade** — gold's earlier sovereign buying bid was not matched by Bitcoin, but the ratio has now bottomed and is recovering, with ETH outperforming amid broader crypto re-bids.
- **Iran conflict enters week three with no Strait of Hormuz resolution** — Dubai International Airport suspended flights after a drone struck a fuel depot; two LPG tankers transited the strait; Iranian missile strikes are down ~90% but still ongoing; oil elevated and feeding inflation risk.
- **Trump's stated objective is uranium control, not just oil** — hosts interpret his comments as implying he won't stop until Iran's nuclear program is under verifiable oversight; the Ayatollah's son is missing or possibly dead, leaving no clear negotiating counterpart.
- **Macro deleveraging is broad and orderly** — hedge fund gross leverage hit peak levels ~one month ago and is unwinding; quant factor models are broken; private credit is deleveraging; only hyperscalers (Meta, Amazon, Oracle) are re-leveraging to buy GPU compute for data centers.
- **Strategy's STRC issuance is the main engine driving Bitcoin bids** — Ahlawalia warns that when STRC marketing slows, the bid for Bitcoin will slow; he is more bearish than neutral at current levels; no real capitulation in equities has occurred yet.
- **Ethereum Foundation published a 38-page PROPS framework** — Campbell argues the EF is "talking out of both sides of its mouth" by championing self-sovereignty while Ethereum hosts billions in RWAs (like Tether) that create cascading systemic risk if smart contract keys are compromised; Perkins counters that EF doesn't control Ethereum and its censorship-resistant moat is the chain's core differentiator.
- **Prediction markets face regulatory crossfire** — federal courts are split on whether Kalshi's products are CFTC contracts or state-regulated gaming; the conflict will likely fast-track to the Supreme Court; Perkins warns prediction markets are "running out of friends in D.C." as both Democrats and many Republicans side with states.
- **Austin's closing prediction:** The chain that most real-world assets eventually settle on "has probably not been created yet" — current chains will look vastly different by the time the RWA market matures, akin to how 1990s tech companies relate to today's internet.

**FIA Boca: Derivatives, Perpetuals, and 24/7 Markets**

Perkins attended FIA Boca, the annual Futures Industry Association conference, reporting unusually heavy crypto participation this year from Kraken, Polymarket, Kalshi, and others. The conference conversation centered on three themes: prediction markets, perpetuals, and 24/7 market structure. Perkins noted that Hyperliquid is already doing ~30% of volume in non-crypto products, illustrating how crypto-native platforms are extending into traditional markets. He flagged that 24/7 trading capability will become "table stakes" rapidly and that legacy exchanges without it risk obsolescence.

On prediction markets, he sees near-term political volatility — Democrats oppose them broadly, many Republicans will defer to state regulators — making the regulatory path uncertain even as Polymarket and Kalshi are "the prettiest girls at the dance." Campbell added that a 50-state framework would be "catastrophic" and a federal CFTC umbrella is the only workable structure. Campbell also noted that multiple federal judges have issued conflicting rulings on whether Kalshi's contracts are CFTC-regulated or state gaming, which creates the conditions to fast-track Supreme Court resolution.

**Prediction Markets, Insider Trading, and Valuation Concerns**

All three hosts agree that insider trading is illegal regardless of venue — Perkins recounted being pitched by a hedge fund whose stated edge was "friends in D.C.," which he immediately declined. Ahlawalia noted that insider trading is happening visibly: energy stocks ramped before the Venezuela raid and the Iran conflict, and apps tracking congressional trades are proliferating. The hosts treated the legal status as clear even if enforcement is lagging.

Ahlawalia offered a bearish take on prediction market valuations: DraftKings is down 30% over three months, Genius Sports down 50%, and public gaming companies are trading at stretched multiples (Genius at ~60x earnings). Private prediction market companies are priced even more expensively while facing new competition from major exchanges like NASDAQ. He characterized the sector as "a bubble" already on its right shoulder.

**Iran Conflict: Tactical Dominance, Strategic Uncertainty**

Perkins reported strong U.S. tactical performance — Admiral Cooper briefings showed the military moving past live munition sites into logistics and supply chain targets. The 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit (MAGTF, ~1,200 Marines) departed Okinawa and is steaming toward the region; Perkins interpreted Senator Lindsey Graham's Semper Fi tweet as telegraphing that the MEU may be tasked with securing Kharg Island oil assets, which control 90% of Iran's oil exports.

Campbell parsed Trump's public statements as the clearest signal on conflict duration: Trump said he invited NATO help but also said "we don't need it" while noting the UK offered two aircraft carriers only after the U.S. was already succeeding. Campbell's read on Trump's objective is control of Iran's uranium, not just oil — Trump stated Iran would have used nuclear weapons on Israel and neighboring countries including Saudi Arabia if it had them. The missing or dead Khomeini son creates a power vacuum with no clear negotiating partner, paralleling the difficulty of predicting who emerges from complex power collapses (Campbell invoked the French Revolution: "Lafayette versus the left, and Robespierre hadn't even emerged yet" — with Napoleon being the unknowable outcome).

Perkins and Ahlawalia agreed that the geopolitical backdrop is ultimately about China: controlling petrodollar pricing and oil chokepoints limits China's options, and combined with Venezuela and domestic U.S. production, the strategy isolates Russia as the remaining variable. Perkins sees Russia as a short-term winner (higher oil, distracted world attention) but a medium-term loser as focus pivots back once Iran stabilizes.

**Macro Markets: Deleveraging, Inflation Risk, and the Fed**

Ahlawalia described broad, orderly deleveraging across asset classes — hedge fund gross leverage hit peak levels about a month ago and is unwinding across quant, fundamental long/short, and multi-strat funds. Good names are being sold simply because funds are reducing risk, not on fundamentals. The only entities re-leveraging are hyperscalers (Meta, Amazon, Oracle) issuing "boatloads of debt" to buy GPU compute; NVIDIA expects $1 trillion in revenue through end of 2027.

On equities, Ahlawalia argued that industrials are a bubble already on its "right shoulder" — Caterpillar and John Deere at 30–35x P/E, GE Vernova and Teradyne similarly stretched. He recommended getting smaller in risk exposures and waiting for real capitulation, which has not yet occurred. The S&P being only ~5 points off all-time highs despite the geopolitical trauma was noted as remarkable but not a green light.

Elevated oil is the central macro risk: it feeds inflation, pressures consumer spending, makes Fed rate cuts harder to justify, and hurts retailers as an input cost. Campbell noted that gasoline in California has hit $8.50, though he partly attributed that to California's energy policy rather than the Iran conflict alone. On what a hypothetical Waller Fed would do differently, Campbell argued the Fed should sit on its hands in a supply shock — cutting rates doesn't produce more oil, and the 1970s proved you risk compounding inflation. Perkins suggested Waller would take a more coordinated approach with Treasury Secretary Bessent versus Powell's current isolated stance.

**Bitcoin and Crypto: Catch-Up Trade and STRC Engine**

Perkins reported that trading desks are focused on a "short gold, long Bitcoin" trade. Gold rallied sharply on sovereign buying earlier in the conflict, and Bitcoin did not follow — but the BTC-to-gold ratio appears to have bottomed and is recovering. ETH is outperforming across the board. Perkins framed this as the "digital gold narrative never actually broke down" — it was just delayed.

Ahlawalia is more bearish, arguing that Bitcoin's current bid is primarily driven by Strategy's (formerly MicroStrategy) STRC preferred stock issuance attracting new flows. STRC had record volumes the prior Friday. He expects the Bitcoin bid to slow when STRC marketing slows and recommends taking chips off the table at current levels (~$74K at time of recording). Campbell offered a counterargument from a non-U.S. perspective: geopolitical shocks in Iran/Russia/China should accelerate Bitcoin adoption among people trying to move wealth outside their domestic systems, and expanding Tether supply would be a confirming signal to watch.

**Ethereum Foundation's PROPS Framework: Principled or Self-Contradictory?**

The EF published a 38-page document defining its role as Ethereum's "first steward" and introducing the PROPS framework (censorship resistance, open source, privacy, security), with the explicit long-term goal of making the foundation unnecessary. Campbell framed his objection as a trilemma: a blockchain cannot simultaneously be fully permissionless/self-sovereign, host real-world assets with external controllers, and support complex interlinked smart contracts without creating systemic risk. He used a hypothetical Tether smart contract hack — if North Korean actors minted a trillion dollars of USDP, Tether could deprecate the contract and issue a new one, but every Uniswap pool and Aave collateral position using that token would be destroyed in the process. He called this "the 2008 interlinked counterparty credit problem" for DeFi.

Perkins pushed back: the EF doesn't control Ethereum and can't force EIPs through — decentralization is the chain's core moat and differentiator. He compared the EF's role to standards bodies for the internet: no single entity controls the internet, and that's the point. He argued that robust app-layer controls (smart contract security, freeze/seize mechanisms on RWA issuers) are the proper locus of protection, not network-level rollback. He also noted that the Iranian foreign minister was appearing on Zoom "using the internet that no one could shut down" as a live demonstration of censorship resistance's value.

Campbell clarified he doesn't object to permissionless chains in principle — he supports Bitcoin and privacy coins — but that the EF is "talking out of both sides of its mouth" by endorsing self-sovereignty and PROPS without addressing the layered fragility that RWAs and composable DeFi introduce. He cited the Bybit hack as an example of ETH not interdicting stolen funds, and the Cetus hack on Sui as a counterexample where a validator patch was pushed to recover assets. His preferred RWA platforms: Avalanche subnets, Stellar's tier-one validator set, Canton, and Tempo — chains designed with harder permission boundaries. He also proposed a "global DTCC" model where the thousand largest global financial institutions co-run a chain, arguing that their geographic and philosophical diversity would produce meaningful decentralization.

Perkins acknowledged that institutions struggle to engage with Ethereum because there's no SLA counterparty — "who do I talk to?" — which validates the RWA-chain differentiation argument. Both agreed the space is analogous to the 1990s internet era, where the dominant infrastructure wasn't obvious until much later.
