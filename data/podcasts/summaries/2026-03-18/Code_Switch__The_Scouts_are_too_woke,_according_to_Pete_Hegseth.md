# Code Switch — "The Scouts are too woke, according to Pete Hegseth"

**Hosts:** Gene Demby and B.A. Parker. **Guest:** Benjamin Rene Jordan (historian of American gender, environmental, and cultural history; author of *Modern Manhood and the Boy Scouts of America: Citizenship, Race and the Environment, 1910–1930*; currently researching scouting's role in the early civil rights movement and environmental activism).

## TL;DR

- **Pete Hegseth declared Scouting America "gravely wounded" by DEI** — he posted the video less than one day before the U.S. started a war with Iran, and gave the organization six months to comply with his demands or lose Department of Defense support.
- **Hegseth's demands included**: removing the citizenship-in-society merit badge (created in 2020 after George Floyd's murder), adding a military service merit badge, banning transgender members, requiring all scouts to use their sex assigned at birth, and waiving membership fees for military families.
- **Historian Jordan calls Hegseth's historical narrative "fake news"** — in the 1910s–30s, the Boy Scouts were more racially and religiously inclusive than most American national institutions, actively recruiting African-American, Jewish, Catholic, and immigrant youth when those were explicitly minority-hostile eras.
- **Scouting was enormous in its heyday** — over 5 million Americans participated between 1910 and 1930, and the Scout Handbook was the highest-selling English-language book of all time after the Bible.
- **The BSA deliberately chose a civilian identity over a military one** — it legally sued the more militaristic "American Boy Scouts" to force a name change, and they rebranded as the American Junior Cadets; that militaristic rival organization faded into obscurity while the civilian, community-service model became the dominant version of scouting.
- **The BSA publicly clashed with the KKK in the 1920s** — when the Klan threatened to pull funding and members unless Catholics, Jews, and Black Americans were excluded, the BSA told them to leave rather than comply.
- **Membership declines predate DEI policies** — Jordan attributes the drop from ~10 million (1970) to ~1 million to urbanization, the 1960s youth counterculture reacting against establishment institutions, and the BSA's own turn toward greater exclusivity in the 1980s under the Reagan era — not diversity policies.
- **The grand irony of Hegseth's demands**: by requiring a military service merit badge, he ensured that transgender, gay, and female members of Scouting America will now earn a badge honoring military service — the opposite of his intent.

**Hegseth's Ultimatum and What He Demanded**

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth posted a video on his personal social media accounts criticizing Scouting America, calling the organization "gravely wounded" by DEI, charging that it had embraced "the destructive myth of gender fluidity and transgenderism," and accusing it of pushing an "insidious, radical, woke ideology that is anti-America and anti-American." He cited a membership drop from 10 million in 1970 to roughly 1 million today and blamed it on DEI. The Department of Defense has had a close relationship with the scouts nearly since the organization's founding, with many troops meeting on military bases. Hegseth leveraged that relationship, giving Scouting America six months to comply with the Trump administration's anti-DEI executive orders or lose DoD support. His specific demands: remove the citizenship-in-society merit badge established in 2020 following George Floyd's murder, add a new military service badge, ban transgender membership, require all members to use their sex as assigned at birth, and waive fees for scouts from military families.

**Why Jordan Says the History Doesn't Support Hegseth**

Jordan, who wrote a book on Boy Scouting's first two decades, says Hegseth's historical framing is "historically inaccurate" — "fake news, if you want to use modern-day parlance." Jordan argues that the early Boy Scouts of America were, by the standards of the 1910s–30s, explicitly DEI-oriented. They actively recruited African-American, Jewish, Catholic, immigrant, and Asian-American youth at a time when the Ku Klux Klan was a mainstream political force, Jim Crow was the law of the land, and ethnic and religious discrimination was widespread in American institutions. The BSA's national leadership pushed local troop leaders and regional offices to be inclusive even when some resisted, framing that inclusivity as a form of patriotism. Jordan notes that the word "nonpartisan," which the BSA used to describe itself in those decades, explicitly meant rejecting the KKK's exclusionary vision of American national identity. Girl Scouting, by contrast, lagged on racial and religious inclusivity until the 1940s or 50s, remaining predominantly white and WASP for its first few decades — a reversal of what most people today assume about the two organizations.

**The BSA and the KKK: A Public Fight Over Membership**

Jordan describes direct public confrontations between the Boy Scouts of America and the Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s. The Klan threatened to withdraw funding and pull members from BSA troops if the organization didn't exclude Catholics, Jews, and Black Americans. The BSA's response, according to Jordan, was to tell them to take their money and their boys and stay out — that the Klan's vision of nationalism was not the BSA's vision. Jordan frames this as evidence that the organization was "quite literally having public debates with the Ku Klux Klan about membership and DEI in the 1920s."

**Why Scouting Became So Large — and Why It Declined**

Jordan explains that scouting emerged during a period of rapid modernization in American society — the 1910s brought cars, movies, electric lights, radio, and mass urbanization. The concept of adolescence as a distinct life stage barely existed before 1900; scouting helped fill the perceived gap in experiential and civic education for young people in a changing world. Scouting troops were often affiliated with churches but also engaged youth directly with their communities through what Jordan describes as early forms of community activism, civil rights work, and environmental conservation. At its peak, the Scout Handbook sold more copies than any English-language book except the Bible.

The membership declines that started in the 1960s, Jordan argues, had nothing to do with DEI. By that era, American society was already thoroughly urbanized, reducing the original need scouting had filled. The 1960s youth counterculture viewed scouting as part of the establishment, and young people were turning to other forms of political and social engagement. When the BSA tried to reverse the decline in the 1970s by leaning into more urban and diverse programming, it didn't work. In the 1980s, aligned with the Reagan-era political climate, the BSA swung toward greater exclusivity and a narrower, more restrictive membership — Jordan says this version was "a different beast" from the organization's first 70 years, less welcoming than the scouts had been in their actual heyday. The BSA sued all the way to the Supreme Court in 2000 to keep gay troop leaders out, arguing that forced inclusion violated their First Amendment rights.

**The BSA's Explicit Civilian Identity — Not Military**

Jordan explains that when scouting arrived in America from Britain (where founder Sir Robert Baden-Powell had given it a somewhat more military character), it competed with more militaristic American versions, including a group called the American Boy Scouts that later renamed itself the American Junior Cadets. The BSA legally sued them for creating the impression that the BSA was a military organization, forcing the name change. The militaristic rival gave its members guns; early in the 1910s, a member shot and killed another boy. The BSA used the incident to draw a clear line: they were a civilian organization focused on character, citizenship, conservation, and community service — not military training. The civilian version became popular. The militaristic version faded. That's why, Jordan notes, there has never been a military service merit badge in scouting — introducing one would actually represent a departure from tradition, not a return to it.

**Scouting America's Response and the Irony of Hegseth's Win**

Jordan says that Scouting America refused absolutely to cave on allowing girls, gay members, and transgender members. The organization gave up the citizenship-in-society merit badge, a relatively minor concession. But it retained its inclusive membership. Jordan argues this means Hegseth effectively lost: Scouting America remains a "frontline DEI organization" — one that will now also offer a military service merit badge earnable by transgender, gay, and female scouts. As Jordan puts it, "what has been accomplished is making sure the girls and trans and gay people feel like they should have the opportunity to serve the military and serve the country." The conservative and Mormon troop-sponsoring organizations that opposed inclusive membership had, for the most part, already left the BSA years earlier — meaning the remaining membership is already composed of people for whom inclusivity is not a deal-breaker.
