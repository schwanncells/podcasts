**Pete Hegseth's Attack on Scouting America**

In late March 2026, Pete Hegseth, Secretary of Defense, posted a video to his social media accounts criticizing Scouting America, the rebranded Boy Scouts of America. He claimed the organization had become "gravely wounded" after 2012, blaming "diversity, equity, and inclusion" for infiltrating the group, the name change to Scouting America, acceptance of girls, and a watered-down focus on God and patriotism. Hegseth characterized this as embracing "paganism," "gender fluidity and transgenderism," and an "insidious, radical, woke ideology that is anti-American." He attributed the organization's membership collapse—from roughly 10 million in 1970 to around 1 million today—directly to these changes.

Hegseth, who has built much of his public career opposing diversity initiatives (removing DEI offices from the military, banning identity months, rejecting climate change focus), gave Scouting America six months to comply with specific demands: eliminate the citizenship in society merit badge introduced in 2020 after George Floyd's murder, prohibit transgender members and require everyone to use their sex assigned at birth, establish a military service badge, waive membership fees for military families, and stop supporting DEI policies. He threatened that if the organization did not comply, the Defense Department would withdraw support, citing the close historical relationship between the military and scout troops, many of which meet on military bases.

**The Historical Record: Boy Scouts Were Pioneers in Racial and Religious Inclusion**

Historian Benjamin Rene Jordan rejects Hegseth's framing as "historically inaccurate" and "fake news." In reality, the early Boy Scouts were unusually progressive for their era. Between 1910 and 1930, the Boy Scouts of America was one of the largest voluntary organizations in American history, with more than 5 million members. The Scout Handbook was the best-selling English-language book after the Bible. Yet unlike other major institutions of that time—an era marked by spectacle lynchings, Jim Crow laws, and active KKK opposition to Catholics and immigrants—the Boy Scouts of America deliberately pursued inclusion.

Jordan notes that the organization actively recruited African-Americans, Jewish people, Catholics, and immigrants, seeing diversity as patriotic and essential to preparing young men for a modernizing, urbanizing society. While some local troops and sponsoring organizations did discriminate, the national organization pushed back, encouraging inclusion across the country. Perhaps most remarkably, the Boy Scouts had public debates with the Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s. When the KKK threatened to withdraw funding and pull out members if the scouts did not expel Catholics, Jews, and Black Americans, the organization rejected the demand. As Jordan relates it, the Boy Scouts told the Klan: "We are inclusive. If you don't like that, you can take your donation check that you threatened to withhold and you can take your members, your boys, and stay out because we aren't you. We aren't the Klan."

This stance was not peripheral to scouting's identity but central to it. The organization described itself as "nonpartisan"—a term that, in the context of the 1910s–1930s, specifically meant accepting African-Americans and religious minorities. Partisan, by contrast, would have meant aligning with the KKK.

**Why Scouting Boomed and Then Declined**

The Boy Scouts emerged at a moment when American society was rapidly modernizing and urbanizing. The advent of electricity, automobiles, movies, and radio created anxiety among adults that something essential—connections to nature, practical skills, moral character—was being lost. The concept of "adolescence" itself was newly invented; before 1900, youth went directly from childhood to young adulthood and work. Scouting filled a cultural need by combining outdoor education, character development, citizenship training, and community activism, often embedded within churches but distinct from schools and family.

By the 1960s, however, scouting's cultural function had changed. Youth counterculture questioned authority and tradition, and scouting began to look like part of the establishment. Membership declined, and in response, the scouts shifted toward greater inclusion in the 1970s, doubling down on urban membership and DEI-oriented policies. This did not reverse the decline. By the 1980s, under the Ronald Reagan era, the organization pivoted toward "traditional" values, but this iteration was actually *less* welcoming and accepting than it had been in the previous 70 years. The organization became narrower and more exclusionary, not broader.

**Military Influence: Less Than Assumed**

Jordan clarifies that the Boy Scouts were deliberately civilian, not military, despite their founder Robert Baden-Powell being a British military figure. Early scouting in America faced competition from more explicitly militaristic youth organizations, like the American Boy Scouts (which later changed their name to the American Junior Cadets to avoid confusion). The American Boy Scouts gave children guns and engaged in heavy military-style training. An accidental fatal shooting in the early 1910s—a youth killed during an argument over a snowball fight—prompted the Boy Scouts of America to establish themselves as an explicitly non-military organization focused on character, citizenship, and patriotism rather than military training.

When the United States entered World War I and World War II, the scouts supported the war effort on a civilian basis, collecting rubber, metal, tin, and aluminum, and selling war bonds. But they never had a military service badge, and they deliberately avoided the militaristic branding that characterized other youth organizations. This civilian identity was part of what made scouting appealing and allowed it to receive a federal charter in 1916—the second federal charter ever granted after the Red Cross and before the American Legion.

**The Girl Scout and Boy Scout Comparison**

Contrary to contemporary assumptions, the Girl Scouts were not historically more progressive than the Boy Scouts on race and religious inclusion. In the 1910s–1930s, the Boy Scouts were far more proactive in recruiting and welcoming African-Americans, Jewish people, and other marginalized groups. The Girl Scouts lagged behind by about 30 to 40 years, remaining predominantly white and WASP through the 1940s and 1950s. However, the Girl Scouts were earlier and more open in accepting transgender members, making an official announcement in 2011 that they had been accepting trans girls.

**Recent Changes and Membership Loss**

The Boy Scouts allowed girls to join and rebranded as Scouting America. The organization also became more explicitly welcoming of LGBTQ+ members. These changes did trigger departures; the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (the LDS church), which had sponsored many troops, left the organization, as did some conservative sponsoring organizations. Splinter scouting organizations emerged offering more traditionalist alternatives, though they remain small—roughly 30,000 members compared to Scouting America's million-plus.

Because conservative groups had already left, the membership rolls now largely consist of scouts and families comfortable with inclusive policies, making these changes less internally controversial than they might have been five or ten years earlier. Troop-by-troop variation remains; some conservative troops affiliated with churches or American Legion posts may interpret scout oaths and laws differently, but they operate within the broader inclusive framework.

**The Ironic Outcome of Hegseth's Pressure**

Scouting America refused to capitulate to Hegseth's demands. The organization's national office issued firm statements refusing to exclude girls, trans members, or gay leaders or scouts. In response, Hegseth forced the scouts to eliminate their diversity, equity, and inclusion merit badge, a clear concession. But the compromise backfired on Hegseth's goals. Scouting America was required to create a military service badge, and now girls, transgender scouts, and gay members will be eligible to earn it. As Jordan notes, this creates "delicious irony": Hegseth's pressure campaign has resulted in making girls and trans people feel welcome in military service, directly contradicting his stated goals of removing what he perceives as woke ideology from national defense.

In essence, as Jordan frames it, "Hegseth lost and Scouting America and DEI won." The organization maintained its core commitment to inclusion—it simply traded one merit badge for another. The broader historical picture, Jordan argues, reveals that Hegseth's vision of "traditional" scouting is itself a fiction; the real tradition of American scouting, from its inception, was progressive inclusion, not exclusion.
