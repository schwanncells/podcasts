# Planet Money — "The little pet fish that saved a town in the Amazon"

**Host:** Jeff Guo (Planet Money, NPR); co-reported with producer Luis Gallo. **Subjects interviewed:** Scott Dowd (conservation biologist, former New England Aquarium; founder of Project Piaba, a U.S.-Brazil nonprofit working to sustain the wild cardinal tetra trade); Valdores "Deco" Cicada (ornamental fisherman, Barcelos, age 54, fishing since age 14); Ara Mar "Mara" Castro (fish broker and self-described "piabera warrior," Barcelos); Vander Julesen Siketa (hotel manager, Hotel Amazonita, Barcelos; Deco's son, age 29).

## TL;DR

- **Barcelos, Brazil — "capital of ornamental fish" — once derived 80% of its economy from the aquarium fish trade**, primarily the Cardinal Tetra, a neon blue-and-red species caught wild in the Rio Negro's acidic, tannin-stained black water.
- **At peak, fishermen collected an estimated 20–40 million Cardinal Tetras per year** from the flooded Amazonian forests; Scott Dowd and colleagues concluded this was sustainable because millions die naturally each dry season as river levels drop.
- **In 2000, Florida fish breeders cracked the code to farm Cardinal Tetras in captivity**, ending Barcelos's monopoly; by the late 2000s, demand for wild-caught cardinals was plummeting, with industrial-scale farms in Singapore, Vietnam, and Malaysia now dominating the market.
- **The number of active piaberos (ornamental fish catchers) in Barcelos has fallen from hundreds to roughly 30**, according to Mara; a single fisherman like Deco earns a few cents per fish and can collect up to 10,000 on a good day.
- **Project Piaba's counter-strategy centers on traceability** — letting buyers trace exactly which piabero caught their fish, via videos and stories, modeled on fair-trade coffee labeling — plus a "finishing school" acclimation regimen and lobbying to cut Brazil's export red tape.
- **This mirrors Barcelos's earlier collapse**: the town was first a rubber boom hub in the early 1900s, which died when Southeast Asian rubber plantations (in what are now Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam) undercut Amazon prices — the same countries now farming Cardinal Tetras.
- **Sport fishing tourism has emerged as a new economic pillar**: approximately 10,000 tourists per year visit Barcelos to catch peacock bass on the Rio Negro; Hotel Amazonita has expanded, and many former piaberos now work as fishing guides, hotel staff, and drivers.
- **Tourism is framed as the industry that can't be outsourced**: rubber trees and cardinal tetra breeding can be replicated elsewhere, but "you can't take the Amazon out of the Amazon."

**The Cardinal Tetra and the Economy of Barcelos**

The Cardinal Tetra — a small, iridescent fish with a neon red stripe and electric blue coloring — is one of the most popular aquarium fish in the world. For decades, the only viable source was the flooded forests of the Rio Negro, a major tributary of the Amazon. The river's black, acidic water, stained by tannins leaching from jungle vegetation, created the specific conditions in which the species reproduced. Barcelos, an isolated town accessible only by river or air, became Brazil's designated "capital of ornamental fish." At its height, 80% of the town's economy depended on the trade. The annual Festival of the Ornamental Fish — a five-hour-plus spectacle held in a dedicated stadium called the Piabodromo — reflects that identity, with the Cardinal Tetra featured on statues, banners, and the sash of Miss Barcelos.

Fisherman Deco describes collecting the fish by canoe in the flooded forest, flicking the water surface with his fingers to attract them, then scooping them into tubs with a net. On a good day he fills tub after tub — up to 10,000 fish. In Brazil the fish sells for roughly two reais (about 40 cents); Deco earns a few cents per fish. He started at age 14 and, now 54, credits the Cardinal Tetra with allowing him to raise five children and put them all through school.

**How 40 Million Fish Per Year Turned Out to Be Sustainable**

When Scott Dowd first came to Barcelos in the 1990s, he was investigating whether the harvesting was ecologically damaging. His team estimated that at least 20 million Cardinal Tetras were being taken annually — a number he expected would alarm the conservation community. Factoring in undercounting, the true figure could have been 40 million. Dowd recalls being "prepared to be pelted with fruit and shoes" when he presented at a major fish conservation conference. Instead, his data convinced colleagues: the Rio Negro contains Cardinal Tetras in such abundance that 40 million is "just a drop in the bucket," and — critically — the dry season kills millions of them anyway as river levels drop. Dowd's conclusion was that harvesting the fish was not only sustainable but potentially beneficial, giving local people an economic incentive to protect the Amazon rather than clear it for cattle.

**Competition Ends the Monopoly**

In 2000, Dowd spotted an article in a fish magazine celebrating Florida breeders who had finally figured out how to reproduce Cardinal Tetras in captivity — a significant technical challenge given the fish's dependence on unusual water chemistry. He brought the news to Barcelos on his next visit. "There were gasps," he recalls. The implications were clear: Barcelos no longer had a monopoly. Demand for wild-caught cardinals eroded steadily through the late 2000s, as industrial fish farms in Singapore, Vietnam, and Malaysia scaled up production. Mara, who operates as a fish broker sorting and shipping fish from local piaberos to exporters, watched fellow catchers give up one by one. The active piabero community shrank from hundreds to around 30.

**The Rubber Boom Echo**

Deco drew the explicit historical parallel himself: his parents' generation worked in the Amazonian rubber industry. Barcelos was part of the early-20th-century rubber boom, when the Amazon was the world's primary source of natural latex. That industry collapsed when foreign operators transplanted rubber trees to Southeast Asia, establishing vast plantations in what are now Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam. The world stopped buying rubber from Barcelos; people couldn't pay their bills, towns emptied, and families like Deco's faced an uncertain future. The Cardinal Tetra essentially rescued the town in the 1950s after explorers identified it as uncommonly beautiful. Now, competition for the cardinal tetra has arrived from the same countries that destroyed the rubber trade. As Guo frames it, there is a recurring pattern: the world notices something unique and valuable in the Amazon — rubber, cardinal tetras, cacao — and eventually engineers a way to cut the Amazon out of the equation.

**Project Piaba's Three-Part Response**

Scott Dowd's nonprofit, Project Piaba, is pursuing three parallel strategies to help wild-caught cardinals compete. First, a traceability system that lets buyers — aquarium stores and hobbyists — identify exactly which piabero caught their fish and watch video of the Festival, giving the product a story analogous to fair-trade coffee's farmer photos. Second, a pre-export conditioning regimen — described by Guo as a "fancy pants finishing school for fish" — that acclimate wild-caught cardinals to the pH and conditions of home aquariums, addressing one of the wild fish's disadvantages relative to farmed ones. Third, Mara and Dowd have been lobbying Brazilian authorities to cut the weeks of export bureaucracy that slow the fish's journey to international markets. Dowd is candid about the odds: "I don't know. I can't stop trying, though."

**Sport Fishing and the Tourism Pivot**

The fuller picture of Barcelos's economy emerged through Deco himself. He mentioned, almost in passing, that he is no longer a full-time piabero — he also guides sport fishing trips on the Rio Negro. Many other former piaberos have made a similar shift, working as fishing guides, hotel staff, drivers, and hospitality workers catering to anglers. The target species is the peacock bass, a large trophy fish (roughly 36 times the size of a Cardinal Tetra) featured alongside the cardinal on the Jesus statue in the town square. Approximately 10,000 tourists visit Barcelos annually, most chasing peacock bass. Hotel Amazonita — where the Planet Money team stayed — has been actively expanding. Deco's son, Vander Julesen Siketa, 29, manages the hotel; he grew up alongside his father's piabero life but now thinks of it as "part of Barcelos' history."

Mara, who remains committed to ornamental fishing, told Guo that most former piaberos she speaks with say they would return to the trade "if the ornamental fish came back to what it was before." She describes ornamental fishing as more peaceful and autonomous than guiding tourists. She also expressed frustration that the town invests heavily in the annual festival — which has grown larger even as the number of active piaberos has shrunk — while doing less to directly support the fishers it ostensibly celebrates. Guo's interpretation is that the festival now serves a dual purpose: honoring cultural heritage and marketing the town's identity to attract tourists.

The episode concludes with the observation that tourism functions as a last-resort economic strategy precisely because it is geographically fixed. Rubber trees can be transplanted; Cardinal Tetras can be farmed in Vietnam; but the Rio Negro, the flooded forest, and the Amazon itself cannot be moved. "You can't take the Amazon out of the Amazon."
