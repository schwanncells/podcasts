# Bits + Bips — "Why Hedge Funds Are Deleveraging and Stocks Are Still Overvalued"

**Hosts:** Ram, Austin, and Chris.

## TL;DR

- **Hedge fund gross leverage peaked roughly one month prior and remains excessive** — quant funds, fundamental long/short, and private credit are all deleveraging, causing "good names" to be sold not on fundamentals but because managers must reduce risk.
- **S&P 500 only ~5 points from all-time highs despite broad market stress** — Ram calls this "remarkable given the trauma of news that's hit markets from multiple angles," but argues no real capitulation has occurred and value has not yet emerged.
- **Industrials called a bubble that has already popped** — Caterpillar and John Deere trade at 30–35x PE; GE Vernova and Teradyne also cited as extended; "we're on the right shoulder of the bubble."
- **Only re-leveraging actors are hyperscalers** — Meta, Amazon, and Oracle issuing large amounts of debt to buy NVIDIA GPU compute; NVIDIA projects $1 trillion in revenue between now and end of 2027.
- **Bitcoin at ~$74K driven by Strategy (STRC) issuance** — Ram turns more bearish, arguing the Bitcoin bid will slow when STRC marketing cools; STRC recorded record trading volumes the prior Friday.
- **BTC/gold ratio showing signs of bottoming** — Austin argues ETH is outperforming on fundamentals (infrastructure + tech + yield + "ultrasound money"), and that the digital gold narrative for Bitcoin "never broke down in the first place."
- **Fed constrained in a supply-shock scenario** — Chris argues cutting rates cannot produce more oil and risks repeating the 1970s inflation mistake; a hypothetical Fed Chair Worscht would take a more coordinated approach with Treasury Secretary Bessant than Powell's "on an island" stance.

---

**Broad-Based Deleveraging Across Asset Classes**

Ram describes what he observed in markets the prior week as consistent, orderly selling resembling "VWAP sell orders" — not panic, but methodical de-risking. Hedge fund gross leverage reached peak levels about a month prior and remains elevated; quant funds whose factors have stopped working, fundamental long/short funds, and private credit are all deleveraging. The effect is that "good names are being sold off because they're deleveraging — it's not about the name." Chris raises the question of how much of this selling is voluntary versus forced, noting rumors that "several of the major hedge funds are down like billions of dollars on some of these oil trades."

The sole re-leveraging exception Ram identifies is hyperscalers: Meta, Amazon, and Oracle are issuing "boatloads of debt" to purchase NVIDIA GPU compute for data centers. NVIDIA projects $1 trillion in revenue between now and end of 2027.

**Equity Valuations and the Case for Patience**

Ram argues that industrials represent a bubble that has already begun to deflate — "we're on the right shoulder of the bubble." Caterpillar and John Deere trade at 30–35x PE; GE Vernova and Teradyne are also extended. The group agrees that no true capitulation has yet occurred, meaning value has not meaningfully emerged. Ram notes a rising 10-year yield driven by inflation concerns hurts stocks and bonds simultaneously, eliminating the typical diversification benefit. He identifies homebuilders as a potential opportunity if mortgage rates decline, but his overall recommendation is to reduce risk exposures and wait for a "real capitulation" and "fat pitch."

**Bitcoin Driven by Strategy Flows**

Ram turns more bearish on Bitcoin, arguing that the current bid is primarily a function of Strategy's STRC preferred stock issuance attracting new flows — STRC recorded record trading volumes the prior Friday. His view: "when that marketing process starts to slow, then I believe the bid for Bitcoin will slow." He recommends taking chips off the table. Ram also notes that a flight to U.S. assets and dollar strength would work against digital assets broadly, pointing to DBC, gold, silver, and copper all showing weakness as an unusual concurrent signal.

**ETH Outperforming on Fundamentals and the Digital Gold Catch-Up Trade**

Austin takes a more constructive view on crypto, particularly Ethereum. He argues the BTC/gold ratio appears to have bottomed and is recovering, suggesting the digital gold narrative "never broke down in the first place" — gold's earlier surge was China-driven, and Bitcoin is now catching up as the BTC/gold ratio normalizes. On ETH, Austin describes the asset's combination of characteristics — infrastructure, tech company, yield-bearing, and "ultrasound money" — as a "delightfully weird" profile that institutional investors are beginning to understand. Austin and Chris note they are actively debating the Ethereum Foundation's evolving role.

**Non-US Capital Flight as a Bitcoin Tailwind**

Chris raises a perspective less visible through the U.S. markets lens: for individuals in countries facing geopolitical stress — particularly Iran, with potential spillover to Russia or China — Bitcoin represents a mechanism to move capital outside the domestic financial system. He argues Tether supply expansion alongside Bitcoin price would serve as a confirmatory signal of this dynamic activating. Ram agrees the thesis is worth monitoring but notes U.S. dollar strength would be a countervailing factor.

**Fed Policy Constrained by Supply-Shock Dynamics**

The hosts discuss what a hypothetical Fed Chair Kevin Worscht would do differently from Jerome Powell. Chris argues the Fed's hands are largely tied in a supply shock: cutting rates cannot produce more oil, and doing so risks "causing even more inflation" — the lesson of the Federal Reserve's 1970s policy errors. The only scenario that would shift that calculus is the labor market beginning to crater, which has not yet occurred. Ram suggests Worscht would take a "much more coordinated approach" with Treasury Secretary Bessant rather than acting in isolation, which the group agrees would likely be more beneficial for the economy than Powell's current stance.
