# Code Switch — "The Scouts are too woke, according to Pete Hegseth"

**Hosts:** Gene Demby and B.A. Parker. **Guest:** Benjamin René Jordan (historian of American gender, environmental, cultural, and social history; author of *Modern Manhood and the Boy Scouts of America: Citizenship, Race and the Environment, 1910 to 1930*; working on a second book on scouting's role in the early civil rights movement and environmental activism).

## TL;DR

- **Pete Hegseth called Scouting America "gravely wounded" by DEI** — his demands included banning transgender members, removing a citizenship merit badge created in 2020 after George Floyd's murder, adding a military service badge, and waiving fees for military families; Scouting America had six months to comply or lose DoD support.
- **Hegseth's historical framing is wrong, says Jordan** — the original Boy Scouts in the 1910s–1930s were explicitly pro-inclusion for African-Americans, Jewish, Catholic, and immigrant youth, and publicly refused Ku Klux Klan demands to expel those groups in the 1920s.
- **Scouting was enormous at its peak** — more than 5 million Americans participated between 1910 and 1930, and the Scout Handbook was reportedly the highest-selling English-language book of all time after the Bible.
- **The BSA was explicitly non-military by design** — a rival organization (the American Boy Scouts, later renamed American Junior Cadets after losing a lawsuit) embraced militarism; it never became popular, while the civilian-oriented BSA received a federal charter in 1916 — only the second organization to do so, after the Red Cross and before the American Legion.
- **Membership declines pre-date DEI shifts** — numbers fell from roughly 10 million in 1970 to around 1 million today; Jordan attributes the 1960s drop to counterculture anti-establishment sentiment, not inclusion policies; a 1980s pivot toward conservative "tradition" made the organization narrower than it had been in its first 70 years.
- **Conservative groups have already departed** — the Mormon Church and other traditionalist sponsors left after the BSA admitted gay and transgender members; splinter organizations now exist but number around 30,000 members, far below Scouting America's million-plus.
- **Jordan calls the outcome a defeat for Hegseth** — Scouting America refused to reverse its inclusion policies; the required military service badge will now be earned by the girls, trans, and gay members Hegseth sought to exclude — what Jordan calls "the grand irony."

**Pete Hegseth's Demands and Their Context**

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth posted a video to his social media accounts criticizing Scouting America less than one day before the U.S. began military action against Iran. In the video, he described the organization as "gravely wounded" after "diversity, equity, and inclusion crept in," and stated that it had allowed "the destructive myth of gender fluidity and transgenderism to infiltrate their membership" while "standards were lowered and merit destroyed in favor of an insidious, radical, woke ideology that is anti-America and anti-American." His specific demands: remove the citizenship in society merit badge established in 2020 following the murder of George Floyd; add a new merit badge for military service; waive membership fees for scouts in military families; and prohibit transgender members, requiring everyone to be registered according to their sex as assigned at birth. Scouting America was given six months to comply with the Trump administration's anti-DEI executive orders or face loss of Department of Defense support, including access to military bases where many troops meet.

**What Early Scouting Actually Looked Like**

Jordan describes Hegseth's framing as "historically inaccurate — fake news, if you want to use kind of a modern-day parlance." In its founding decades, the Boy Scouts of America was more proactively inclusive on race, religion, and ethnicity than most major American institutions of the era. Jordan says the organization recruited African-American, Jewish, Catholic, Asian-American, Mexican-American, and Italian-American youth and pushed regional leaders to welcome them, framing this inclusion as a form of patriotism. The term "nonpartisan," which BSA leadership used frequently, was explicitly defined in contrast to groups like the Ku Klux Klan: "Partisan would have been those, the Ku Klux Klan, who wanted to exclude or marginalize Catholics, Jews, immigrants, and African-Americans." When the Klan threatened to withdraw funding unless the BSA expelled those groups, BSA leaders refused publicly, telling the Klan to "take your donation check that you threatened to withhold and take your members, your boys, and stay out because we aren't you."

**Why Scouting Grew So Large**

Jordan situates scouting's rise in the social disruption of the early 1900s: rapid urbanization, industrialization, World War I, and the emergence of cars, movies, and radio. The concept of adolescence as a distinct life stage barely existed before 1900 — young people were generally treated as either children or near-adults by their early teens. Scouting filled a perceived gap by combining outdoor skills with community engagement, civil rights activism, and environmental conservation, operating as a complement to home, church, and school rather than a replacement. About half of all troops were affiliated with churches. The organization attracted supporters across the political spectrum, from the Rockefellers and the Roosevelts to Socialist labor leader Eugene Debs, who was actively courted by BSA leadership to sponsor union-affiliated troops.

**The Military Question: Why There Was Never a Military Badge**

When scouting spread from Britain to America, several competing versions emerged: a pacifist organization (the California Peace Scouts), and a militarist one (the American Boy Scouts, later the American Junior Cadets after losing a lawsuit to the BSA over the potential association of their name with a military identity). The military version gave its members guns; an incident in the early 1910s in which one scout shot and killed another during a dispute prompted the BSA to formally distance itself. Jordan notes that having a military service merit badge would be new: "They've never had one." The BSA did support the civilian war effort in both World War I and World War II — collecting rubber, metal, tin, and aluminum and selling war bonds — but remained explicitly civilian in structure and identity. The federal charter it received in 1916, Jordan explains, brought preferred access to national and state park and forest land, camping rights, and training from government rangers; the DoD relationship represented only about 10 percent of those government-linked benefits.

**Membership Declines and the 1980s Rightward Shift**

Hegseth attributes the drop in membership — from roughly 10 million in 1970 to around 1 million today — to DEI-related changes. Jordan offers a different account. Membership began falling in the 1960s when scouting came to be seen as part of the establishment, a liability as youth counterculture pushed against traditional authority. The BSA responded in the 1970s by doubling down on urban outreach and more inclusive policies; that didn't reverse the decline. In the 1980s, under the Reagan-era cultural shift, the organization swung toward a more conservative and exclusionary stance — one Jordan describes as "a different beast" and "more narrower, less welcoming, less accepting in the 80s than they had been the previous 70 years." As recently as 2000, the BSA sued all the way to the Supreme Court to preserve the right to exclude gay troop leaders, arguing that forced inclusion violated its First Amendment rights.

**Where Things Stand After Hegseth's Ultimatum**

Jordan assesses Scouting America's position as more stable than the controversy might suggest. Conservative sponsoring organizations — including the Mormon Church — that objected to the admission of girls, gay members, and transgender members have already left, taking their membership with them. Splinter conservative scouting organizations now exist but remain small, numbering around 30,000 members. What remains of Scouting America's membership is composed largely of those for whom inclusion is not a deal-breaker. The national organization issued a flat refusal to reverse its policies on girls, gay members, and transgender members. On Jordan's reading, Hegseth's intervention produced an ironic result: Scouting America dropped its DEI-specific merit badge and committee but retained all the underlying inclusion policies, and will now offer a mandatory military service badge that transgender, gay, and female scouts will earn — reinforcing, rather than reversing, the message that those groups can and should serve the country.
