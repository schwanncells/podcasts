# Bits + Bips — "The IRGC Is Weakened, but the Iranian People Still Won't Revolt, Says Major-General"

**Host:** unnamed (Bits + Bips host). **Guest:** Major-General "Spider" Marks (retired U.S. Army; intelligence background; brother-in-law is Iranian).

## TL;DR

- **93 million Iranians are unlikely to revolt despite regime weakness** — Marks says the population has absorbed "a bunch of body blows over the course of years" and is "very submissive," accepting the IRGC's presence as an oppressor without being prepared to sacrifice for an alternative.
- **The U.S. struck because it saw a window in civilian unrest** — Marks says the administration "saw the unrest over the course of the last six weeks, two months" and hoped to "accelerate this unrest" after decapitating the regime, but "hasn't happened yet."
- **The IRGC is "immensely weakened" but still functions as internal control** — it can no longer project power externally but remains the domestic enforcement mechanism, making revolution from within difficult.
- **Iran won't dissolve like Yugoslavia** — despite ethnic diversity (Kurds, Azerbaijanis), Marks argues Iranian societal differences "have either been reconciled or have been tolerated over the course of years," unlike the "millennia old hatred" in the Balkans.
- **A reported CIA operation to arm the Kurds is a sideshow** — Marks calls it "an economy of force" that won't "significantly alter the dynamics in Tehran," and is angered that the operation leaked: "routine leaks like that are un... something that needs to be addressed."
- **Israel is now the undisputed regional hegemon** — with Iran degraded, Israel becomes "the regional hegemon" that will "routinely be involved in what Iran 2.0 or whatever the heck it is looks like," with Saudi Arabia as a rising but secondary power.

---

**Why 93 Million Persians Won't Rise Up**

Marks draws on his Iranian brother-in-law as a source, describing a "very modern society" that has been ground down over 50 years of theocratic rule. The population is proud — "he says, we're very proud people" — but the accumulated repression has become "part of their DNA." The IRGC, while immensely weakened by the U.S. strikes, still exists as a domestic control mechanism. Marks sees no near-term uprising, though he allows that if fighting reaches a "more sustainable" level over time, "there could be an uprising."

**The U.S. Decision to Strike and Its Limits**

The administration seized what it perceived as an opportunity: civilian unrest combined with "incredible weapon systems" enabling an "unprecedented" maritime air campaign that decapitated the regime. But the hoped-for popular revolt hasn't materialized. The regime's new leadership now resembles "a hereditary monarchy" — precisely what the original Ayatollah campaigned against — with Khamenei's son taking power.

**Ethnic Fissures and the Kurdish Question**

Asked whether Iran could fracture like Yugoslavia, with Kurds and Azerbaijanis breaking away, Marks gives a flat "no." He distinguishes Iran's internal dynamics from the Balkans' "millennia old hatred," arguing Iran's ethnic tensions have been managed or tolerated. A reported CIA operation to arm Kurds migrating across the border would be, at most, a distraction that pulls regime resources — not a game-changer. Marks is visibly frustrated that the operation was leaked publicly.

**The New Regional Order**

With Iran degraded, Israel has emerged as the undisputed regional hegemon. Saudi Arabia is rising but secondary. Marks sees Israel as inevitably shaping whatever post-war Iranian governance emerges, maintaining ongoing involvement in "Iran 2.0."
