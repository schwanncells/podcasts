# Time To Say Goodbye — "Equator, a New Magazine for the World with Jonathan Shainin"

**Host:** Jay (formerly of The New Yorker, current journalist and podcaster). **Co-host:** Tyler Austin Harper (former academic, journalist). **Guest:** Jonathan Shainin (founding editor of Equator; former editor at The New Yorker and The Guardian, where he ran the Guardian Long Read section for 10 years; based in London; also worked in Abu Dhabi 2007–2010).

## TL;DR

- **Equator launched at equator.org** — a new English-language global magazine founded by a collective including novelist Pankaj Mishra, Pakistani novelist Mohsin Hamid, journalist Samanth Subramanian, Guardian columnist Nasreen Malik, and others; the site went live with 15–16 pieces on day one.
- **The Barbican/LRB cancellation was a catalyst** — the Barbican cancelled the entire London Review of Books winter lecture series after Pankaj Mishra's talk "The Shoah After Gaza" was listed, then "lied and said we never agreed to do this anyway."
- **Core editorial critique: Anglophone media is "stuck in the 90s"** — Shainin argues prestige commentators who covered the fall of communism absorbed a Fukuyama-ish teleology where all societies evolve toward America, a framework that still governs foreign coverage.
- **AI translation as editorial discovery tool** — Equator used DeepL to assess a 90,000-word Svetlana Alekseevich-style oral history by a Chinese factory poet, then commissioned a human translator to publish 4 chapters; Shainin calls this "really radical" for small publications.
- **Business model: Jewish Currents, not Substack** — the publication operates as a nonprofit with a free email tier, paid memberships, print subscriptions (3 issues planned for 2026), and upcoming online seminars including a David Goldblatt World Cup 2026 class at £150 for 4 sessions.
- **"The revenge of standpoint epistemology"** — Tyler's phrase for the media's over-reliance on diaspora voices (Iranian monarchists in America, Cuban exiles) to explain those countries, treating an ideologically specific expat view as a neutral one.
- **250 people attended a Zoom event** with Quincy Lebodjian and Melinda Cooper on the Epstein files, which Equator turned from an academic paper into a long read.

**The Founding Collective and Its Origins**

Equator grew out of friendships across Shainin's 25-year career in journalism. The founding group includes Indian British novelist and critic Pankaj Mishra; Pakistani novelist Mohsin Hamid; Indian journalist Samanth Subramanian (who has written for The New Yorker); Gavin Jacobson, a former New Statesman editor; Suzy Hansen, an American foreign correspondent who lived in Turkey for years; Negar Azimi, longtime editor of the Middle Eastern art magazine Bidoun; and Nasreen Malik, a Sudanese-Kenyan writer and Guardian columnist. Shainin describes it as a group that could "just do things" — a phrase he connects to the current vogue of voluntarist action, though he notes it also sounds uncomfortably close to American foreign policy right now.

The proximate catalyst was the Barbican cancelling the LRB winter lecture series after Mishra's lecture, titled "The Shoah After Gaza," was listed in the program. The Barbican, which Shainin describes as "a big concrete brutalist art center in London," cancelled the entire series and denied it had ever agreed to host it. The incident crystallized a sense among the founders that even well-regarded Anglophone institutions were unable to hold space for perspectives from outside a narrow consensus.

**The Critique of Anglophone Media's "End of History" Framework**

Shainin's core diagnosis is that prestige foreign coverage remains governed by a post-Cold War assumption he traces to journalists who covered Eastern Europe at the fall of communism. Those writers experienced Clinton-era American unipolar hegemony as "literally the end of history in a sort of teleological sense," and their worldview now dominates what the Atlantic, New York Times, and Economist consider legitimate frameworks. Behind this sits what he calls "American modernization theory": the post-war sociological paradigm that economic development produces liberalism and democracy.

Tyler connects this to a "pro-institutional mindset" rather than pure Fukuyamaism — a reflexive trust in expertise and established institutions that pervades prestige journalism. He argues this isn't a conspiracy but a sociological effect: the same kinds of people, from the same universities, staff these institutions and produce the same blind spots. "My wokest opinion about journalism is that diversity matters so much, but like especially class diversity, because it just feels like so many of the problems we have are just a function of that sociological bedrock." Shainin agrees and extends this to the editorial culture of places like The Guardian, where the pressure is to avoid being "the person who stands up and says isn't this a radical oversimplification" — a dynamic he calls "lowest common denominator" group behavior, anthropological rather than conspiratorial.

**The Expat Perspective Problem**

Tyler identifies a pattern across media coverage of Iran, Cuba, and Venezuela: the people called on to explain these countries to American audiences are invariably expats who left for specific political reasons. "Not that those perspectives aren't valuable, but often those people have left those countries for very specific reasons, often very specific sorts of political reasons." Treating that diaspora view as a neutral window onto a country, he argues, is "the revenge of standpoint epistemology." Shainin acknowledges this as partly structural — journalists talk to who is nearby — but says Equator has tried to work against it. An Iranian novelist, Salar Abdo, was in Tehran and then Bushehr during the protests, left just as the crackdown intensified, and filed a piece called "The War Within" from Dubai airport on his way home.

**What Equator Actually Publishes**

Rather than claiming to "represent the rest of the world" — a framing Shainin explicitly rejects — Equator aims to be curious about modern life in places that aren't the center of Anglophone discourse. A Chinese journalist of Mongolian origin, Soyonbo Borjijin, contributed "The Xi Jinping School of Journalism," a near-oral-history of working at a state-run newspaper in the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region. A piece by Indian novelist Siddhartha Deb traces his 1990s journey from India to Columbia University, knowing Edward Said taught there — "a story of enchantment and then disenchantment." Shainin notes he didn't expect the magazine to publish as much first-person polemical memoir as it has.

AI translation enabled one of Shainin's most prized commissions. A Chinese factory worker poet — whose name he mangles badly enough to become a running joke — had an untranslated 90,000-word oral history of the mass migration to Guangdong from rural China in the 1990s and 2000s, which her translator Eleanor Goodman described as "Svetlana Alekseevich style." Rather than paying for a sample translation or passing on the piece, Shainin poured it into DeepL, read it in rough translation, confirmed its value, and then worked with Goodman to publish 4 chapters. He is careful to say Equator wouldn't publish AI-translated work directly, but calls it "really radical" as a manuscript evaluation tool. "Once upon a time, you would have been really stuck."

**Business Model and Media Landscape**

Shainin looked at Substack but chose an independent website to preserve design identity and brand distinctiveness. He cites Jewish Currents — a small nonprofit magazine in the lineage of Dissent and n+1 — as the closest model: build a large free mailing list, convert a small percentage to paying members, take in donations and grants as a nonprofit. Equator is now building around the free-to-paid funnel through three mechanisms: a weekly newsletter with member-only bonus content, in-person and online events (a Zoom session with 250 attendees on the Epstein files), and upcoming online seminars. David Goldblatt — author of a sprawling multi-volume history of world football — will run a World Cup 2026 seminar for £150 for 4 classes. Molly Crabapple is doing something on the Jewish Bund in June. Evgeny Morozov is planned for a seminar on technologists in the global south.

Three print issues are planned for 2026. The publication has a registration wall — just an email address — and Shainin says he was "shocked" by how many readers simply leave rather than submit an email. The contrast with his Guardian years is stark: even a mediocre traffic day at The Guardian yielded 100,000 hits with no registration required.

On the broader media landscape, Shainin thinks the Times and Guardian are in some ways "bigger than ever" even as the overall media ecosystem fragments. The "share of brain" that used to belong to legacy media has dropped from something like 85% to 45%, he estimates. Cable news has effectively ceased to matter — Jesse Waters is "not the replacement for Tucker" — but what's replaced it is 22 million little silos rather than anything resembling a genuinely international conversation.

**The Long Game**

Shainin frames Equator's ambition in terms of institutional longevity rather than growth hacking. He points to the London Review of Books and New Left Review as examples of publications that simply decided to exist and, through accumulated commitment, became permanent fixtures with an "almost magic of permanence about them." His pitch to potential members is explicitly durational: "come be a part of this thing that maybe will sustain itself, and then like 20 years from now, it'll be like a place that you can go and read things." When he taught a semester at Columbia Journalism School, students still wanted to attach themselves to established institutions rather than build personal brands on Substack — suggesting the instinct toward institutional belonging hasn't died even as the institutions themselves thin out.
