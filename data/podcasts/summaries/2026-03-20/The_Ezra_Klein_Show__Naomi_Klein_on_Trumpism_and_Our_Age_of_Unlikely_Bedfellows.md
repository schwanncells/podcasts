# The Ezra Klein Show — "Naomi Klein on Trumpism and Our Age of 'Unlikely Bedfellows'"

**Host:** Ezra Klein. **Guest:** Naomi Klein (author; columnist, the Guardian; professor of geography, University of British Columbia; author of *No Logo*, *The Shock Doctrine*, and *Doppelganger: A Trip into the Mirror World*; forthcoming book with Astra Taylor, *End Times: Fascism and the Fight for the Living World*).

## TL;DR

- **The "mirror world" has taken over:** Klein's 2023 book tracked a parallel right-wing media ecosystem — replica platforms, Bannon's coalition-building, conspiracy culture — that most liberals ignored. She argues "that world runs our world now."
- **Naomi Wolf's trajectory shows how "ejection" works:** Wolf's public shaming on live BBC over a factual error in her 2019 book *Outrages* preceded her full plunge into right-wing conspiracy media; Klein argues that public humiliation frequently precedes migration to the mirror world, where "facts matter a lot less."
- **"Diagonalism" names the core coalition dynamic:** Scholars Quinn Slobodian and William Callison coined the term for alliances between wellness influencers, anti-lockdown liberals, and the hard right — pro-choice feminists with Steve Bannon, RFK Jr. with MAGA. Klein calls these the "unlikely bedfellows" of the episode title.
- **Trumpism as fascism driven by elite injury:** Klein argues that fascism is "a pathology of injured power" — elites (tech oligarchs, powerful men threatened by MeToo, the Epstein network) revolting against encroaching accountability, not powerless people rising up. Bannon is "a terrible fraud" who "performs being the voice of the little guy" while texting Epstein about Israeli political contacts.
- **COVID was a "fork in the road" between two futures:** Either a stronger activist state (eviction moratoriums, free tests, paid leave) or mass-death acceptance and culling logic. Klein argues Silicon Valley chose the latter partly because quiet quitting and labor demands during the pandemic threatened boss power more than COVID itself hurt them financially.
- **Tech oligarchs bankrolled Trump as a revolt against regulation:** Klein frames the 2024 Trump coalition as partly a tech-industry backlash against crypto and AI regulation, MeToo accountability, and the threat of employees having a say over what they build. Marc Andreessen describing routine crypto oversight as "basically a terror campaign" illustrates how any accountability reads as existential attack.
- **AI raises two unanswered questions:** What are humans for if AI replaces economically useful cognition? And what is AI itself for, beyond automating call centers? Klein says OpenAI "was a bait and switch" — sold as a Wikipedia-style public interest entity, now a for-profit company dumping money into super PACs against AI regulation.
- **The left's opening: eco-populism, neighborism, and talking to your neighbors:** Klein points to the Mamdani mayoral campaign (100,000 volunteers, ~10% of Trump voters) and the Minneapolis anti-ICE "neighbourism" movement as models — welcoming, non-jargon, rooted in place. "Free public transit" as a climate policy that also addresses cost of living is her example of where left politics could converge.

**Doppelgangers and the Mirror World**

Klein opens by defining her two central concepts. A doppelganger, literally "double walker" in German, is the uncanny experience of encountering someone who looks just like you but isn't you — a destabilization of the self. The "mirror world" describes the parallel right-wing media and social ecosystem: replica platforms, replica publishing, replica celebrity culture, all built on similar anxieties as the liberal left but warped into different conclusions. Klein was drawn to the concept partly because she found herself in the pandemic being confused on a mass scale with Naomi Wolf — left Twitter's running joke became her "branding crisis" and the entry point for a much larger inquiry.

She traces the mirror world back to her first book, *No Logo* (2000), which argued that multinational corporations had stopped making products and started selling brands, offshoring production while the "real work" became image-making. That same logic now governs individuals, she argues — everyone is told to be their own brand, but the critique of that is harder to make when everyone is implicated in it.

**Naomi Wolf: Ejection and the Journey to the Mirror World**

Wolf's arc from feminist bestseller (*The Beauty Myth*, Al Gore advisor) to prominent right-wing conspiracist illustrates a pattern Klein observes repeatedly: public shaming precedes migration. Wolf's 2019 book *Outrages* contained a foundational factual error — she misread the historical term "death recorded" to mean state executions of gay men, when it actually meant sentences commuted. She was corrected live on the BBC, generating mass online ridicule. Klein argues that "when we ask, what happened to that person, we'll often find some kind of public shaming" right before the conversion — not just cruelty, but something "maybe unforgivable" followed by embrace in a world with different epistemological rules.

Wolf became a major vector for COVID misinformation — an NPR-commissioned study traced the "vaccine shedding" myth (vaccinated people harming unvaccinated people) significantly back to Wolf, because of her association with women's health. Bannon would introduce her by emphasizing that she "used to consult for Al Gore, she consulted for Bill Clinton" — her value was precisely her ability to deliver constituencies Trump was weak with: suburban women, former Democratic voters.

**Diagonalism: How Unlikely Bedfellows Cohere**

The term "diagonalism," borrowed from scholars Quinn Slobodian and William Callison's work on the German anti-lockdown movement, describes alliances between liberal wellness culture and the hard right — not the left-right horseshoe, but yoga-studio types meeting Steve Bannon types over shared anti-institutional distrust. Klein sees this as more significant than the traditional horseshoe theory because the people involved weren't ideologically adjacent; they were united by bodily-integrity politics, distrust of expertise, and a sense of being "ejected" from respectable discourse.

RFK Jr. exemplifies the type: genuinely compelling on environmental degradation and alienation from the natural world ("he speaks really poetically about the natural world"), but now embedded in a movement that is, Klein argues, "nonstop grifting" — meme coin scams, supplement sales, seminars. Bannon understood that Wolf, RFK Jr., and "angry COVID moms" were the "plus one for MAGA," constituencies that could extend the coalition beyond its white union base.

**Fascism, Epstein, and the Revolt of Elites**

Klein is willing to call Trumpism fascist, and she locates its specific character in the concept that fascism is "a pathology of injured power" — it emerges among powerful people who feel threatened, not powerless people rising up. Italy and Germany emerged from the injuries of World War I to soldiers, generals, and industrialists. The current version, she argues, is elites revolting against: MeToo accountability, crypto and AI regulation, employee activism in tech companies, and the generalizing sense that their impunity was being chipped away.

The Epstein files illustrate this. Klein argues Epstein represented, for many wealthy people, the fantasy of consequence-free living — an object of envy, not just access. His "island" and wealth embodied "you don't really have to play by the rules." She connects this to how Bannon platforms conspiracy theories: "it is very useful for people to believe outlandish things, in part because it distracts them from the conspiracies that can be proved." Bannon is himself implicated in the Epstein network, texting Epstein about connecting a contact to Ehud Barak hours after sending him a link about Epstein's "alleged sex ring." Klein calls the Bannon world "really in crisis right now because of the Epstein files" and notes he appears to be flooding his show with other conspiracies to avoid the subject.

**COVID as Fork in the Road**

Klein frames COVID as a "fork in the road" that revealed two possible futures: an activist state (eviction moratoriums, paid leave, free testing, universal healthcare glimpses) versus what she calls the "culling" logic — acceptance of mass death as nature taking its course, survival of the fittest. She argues the diagonalist alliance was partly united by the culling logic: New Age wellness types claiming immune system supremacy and Bannon's world found common ground in comfort with mass death.

What frightened Silicon Valley most, she argues, wasn't COVID itself — which was financially good for tech — but what came with it: quiet quitting, workers demanding premium pay for essential risk, and a brief period where boss power over labor was genuinely weakened. She also ties COVID's aftermath to Gaza: both events, she argues, forced a choice about whether a society would harden its heart or expand its care, and the Trump moment is the product of a significant portion choosing to harden.

**Tech Oligarchs, Regulation, and AI**

Klein characterizes the 2024 Trump coalition partly as a tech industry revolt against regulation. Andreessen's description of standard crypto oversight as "basically a terror campaign" and AI regulation as an "existential" threat reveals, Klein argues, men who came up in the 1990s being treated as gods and now experiencing any accountability as terrorism. She connects this to Marc Andreessen's Time magazine cover at 23 on a "golden throne" — a culture that treated wealthy people "as if they were gods for a while" and is now dealing with the fury of gods who feel dethroned.

Klein says she once had conversations with AI lab leaders who told her that if their technology became as powerful as they believed, "at some point we'll have to be nationalized." She scoffed. "If you get to that point, there is no way you will allow yourself to be nationalized." That bet, she says, has proven true as OpenAI dumps money into super PACs against AI regulation — a company that sold itself as "basically Wikipedia, a public interest company."

She raises two fundamental questions AI forces: What are humans for, if economically useful cognition can be automated? And what is AI itself for, if not just replacing white collar workers? Neither question, she argues, has a public answer yet. She notes that the DOD is currently fighting Anthropic to use military AI "extremely unconstrained," and that the integration of Grok into war-fighting and Palantir's data work represent a surveillance-warfare merger the public doesn't yet understand.

**Data Centers and the New Anti-Tech Populism**

Klein identifies data center opposition as one of the most promising examples of diagonalism on the left — communities organizing across partisan lines because they know their water. Amazon's proposed "Project Blue" data center in Tucson generated cross-partisan organizing driven by desert water scarcity. She describes people characterizing it as a "spiritual war." She argues these battles are the public trying to have a democratic conversation about AI that Washington has decreed they have no role in — "rolled out with absolutely no public input."

**What the Left Should Do**

Klein's prescriptions are rooted in place, simplicity, and economic populism. She points to the Mamdani New York mayoral campaign — 100,000 volunteers, door-to-door contact, a video celebrating night-shift taxi drivers and LaGuardia workers — as a model of "neighborism": welcoming, non-jargon, rooted in the specific fabric of a community. She also points to Minneapolis anti-ICE organizing, describing it as "live action history lesson" connecting the city's history (birthplace of the American Indian Movement, site of the largest mass execution in U.S. history) to contemporary ICE raids.

She coins "eco-populism" to describe the climate politics she thinks is needed: free public transit as a climate policy that also addresses cost of living, access to nature as a spiritual right, language that starts with love of trees and oceans rather than carbon markets. She says the climate movement's mistake was leading with the "most bloodless" language — carbon trading — when "it makes sense to start with our connections to the natural world."

She is skeptical the Democratic Party will seize the moment and more hopeful about decentralized movements. She warns that Trump "doesn't seem to care about reelection," which removes the usual check on recklessness with his own coalition.

**Book Recommendations**

Klein recommends: *Empire of AI* by Karen Hao ("globe-trotting investigative reporting" combined with an empire-building thesis); *Here Where We Live* by Molly Crabapple (forthcoming April, on the Jewish Labor Bund and "committing to here"); and *Fire: Reading Walter Benjamin's 'On the Concept of History'* by Michael Löwy, engaging Benjamin's final text written while fleeing the Gestapo in 1940 — the idea that "history doesn't repeat, but compounds, piling wreckage upon wreckage."
