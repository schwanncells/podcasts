# Cautionary Tales with Tim Harford — "Presenting: Drug Story - On Xanax and Anxiety"

**Host:** Tim Harford (presenter). **Guest:** Thomas Getz (host of Drug Story; created, written, and hosted the episode with research director Molly Warner). Featured patient: Martha McPhee (author; took Xanax for 16–17 years; wrote essay on withdrawal for Vogue). Psychiatrist: Dr. Andrew Saxon (professor emeritus, Department of Psychiatry and Behavioral Science, University of Washington School of Medicine).

## TL;DR

- **Anxiety as disorder emerged officially in 1952** — The first Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM) codified anxiety as a treatable medical condition, though anxiety existed for millennia; ancient Stoics like Seneca distinguished state anxiety (acute) from trait anxiety (chronic disease).
- **Xanax approved 1981, became blockbuster via marketing to psychiatrists** — Upjohn promoted Xanax (alprazolam) as superior to Valium because it caused fewer lingering side effects and worked faster; within years it was the most prescribed benzodiazepine, perfectly timed to the "Decade of Anxiety" label applied to the 1980s.
- **FDA guidance: benzodiazepines for no more than 4 months; widely ignored** — Martha was prescribed Xanax in 2006 for panic attacks and took it nightly for 16–17 years, following doctors' recommendations despite official limits; rebound anxiety made withdrawal nearly impossible.
- **Xanax is euphorogenic (induces euphoria), driving abuse and recreational use** — Alprazolam enters the brain faster and causes euphoria more readily than other benzodiazepines; street names include "Zannies," "Z bars," "blue footballs," "school bus"; named-dropped in rap songs as often as Hennessy or Rolex.
- **Overdose deaths from benzos increased 10-fold in 15 years** — Benzodiazepine-involved overdose deaths rose from 1,320 in 2010 to 12,500 in 2021; over half involved prescription opioids; counterfeit Xanax laced with fentanyl added a silent killer (DEA campaign: "One pill can kill").
- **Withdrawal is medically dangerous and often causes worse anxiety** — Tapering requires weeks or months of gradual dose reduction; rebound and withdrawal symptoms overlap with anxiety disorder symptoms, trapping patients in long-term use; some have committed suicide unable to wean.
- **Current medical consensus: benzos not recommended for long-term anxiety** — American Psychiatric Association advises against them; CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy) and SSRIs are preferred; benzos should only be short-term relief (1–2 weeks), yet half of patients in 2018 took them for 2+ months.

**Anxiety as Emotion vs. Disorder**

Anxiety is an evolutionarily adaptive response—the fight-or-flight mechanism that helped humans detect danger. When you sense a bear in the woods, anxiety is protective. However, when anxiety persists over threats that are not immediately life-threatening (a subway ride, a work presentation), it becomes maladaptive. Like an overactive immune system, anxiety can turn on the sufferer. Dr. Saxon explains that in primitive human societies, the adrenaline surge was appropriate for immediate physical danger; in modern life, the same response to abstract threats prevents rational thinking and problem-solving. A panic attack, which can feel like a heart attack—racing heart, breathlessness, sweating—is not typically deadly, but the psychological conviction that one is dying makes it terrifying. Animals also experience anxiety disorders: dogs with separation anxiety hide under beds for hours after fireworks; lab rats removed from mothers develop PTSD-like behavior.

**Historical and Philosophical Roots**

Anxiety has been recognized for millennia. A four-thousand-year-old Babylonian tablet prescribed dates and mutton fat for those with frequent nervous breakdowns. The ancient Greeks and Romans, particularly Stoic philosophers like Seneca and Cicero, understood anxiety deeply. Seneca distinguished between state anxiety (a normal, acute response to danger) and trait anxiety (a chronic, day-to-day condition that constitutes sickness or disease). His practical advice—to combine "all times into one" and focus on the present moment—foreshadows modern mindfulness and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), a highly structured psychotherapy developed in the 1960s. CBT is proven effective for depression, anxiety, PTSD, insomnia, and eating disorders. The term "psychoneurosis" was used until 1952, when the American Psychiatric Association published the first DSM, formally recognizing anxiety as a diagnosable medical condition.

**The Rise of "The Decade of Anxiety" and Drug Treatment**

Post-World War II, anxiety among the American population, especially women, appeared to rise—or at least rose in medical awareness. Betty Friedan's 1963 *The Feminine Mystique* documented desperation among women trapped by social expectations and facing undiagnosed neuroses. The pharmaceutical industry responded with tranquilizers. Miltown (1955) offered almost instantaneous relief but proved too sedative and was reclassified. Barbiturates, invented in 1903 but reaching peak use in the 1960s, were produced at a rate of four billion tablets per year in the US alone. They worked exceptionally well but carried catastrophic risks: patients rapidly developed tolerance, requiring larger doses; dependency and addiction followed. As early as 1947, a journal article called barbiturates "a blessing and a menace." Overdose deaths from non-medical use became epidemic among students and housewives. Marilyn Monroe (1962), Judy Garland (1969), and Jimi Hendrix (1970) all died with barbiturates in their systems. The 1967 film *Valley of the Dolls* popularized the slang term "dolls" for barbiturate pills. By 1971, the Controlled Substances Act classified barbiturates as Schedule II drugs, the same category as fentanyl and morphine.

**Benzodiazepines and Valium's Dominance**

As barbiturate risks became undeniable, a new class of drugs emerged: benzodiazepines (benzos). Librium (1960) was the first, but Valium (1963), developed by Upjohn, became a cultural phenomenon. The Rolling Stones' 1966 hit "Mother's Little Helper" celebrated Valium's "little yellow pills." Valium was more potent and effective than Librium, worked remarkably fast (relief within 30 minutes to 1–2 hours), and seemed much safer. It became the first drug to reach $100 million in sales and the most prescribed drug of the 1970s; as many as 20% of American women reported taking Valium. However, Valium lingered in the body for days, causing confusion, dizziness, and drowsiness. It created dependency, and the risk became apparent. By the time new benzos arrived—Klonopin and Ativan/lorazepam—the market was primed for another solution.

**Xanax's Meteoric Rise and Marketing Genius**

Alprazolam, branded as Xanax, was approved in 1981. Upjohn faced a challenge: Valium was entrenched and culturally iconic. The company executed a novel marketing strategy. First, they narrowed the indication to clinical anxiety disorder, more precise than Valium's broad anxiolytic use. Second, they emphasized Xanax's faster action and fewer lingering side effects (less drowsiness). Third, they targeted psychiatrists directly, mailing informational materials weeks before launch and even offering reproductions of Impressionist masterpieces for office walls. The strategy worked spectacularly. Within years, Xanax became the most prescribed benzodiazepine. The American Journal of Psychiatry labeled the 1980s "The Decade of Anxiety"; by 1987, a dedicated journal, *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*, was founded. Xanax permeated pop culture: Tony Soprano's Xanax prescription on *The Sopranos* (1999) became so iconic that a prop pill bottle sold for $1,950 at auction in 2024 (double the estimate). In 2012, *New York Magazine* put Xanax on the cover, describing it as dissolving worries "like a special kiss from mommy." Paradoxically, the "Decade of Anxiety" label was applied to the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s as well—anxiety became perpetual.

**Xanax's Unique Appeal and Abuse Potential**

Xanax's mechanism relies on amplifying GABA, a neurotransmitter that lowers stress and creates relaxation. However, Xanax is distinguished by its pharmacokinetics: it enters the brain very quickly and causes effects very rapidly. Critically, it is euphorogenic—it induces a euphoric "high," a sense of well-being, and a feeling that "special kiss." Dr. Saxon notes this is why people take it once, feel that sensation, and immediately crave it again. The brain becomes primed to want that sensation. Tolerance develops; users then escalate to feel the effect, creating a vicious cycle. This euphoria explains Xanax's explosive abuse as a recreational drug. A 2017 Bloomberg article noted Xanax was name-dropped in rap songs as frequently as Hennessy, Rolex, and Air Jordan. Street names proliferated: Zannies, Z bars, Zan bars, handlebars, totem poles, blue footballs, bicycle parts, yellow boys, white boys, school bus, footballs, planks. Recreational users often consume multiple pills in sequence to chase the high, dramatically increasing overdose risk. Moreover, Xanax eliminates the feeling of risk and fear, causing users' sense of caution to vanish entirely.

**Martha's Story: Long-Term Dependency and Withdrawal**

Martha McPhee, a 60-year-old author, experienced her first panic attack in 2006 while alone in her New York apartment—racing heart, feeling like she was dying of a heart attack, desperate to flee. A doctor prescribed Xanax; after a second panic attack in the subway, she took it and found immediate relief. Initially, she took small doses sporadically, but use escalated over months. She discovered that a "tiny bite" of Xanax helped her sleep when she woke mid-night with racing thoughts. Doctors reassured her that her use was benign. However, over 16–17 years, she became dependent. When a new doctor explained rebound anxiety—that anxiety doesn't disappear when you take Xanax but rather accumulates and returns with vengeance—Martha resolved to quit. She attempted tapering but switched to cold turkey. The withdrawal was brutal: she was "bouncing off the walls." However, she persisted, and after stopping, her anxiety eventually improved. She now reports being "a different person," much calmer. She manages anxiety through conscious living, avoiding pasta and chocolate before bed, and reframing restlessness as an opportunity to read rather than spiral into frustration—lessons aligned with Stoic philosophy and the advice of her cousin with ALS: "be conscious, be conscious."

**Prescription vs. Recreational Abuse and Counterfeits**

The CDC reported in 2016 that more people visited emergency rooms for non-medical (recreational) benzodiazepine use than for prescription opioid use. There are two categories of abuse: intentional recreational use (party drugs, chasing euphoria) and improper prescription use (taking as directed but in harmful ways). The second category is larger and quieter. Original benzodiazepine guidance stipulated use for only days or a few weeks; in 2018, however, half of all patients prescribed benzodiazepines took them for 2+ months. This long-term use creates profound dependency. Benzodiazepine-involved overdose deaths have exploded: from 1,320 in 2010 to 12,500 in 2021—a tenfold increase in 15 years. Over half involved concurrent prescription opioid use. An even darker threat emerged: counterfeit Xanax pills laced with fentanyl. Since 2021, the DEA has campaigned with the slogan "One pill can kill." Millions of fake pills circulate in the US and globally, often indistinguishable from legitimate products.

**The Withdrawal Crisis and Suicide Risk**

Withdrawing from long-term Xanax use is medically treacherous. Withdrawal symptoms—anxiety, agitation, insomnia, tremors—overlap almost perfectly with anxiety disorder symptoms. Patients often mistake withdrawal for a return of their original condition and resume use. The approved method is tapering: gradually reducing the dose over weeks or months, allowing the body to readjust. However, tapering often fails because not only do original anxiety symptoms rebound, they worsen beyond baseline—exacerbating the very condition the drug was meant to treat. Some patients, unable to wean off despite months or years of effort, have committed suicide. The recognition of this danger prompted the FDA to issue a black box warning (the strongest caution) on all benzodiazepine prescriptions in 2020. The warning explicitly states that benzodiazepines, including Xanax, expose users to risk of abuse, misuse, addiction, overdose, and death. The FDA also mandated that prescribers implement tapering protocols when discontinuing the drug after weeks or months of use.

**Current Medical Consensus and Alternative Treatments**

Modern psychiatric guidelines now explicitly advise against benzodiazepines for anxiety, especially long-term use. The American Psychiatric Association recommends psychotherapy, particularly CBT, as the gold standard. CBT teaches patients coping skills—mindfulness, present-moment awareness, thought restructuring—rooted in ancient Stoic philosophy. SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors), which take weeks to show benefit, are the preferred pharmacological option. Benzodiazepines may have a narrow role as short-term relief (1–2 weeks) while waiting for an SSRI to take effect, but many prescribers fail to enforce the time limit. Two weeks later, patients report feeling much better and resist discontinuation. Dr. Saxon notes the ideal approach is to address this expectation at the outset, yet it remains "very challenging to do so." Despite guidelines, benzodiazepines remain in use. Psychiatry has reduced prescribing rates, but general practitioners, who prescribe far more benzos by sheer volume, have been slower to shift practice. Overall benzodiazepine prescriptions fell from ~130 million in 1996 to ~90 million in 2019, a 30% decline—likely reflecting better awareness of risks. Yet the problem has shifted: as prescriptions fall, counterfeit, fentanyl-laced pills have proliferated, creating a new and potentially deadlier crisis.

**Conclusion: The Illusion of Magic and the Cost of Tranquility**

Xanax seemed magical: fast, effective, apparently safer than barbiturates, and culturally iconic. But the episode's central cautionary message is clear: if a treatment seems too good to be true, it probably is. Tranquility has costs and trade-offs. Xanax solved the immediate problem—panic attacks stopped, anxiety faded—but at the price of dependency, withdrawal dangers, overdose risk, and a generation of patients now struggling to escape a drug they were told was safe. The story mirrors the trajectory of barbiturates: initial celebration as a medical miracle, gradual recognition of catastrophic risks, and societal harm. The lesson extends beyond Xanax to all quick-fix pharmaceutical solutions: real healing requires discomfort, skill-building, and time—the slow work of conscious living and therapy rather than the illusion of a pill.
