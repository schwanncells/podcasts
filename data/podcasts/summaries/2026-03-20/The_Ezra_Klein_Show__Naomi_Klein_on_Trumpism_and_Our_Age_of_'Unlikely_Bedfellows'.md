# The Ezra Klein Show — "Naomi Klein on Trumpism and Our Age of 'Unlikely Bedfellows'"

**Host:** Ezra Klein. **Guest:** Naomi Klein (columnist, The Guardian; professor of geography, University of British Columbia; author of *Doppelgänger: A Trip into the Mirror World*; forthcoming co-author of *End Times: Fascism and the Fight for the Living World* with Astra Taylor).

## TL;DR

- **Doppelgängers and mirror worlds** — Klein developed the concept to describe how the pandemic scrambled traditional politics, creating "unlikely bedfellows" (pro-choice feminists aligning with right-wing populists, for example). The mirror world is a replica ecosystem of social media platforms, publishing, narratives, where ejected people end up living according to different rules.
- **Naomi Wolf's trajectory as political case study** — Wolf went from celebrated 1990s feminist author (*The Beauty Myth*) to COVID-era conspiracy theorist, publicly shamed for a factual error in *Outrages* (2019) before being embraced by Steve Bannon's coalition. She became a vector for medical misinformation about vaccine shedding, infertility, and bioweapons, reaching suburban women voters.
- **Bannon's "MAGA plus" coalition** — Strategically peeling off angry unionized men (2016) and later suburban women, conspiracy theorists, and wellness influencers. Bannon platforms conspiracies deliberately to distract from real conspiracies he's implicated in, particularly the Epstein files.
- **Diagonalism as political realignment** — Wellness California types (focused on bodily purification) forming alliances with national border hardeners. RFK Jr. exemplifies this: anti-corporate rhetoric masking integration into a MAGA administration; he speaks powerfully to nature connection and alienation from the natural world, which fascist-adjacent movements historically exploit.
- **Silicon Valley's role in the Trump administration** — Tech oligarchs backed Trump partly as a "tech revolt" against AI and crypto regulation; this included reversing restrictions and opposing state-level regulation. Marc Andreessen and others saw their power threatened by employee activism and mild accountability (MeToo, regulation attempts).
- **Epstein files reveal true power dynamics** — Billionaires and oligarchs, including Bannon, were in contact with Epstein and envy his unabashed impunity. Their fury at MeToo, ESG, and accountability measures stems from fears of losing the rules-exempt status they believed was their due.
- **Opportunities for the left through contradictions** — Trump's coalition is collapsing on immigration (ICE now targeting all Latino people, not just "certain people"), AI deregulation (opposed by the base), and endless wars. Movements like Minneapolis's "neighborism" and NYC's Mamdani campaign showed 10% of Trump voters can be reached through economic populism, climate action, and appeals to dignity.
- **Data centers and AI as new political flashpoint** — Cross-partisan organizing against data centers in Tucson and elsewhere; people describe it as a "spiritual war." Fundamental questions about what humans and AI are "for" remain unanswered by institutions; public has been excluded from this debate entirely.

**The Doppelgänger Concept and Mirror Worlds**

Klein introduces the German concept of the doppelgänger—a double who uncannily mirrors oneself—as a lens for understanding political realignment. The book emerged when she was personally confused online with Naomi Wolf during the pandemic's second year, a confusion that became a recurring left Twitter joke. But rather than dismissing this as trivial, Klein realized it spoke to something deeper: the destabilization of identity and the fracturing of political space into mirrored, self-contained worlds with their own rules, narratives, and social media platforms.

The mirror world isn't simply "the other side." It's where people land after ejection from mainstream institutions and social networks. Klein observes that liberals and leftists, having concentrated power in media, academia, and government over decades, relied on shunning as a political tool—pressing mute, blocking, banning. But this created invisible blindness. When Wolf was banned from Twitter for conspiracy theories, liberals celebrated her disappearance, unaware she had become a regular on Bannon's show, building a parallel ecosystem they couldn't see. Klein describes this as akin to children believing the world disappears when they close their eyes.

**Naomi Wolf's Public Shaming and Reinvention**

Wolf's transformation from celebrated 1990s feminist intellectual to 2020s conspiracy luminary illustrates Klein's thesis about ejection and reinvention. Wolf's 1991 book *The Beauty Myth* was canonical third-wave feminism; she advised Al Gore's campaign. But in 2019, she published *Outrages*, a book about persecution of gay men in England, and made a foundational factual error. She misinterpreted the historical term "death recorded"—a bureaucratic category allowing judges to abstain from pronouncing sentence on capital convicts—as evidence of state executions. This was exposed live on the BBC. The public shaming was savage and total; every time Wolf stumbled online, people said "thoughts and prayers" to Naomi Klein.

Klein notes this pattern repeats: when examining why people "changed," they often experienced public humiliation first—sometimes for something genuinely wrong. What happens next matters. Wolf found a world where facts matter far less. She pivoted to platforms where she could speak without fact-checking—Bannon's show, Tucker Carlson appearances. Bannon explicitly weaponized her as a vector to deliver suburban women voters concerned about vaccines, bodily autonomy, and health. During the pandemic, she promoted claims about vaccine shedding, infertility, and bioweapons. Research tracing medical misinformation about vaccine shedding found much of it traced back to Wolf precisely because she was coded as an authority on women's health.

**Bannon's Coalition Strategy and Real Conspiracies**

Klein observed Bannon building what he called "MAGA plus"—a deliberately constructed coalition of unlikely allies. In 2016, he peeled off white unionized men angry at free trade. By 2021–22, he was targeting suburban women, particularly those engaged with wellness culture and concerned about bodily autonomy. Bannon explicitly introduced Wolf with language celebrating her as a "renegade" and "rebel" who had broken with elite orthodoxy, positioning her as a credible voice for women whom Trump struggled to reach.

Klein argues Bannon platforms conspiracy theorists strategically because outlandish beliefs distract from provable conspiracies he's personally implicated in. The Epstein files reveal Bannon in email contact with Epstein in 2019, casually forwarding him a Daily Beast story about "alleged sex rings" and then pivoting to another ask: "my guy's going to Israel. Can you meet with Ehud Barak?" Bannon's current strategy involves flooding the zone with alternative conspiracies—the Muslim Brotherhood hijacking elections in various states—while ignoring the biggest conspiracy in which he's centrally implicated. What makes Bannon's Epstein connection significant isn't just scandal; it reveals his true alignment. He was interested in Epstein as a potential funder for the Populist International, a network of far-right and openly fascist parties globally. The impunity Epstein embodied—living by no rules, no accountability, pure power—is precisely what fascinates Bannon.

**Diagonalism: Unlikely Bedfellows Unite**

The term "diagonalism," borrowed from German scholars Quinn Slobodian and William Callison analyzing the German anti-lockdown movement, describes coalitions that don't fit the left-right horseshoe. Instead of polar opposites, it's liberal wellness influencers (focused on bodily purity, bunkering) aligning with national border hardeners. These groups seem incompatible until you recognize the shared underlying impulse: control over one's own body/borders, distrust of institutions, rejection of externally imposed rules.

RFK Jr. exemplifies this. He entered 2024 as a fringe Democratic candidate with a powerful anti-corporate message ("we declare independence from Big Pharma, Big Tech, Big Ag, the military contractors") and wound up as Trump's HHS secretary. His appeal spans wellness communities and populist right figures because he speaks eloquently about alienation from nature—his childhood memories of peregrine falcons nesting on the White House roof, the bird's extinction from DDT. This is not mere nostalgia. It's a spiritual wound, a story about loss that he can narrate better than most politicians. Klein notes that fascist movements historically exploited this exact wound—the 1920s and 1930s European fascists were deeply invested in back-to-nature rhetoric and mysticism. The difference in the contemporary moment is that wellness culture, previously coded as leftish or apolitical, can now mobilize as a political force precisely because the institutional left has lost the language to speak to spiritual unease and disconnection from nature.

**Silicon Valley's Injury and the Tech Revolt**

Klein and Ezra explore the injury that drove tech oligarchs into Trump's camp. Marc Andreessen, elevated to near-messianic status in the 1990s (appearing on Time's cover at 23, seated on a golden throne), faced something he experienced as existential threat: employee activism. Quiet quitting. Workers demanding a say in what they built, particularly regarding ICE contracts and military AI work. Workers unionizing. Mild regulation of crypto and AI felt, to Andreessen, like terrorism. This is where inequality of power becomes visible: Andreessen interprets basic accountability as existential attack because he came of age in an era when being rich meant being treated like a god.

The tech oligarchs bankrolled Trump partly as a "tech revolt"—a way to prevent AI and crypto regulation that they see as existential threats. This overlaps with another injury: Epstein. The Epstein files show how billionaires, including those now in or close to power, envied his unabashed lawlessness. He didn't have to pretend to care about climate, ESG, or social responsibility. He had wealth and kept it. The files reveal conversations where powerful men grapple with MeToo as an existential threat—they're asking Epstein, who is a sex criminal, for advice on surviving accountability movements. This suggests that contemporary fascism, at least in this tech-oligarch iteration, is fundamentally a reaction to the brief moment when the most powerful people on Earth faced marginal accountability.

**What Is Fascism for Elites? A Counter-Revolution Against Accountability**

Ezra and Klein discuss whether "fascism" is the right word. Klein says it is. Historically, fascism emerges in response to injuries to powerful people—World War I losses for Germany and Italy's soldiers and industrialists. Contemporary Trumpism similarly arises from injuries to oligarchs and powerful men who experienced brief moments of accountability (MeToo, employee organizing, climate action pressure, antitrust talk). The counter-revolution isn't against leftist power but against the idea that billionaires should follow the same rules as everyone else.

The Epstein files prove this. Powerful men weren't necessarily aware of, or consenting to, Epstein's child sex trafficking. But they envied his refusal to play by any rules—his island, his connections, his utter lack of consequences. When Trump arrived promising to govern without pretense or constraint, it was deeply appealing to men who suddenly had to imagine a world where they couldn't act with total impunity.

**COVID as a Fork in the Road**

Klein and Ezra identify COVID-19 as a moment that crystallized the stakes. The pandemic offered two possible futures. One: a much stronger, more activist state—governments paying people to stay home, eviction moratoriums, universal healthcare gestures (free masks, testing), expanded unemployment. A hint of what a different economic system could look like. Two: accept mass death. Some people started asking whether pandemic deaths might actually be "culling," survival of the fittest, even preferable for the environment. This wasn't explicit ideology but an underlying current among some tech figures and right-wing voices.

The coalition that emerged around Trump unified against the first option. They needed government out of the way, deregulation, and a return to the rule that if you're rich enough, the rules don't apply.

**What Are Humans For? What Is AI For? Unanswered Questions**

Klein emphasizes that AI poses two profound questions that remain entirely unanswered in public debate. First: What are humans for? What do we value in education, work, creativity if AI can replicate these outputs? Second: What is AI itself for? So far, the only public answer is replacing white-collar workers—a profoundly uninspiring vision.

This is not a technical question. It's a political one. There's been zero public deliberation, no sense that society gets a say in how to orient trillions of dollars in investment. Companies like OpenAI promised to be Wikipedia-like, public-interest entities, then pivoted to for-profit. They're now fighting regulation and dumping money into super PACs. They won't allow themselves to be nationalized even though they benefit from the accumulation of all human knowledge.

The Trump administration chose "let it rip"—reversing even state-level regulatory capacity. This is wildly unpopular with Trump's base, who have organized cross-partisan coalitions against data centers in Tucson, Iowa, and elsewhere. Locals recognize that wasteful duplication of data centers (every major AI company building redundant capacity in an arms race for market dominance) consumes scarce resources, particularly water in arid regions. People describe these conflicts as spiritual wars.

**Possibilities: Neighborism, Economic Populism, and Rooted Politics**

Despite the darkness, Klein sees openings. Trump's coalition is imploding on multiple fronts. ICE raids targeting all Latino people, not just undocumented immigrants, have alienated significant portions of his base. AI deregulation opposes both the base and many elites. Endless wars weren't part of his campaign pitch. He broke his quantum superposition the moment he had to govern.

Klein points to emerging movements as alternative futures. Minneapolis's "neighborism" is welcoming, jargon-free, rooted in acts of mutual aid—doing laundry for shut-ins, trailing ICE agents and filming them, organizing around shared place and shared vulnerability. The Mamdani campaign for NYC mayor, though it lost, captured 10% of people who voted for Trump. His strength lay in economic populism ("the night shift," taxi drivers in debt peonage, essential workers) combined with real material promises: wage increases, sick days, dignity for workers society depends on.

Klein argues the left must return to climate action (currently absent from political discourse), economic populism (free public transit, addressed both climate and cost of living), and what she calls "eco-populism"—a politics rooted in care for place. She contrasts this with carbon markets, which are "bloodless"—they abstract the living world into financial instruments. What people actually respond to is being asked to cherish where they live, to know their land's history (including difficult histories of genocide and injustice), and to imagine futures rooted in that specificity.

**The Surveillance Reckoning**

Klein warns of an emerging threat: the integration of Silicon Valley with the Trump administration creating surveillance capacity beyond anything previously possible. The merger of Palantir with government, GROK's integration into war-fighting, data flows through DOGE—these represent systems of control being built in real time with minimal transparency. People are increasingly "touching grass" not just because they like nature but because they're becoming afraid of devices that have flipped from promising liberation to enabling state surveillance and corporate control.

When subsequent administrations gain subpoena power and investigators begin examining this era, the revelations will likely be chilling.

**Restoring the Irreplaceable**

Klein ends with a moral argument. Billionaires seem incapable of enjoying what they have—Elon Musk never posts about normal human pleasures (pets, films, books), Bezos couldn't appreciate William Shatner's transcendent overview effect from space and just wanted to spray champagne. This failure to appreciate the irreplaceable connects to their willingness to replace universities, artists, writers with AI. She calls tech oligarchs "traitors to creation" for their refusal to cherish the natural world.

Pope Francis's encyclical on ecology offered what institutional environmentalism lost: a spiritual language for why the natural world matters. The left must recover language about dignity, irreplaceability, and the things that make life worth living—not as policy add-ons but as foundational to politics.
