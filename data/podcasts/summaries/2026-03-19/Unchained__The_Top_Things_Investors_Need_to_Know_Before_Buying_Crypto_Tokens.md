# Unchained — "The Top Things Investors Need to Know Before Buying Crypto Tokens"

**Host:** Laura Shin. **Guests:** Ryan Yee (founder, Onchain Group; crypto VC investor and advisor to founders navigating token-to-equity transitions), Felipe Montalegre (co-founder and Chief Investment Officer, Theia; liquid token fund focused on long-term value investing in projects with revenue and fundamentals).

## TL;DR

- **Across Protocol retiring ACX token via equity swap** — Risk Labs foundation proposed converting the DAO-governed protocol to a traditional company, retiring ACX tokens, and offering token holders a choice: exchange tokens for equity, receive a cash buyout at 4.375 cents (25% above recent trading price), or hold the token. Motivation: institutional and enterprise partners require enforceable contracts and stable counterparties that DAOs cannot easily provide.
- **Token-equity split is a structural liability** — When a team or protocol has separate token and equity holders, equity holders (often VCs) can siphon value away from token holders, creating misaligned incentives. This "token equity problem" has repeatedly undermined long-term token investors and is described as existential for liquid token funds.
- **Multiple tokens from one team destroy investor optionality** — Across Protocol (bridge) and UMA (oracle) held separate tokens despite sharing infrastructure. Across TVL collapsed 80% from peak; UMA's core activity shifted to Polymarket prediction markets. Splitting one team's output into multiple tokens means investors lose exposure to future successful products launched under new tokens, whereas traditional equity companies can launch products post-IPO without diluting shareholder optionality.
- **Institutional growth requires centralized decision-making** — B2B partnerships, especially with enterprises moving on-chain, require enforceable contracts negotiated by a single counterparty. DAO governance forums cannot move fast enough; proposals that should take one day often take weeks of forum posts, temp checks, and votes. Competitors with centralized leadership ship faster and win deals.
- **Token-versus-equity is now a choice, not a forced split** — Regulatory clarity under the new US administration has lifted constraints that previously forced teams to maintain separation between equity and tokens. Teams like Uniswap and Aave have unified their structures around tokens; others like Across are consolidating around equity. The trend reflects founders regaining agency in capital structure decisions.
- **DAOs excel at accountability but fail at execution** — Decentralized governance imposes controls and prevents theft effectively, but becomes politicized, slow, and bureaucratic. Token holders form political alliances; the loudest voices dominate. Trading hours to decide on simple resource allocation. By contrast, established fintech companies compete globally with centralized leadership and rapid decision cycles.
- **Across's move signals fairness and converts aligned holders** — The token price doubled immediately post-announcement and stabilized near the offer price (4.375 cents), suggesting the market perceives fair terms. Founders gain a DAO exit strategy that converts long-term believers into equity holders while maintaining moat and focus; token holders gain direct equity upside and liquidity certainty.
- **Circle's Axelar acquisition and Tensor deals are cautionary contrasts** — Previous M&A deals extracted development teams while leaving token holders exposed (or wiped out). Across's tender-offer model—allowing token holders to convert to equity or receive cash—represents the ethically sound alternative that will likely become standard as token holder rights gain legal weight.

**Tokens Versus Equity: The Core Structural Problem**

Felipe Montalegre frames the token-equity split as a "leaky bucket" for token holders. When a protocol or team has issued separate tokens and equity, previous investors who hold equity can extract value away from token holders through strategic decisions, new fundraising, or spin-offs. Theia, a liquid token fund betting on on-chain businesses with revenue and real fundamentals, has encountered this problem repeatedly despite careful diligence. Every other liquid fund has hit the same wall. The issue is existential for long-term token investing because it undermines the basic premise: that owning a token gives you economic upside aligned with business growth.

Ryan Yee adds that this split also creates optionality destruction for equity investors. When one team launches multiple products with separate tokens, equity holders in the parent company lose exposure to future successes. If Product A issues Token A and then the team launches Product B with Token B, an equity holder in the original entity has no automatic claim to Product B's upside. By contrast, in traditional finance, an investor in Amazon equity benefits from AWS, launched eight years post-IPO. Apple's equity holders benefit from the iPhone, despite it being developed decades after IPO. Multiple tokens per team abandon this model, forcing investors to chase the "hot new token" rather than holding a diversified portfolio of management optionality.

**The Across Protocol Case: A Fairness Benchmark**

Risk Labs Foundation's proposal sparked the conversation. After acknowledging that Across (a bridge protocol) and UMA (an oracle protocol) share infrastructure but have diverged in importance—Across now represents less than 10% of UMA's activity and has lost 80% of its TVL—the team proposed retiring ACX tokens and converting the DAO to a private company. Token holders would receive three options: convert to equity (at terms to be disclosed post-KYC), receive a cash buyout at 4.375 cents per token, or hold the token and stay invested in a non-protocol entity.

The token price doubled immediately on news of the proposal, then settled back to approximately the offer price, a signal the market interprets as fair terms. Montalegre notes this price action is a useful heuristic: if token holders are getting a bad deal, the token should fall below the offer price. If they're getting a good deal or gaining clarity, it should trade above. The fact that ACX buyers bid it above the offer price suggests the market perceives real upside in the equity conversion or sees downside avoidance as valuable.

Ryan Yee frames the tender offer as "anti-dilution" for believers in the team. Token holders who convert to equity eliminate overhang from liquid, speculative traders, concentrating ownership among long-term believers. The team sheds the operational burden of managing a public token market, listing relationships, and market-making arrangements—costs equivalent to an early-stage startup going public. Meanwhile, the protocol gains a tighter counterparty for B2B deals. Yee notes this is effectively a selective IPO exit for the most-aligned token holders.

**Why Institutions Reject DAOs**

Across Protocol's stated motivation was blunt: institutional and enterprise partners will not sign long-term contracts or strategic partnerships with DAOs. The team cannot unilaterally commit resources, enter revenue-sharing agreements, or guarantee enforcement. Even simple B2B deals require multiple forum posts, temperature checks, and governance votes. Hart Lamper (Across's head) highlighted the team's pivot to agentic payments and noted that securing new partnership deals requires "contracts and out-of-protocol payments, which are functions not well suited for DAOs."

Felipe emphasizes that institutional capital is moving on-chain, and those partners require centralized decision-making. If you're a fintech company or institutional player, you do not want to negotiate with five DAO stewards working at different organizations. You want one counterparty. The competitive advantage of centralization is undeniable: Morpho (a lending protocol competing directly with Aave) deliberately consolidated control under a central team paired with a non-for-profit entity holding the Morpho token. The result: Morpho's performance and valuations have outpaced competitors still wrestling with DAO overhead.

**DAO Governance: Bureaucracy Without Accountability Teeth**

Stani Kulechov (Aave founder) crystallized the DAO problem in a recent essay: "proposals that should take a day often take weeks of forum posts, temperature checks, and multiple votes. Meanwhile, your competitor ships." DAOs become politicized. Token holders form alliances. The loudest voices dominate. The result is bureaucracy with less accountability than corporations, not more. Felipe notes that DAOs are excellent at imposing controls and preventing direct theft (which is why they were invented), but they fail at the flip side: execution and competition.

Ryan counters that the root issue is resource allocation. A DAO can ask for a lump-sum budget upfront, but retroactively, token holders often feel the team squandered capital. Alternatively, teams request resources piecemeal, requiring constant governance approval. Neither scales. By contrast, a CEO has full discretion to spend resources within a board-level budget. DAOs impose consensus costs on every operational decision, killing speed.

Both guests agree the future is likely centralized leadership with periodic token-holder checkpoints on major decisions (capital allocation, CEO replacement, acquisitions, liquidations). Metadao's model—allowing token holders to trade on proposed governance outcomes, with the business choosing the outcome that maximizes token price—is a credible way to align CEO incentives with long-term token holder returns without requiring daily governance.

**Token Versus Equity: Regulatory Clarity Unlocks Choice**

Ryan and Felipe both emphasize that regulatory uncertainty under the prior Gensler administration forced teams to maintain separation between token and equity. Lawyers advised against public language associating teams with their tokens, creating the absurd situation where a team issued a token but then denied it had any relationship to the token. This wasn't founder choice; it was legal compulsion.

The new regulatory environment has lifted that constraint. Teams like Uniswap and Aave have consolidated their token-equity structures, choosing unified tokens as the primary instrument. Across is choosing equity. Derive is recommitting to tokens. Derive published formal language stating "the token is at the center of our business, there is no competing equity, everybody is paid in token," a representation that carries legal weight for investor committee decisions.

Ryan notes this is the most underappreciated shift in 2026. Founders regained the agency to make sound business structure decisions instead of having structure imposed by regulatory fear. The downstream effect: real signal on founder conviction. If a team chooses tokens despite regulatory clarity, it means they believe in token economics. If they choose equity, it reflects institutional growth stage.

**Cautionary Tales: Axelar and Tensor**

Circle's acquisition of Axelar's development team while leaving token holders stranded, and Tensor's similar structure with Coinbase, are contrasts to Across. In those cases, the acquirer took the IP and team (housed in the equity entity) while token holders faced potential value loss. Felipe is direct: "If you promised anybody that the token will benefit from business growth, you should do what Across did" and allow token holders a tender offer.

Circle and Tensor's model—which became common in the prior regime—could no longer survive legal scrutiny. If a team has represented that tokens hold economic rights, lawyers will now flag token holder lawsuits as a real liability if M&A excludes them. Across becomes the template: fair conversion terms disclosed post-KYC, cash option for those who prefer certainty, and equity conversion for believers.

**Optimal Future: Accountability Plus Speed**

Felipe's closing argument is that crypto's true innovation is global, decentralized capital raising—not governance. Traditional finance has roughly 8,500 public companies for a global population; most founders cannot access public capital without San Francisco VC connections. Tokens unlock that. But the industry has been "stuck in the mud of the token problem" for five years instead of unlocking that vision.

The solution is balance: centralized leadership for execution (backed by proper accountability mechanisms like regular equity-like reporting, predictable token holder checkpoints, and verifiable resource allocation) paired with token economics for global optionality. Ryan agrees, framing crypto's end state as "a token at scale"—a company globally capitalized through tokens, with professional management, but without the traditional finance gatekeeping.

Both guests note the irony: Bitcoin and Ethereum, the most successful tokens, operate with either anonymity (Satoshi) or soft power (Vitalik), not governance bodies. Token scaling is solved when founders and teams earn legitimacy through execution, not votes.
