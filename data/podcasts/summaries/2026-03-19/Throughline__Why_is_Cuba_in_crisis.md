# Throughline — "Why is Cuba in crisis?"

**Host:** Rund Abdelfatah, Ramtin Arablouei. **Guest(s):** Eloy Viera (lawyer and journalist, El Toque; born in Cienfuegos, Cuba; left in 2019 after multiple detentions; writes for independent outlet critical of the Cuban regime, now based in Canada); Lillian Guerra (Cuban-American history professor, University of Florida; family remained in Cuba); Maria De Los Angeles Torres (professor of Latin American and Latino Studies, University of Illinois Chicago; born in Cuba; has written extensively on U.S.-Cuba relations).

## TL;DR

- **Cuba's worst economic crisis in decades is driven by both external pressure and internal failure** — three island-wide blackouts in four months by mid-March 2026, triggered in part when the Trump administration blocked Venezuelan oil shipments that had supplied Cuba 100,000 barrels per day in exchange for Cuban doctors.
- **The 1959 revolution had broad popular support initially** — Castro's land reform affected only 3% of landowners but dispossessed massive U.S. sugar companies that operated "like states within states," paying workers in company scrip redeemable only at company stores; by December 1960, without ever declaring himself communist, Castro controlled 80% of the economy.
- **The U.S. embargo, imposed in 1962, was explicitly designed to sow "hardship and disenchantment among the Cuban people"** — it cut off food, spare parts, and consumer goods the island depended on, and a version of it remains in place 64 years later alongside at least eight CIA plots to assassinate Castro.
- **The Cuban government ran what critics call its own "internal blockade"** — using "Committees for the Defense of the Revolution" as neighborhood surveillance networks, nationalizing all small businesses so that even hiring a plumber became a crime, and tolerating a black market cronyism called "sociolismo" (buddy-ism) that skimmed rations for personal profit.
- **The 1991 Soviet collapse triggered Cuba's "Special Period"** — blackouts stretched to 20 hours a day, hunger spread, and in 1994 roughly 35,000 Cubans fled on homemade rafts; the government briefly legalized private businesses and foreign investment, then reversed those openings once the crisis eased.
- **Cuba's government chose repression over liberalization after the Soviet collapse** — Torres argues this decision is the core structural reason Cuba faces its current crisis; if a different path had been taken in 1991, the island would not be where it is today.
- **The Obama "Cuban Thaw" briefly connected Cuba to the world** — after Obama's visit, the government opened public Wi-Fi hotspots and mobile internet for the first time; Trump's 2016 election reversed the opening, which Viera calls "one of the main or worst frustrations of my generation."
- **Cuba's 2021 protests were crushed** — around 1,000 people were detained, hundreds remain imprisoned today, and the conditions that fueled those protests have only worsened since.

**Cuba's Current Crisis**

Cuba in early 2026 is suffering blackouts, food shortages, and long gas lines — three island-wide blackouts in four months by mid-March. The immediate trigger is the Trump administration's decision, after the U.S. capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, to block Venezuelan oil from reaching Cuba and threaten tariffs on any third country that steps in. Venezuela had been supplying Cuba 100,000 barrels of oil per day. Without it, Cuba's already fragile infrastructure is buckling. Eloy Viera, a Cuban lawyer and journalist based in Canada, describes relatives in Cienfuegos cooking on charcoal — a large bag of which sells for 1,500 Cuban pesos on the informal market, more than a quarter of a monthly wage. Cubans on the island speak about "la cosa" — the thing — in a kind of code to avoid appearing critical of the government: "How's the thing? It's super high, it's super expensive, it's super bad."

**The 1959 Revolution and Its Early Promise**

In January 1959, Fidel Castro overthrew Cuba's dictator Fulgencio Batista and was widely celebrated as a national hero. His first major act was land reform designed to break up large foreign-owned sugar plantations. History professor Lillian Guerra notes the reform technically affected only 3% of landowners, but those included massive U.S.-owned operations running "like states within states" — with toll roads, private port facilities, and company scrip redeemable only at company stores. The economic vision was nationalist and capitalist, not initially communist. But Castro moved quickly to nationalize the press, then foreign-owned industries, then all domestic medium- and large-scale businesses. By December 1960, without ever publicly declaring himself communist, he controlled 80% of the economy — "communist in all but name," as Guerra puts it.

**The U.S. Embargo and the "External Blockade"**

The Bay of Pigs invasion in 1961 — a CIA-organized force of Cuban exiles — failed catastrophically, and Castro declared the revolution socialist. Cuba aligned firmly with the Soviet Union, and in 1962 the U.S. imposed a sweeping trade embargo explicitly aimed at sowing "hardship and disenchantment among the Cuban people." Guerra describes the immediate impact as disastrous: food, spare parts, clothing, and consumer goods that Cubans had relied on vanished overnight. The Cuban Missile Crisis the same year nearly triggered nuclear war; its resolution required the USSR to remove missiles from Cuba in exchange for a U.S. pledge not to invade. The embargo, however, stayed — and a version of it continues today. The CIA also plotted to assassinate Castro at least eight times, and 64 years of economic sanctions have persistently limited the island's options.

**The Internal Blockade: Repression and Economic Mismanagement**

Alongside the U.S. embargo, the Cuban government imposed what some critics call its own "internal blockade" — restricting economic autonomy and crushing dissent. The regime created "Committees for the Defense of the Revolution," mass organizations of neighbors tasked with surveilling and silencing complaints. Their role, as Castro described it, was as "tapabocas" — mouth-shutters. A black market economy called "sociolismo" — a play on "socialismo" and "socio," meaning buddy — became endemic: loyalists controlling ration distribution skimmed half the allotments to sell under the table, enriching a connected class while ordinary Cubans went short. In 1970, Castro mobilized the entire country for a 10-million-ton sugar harvest, "La Zafra de los 10 millones," stripping resources from the rest of the economy. It fell short and devastated other sectors. By 1971, Guerra notes, 20% of Cuba's workforce wasn't even showing up to work. The government subsequently nationalized all remaining small businesses — "even hiring a plumber" became a crime requiring state intermediation — while factories that had emerged to substitute for embargoed imports were shut down.

**The Special Period: Soviet Collapse and Forced Opening**

When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, Cuba lost its primary trade partner and economic lifeline overnight. Blackouts stretched to 20 hours a day. The public health and education systems, both dependent on Soviet aid, were crippled. In 1994, roughly 35,000 Cubans took to the open sea on homemade rafts in a desperate 90-mile attempt to reach Florida. The government — reversing years of ideological principle — was forced to legalize some private businesses, invite foreign investment, and develop the tourism industry. But as professor Maria de los Angeles Torres describes it, the arrangement enriched the state rather than workers: foreign companies paid the Cuban government $500 per worker per month while the worker received only $50. As soon as the worst economic pressure eased, the government scaled back private business opportunities and returned to central control. Torres argues this pattern — opening only under duress, then closing again — reflects a government that prioritized political control over economic development.

**Venezuela, Remittances, and How Cuba Survived**

In the early 2000s, a new lifeline emerged through Hugo Chávez: Venezuela sent Cuba 100,000 barrels of oil per day in exchange for Cuban doctors and professionals — Cuba's so-called "white coat army" deployed to Guatemala, Brazil, Honduras, Haiti, and elsewhere. Studies estimate professional leasing brought in billions annually. But Torres says the government didn't reinvest that money in infrastructure: roads deteriorated, potholes grew, and the energy grid was neglected — contributing directly to the blackout crisis now. Simultaneously, growing waves of Cuban emigration in the 1990s forged closer ties between the diaspora and those on the island. Unlike the first wave of exiles who went 20 years without contact, Cubans who left after the Special Period maintained active family relationships. Remittances became a crucial economic pillar — and, as Torres notes, meant Cubans increasingly felt their relatives were taking care of them rather than their government, shifting loyalty away from the state.

**The Obama Thaw and Its Reversal**

Barack Obama's presidency brought the "Cuban Thaw" — the most significant diplomatic opening in over 50 years. Obama relaxed travel and trade restrictions, became the first U.S. president to visit Cuba since 1928, and allowed U.S. businesses to invest in Cuba's telecommunications sector. The government, responding to the changed political climate, opened public Wi-Fi hotspots and allowed mobile internet access for the first time — a major shift for a population long limited to a heavily censored state intranet. Viera describes the cultural impact as profound: Cubans packed into public parks, screaming into phones to reach relatives abroad, expressing "that hunger... to feel part of the world." But Trump's 2016 election reversed everything — travel restrictions returned, U.S. investment dried up, and hardliners within the Cuban government were re-empowered. Viera calls it "one of the main or worst frustrations of my generation."

**2021 Protests and Ongoing Repression**

In 2021, compounded by pandemic-era shortages, Cubans took to the streets in what observers called the most astonishing display of mass dissent in decades. Crowds demanded food, medicine, and vaccines, but their loudest cries were calls to end the communist regime. The government responded with mass detentions — around 1,000 people were arrested, and hundreds remain imprisoned today. The regime continues to filter and monitor internet use, targeting activists who spread dissent on social media. The conditions that fueled the 2021 protests have only worsened since, leading directly to the current crisis.

**Who Is Responsible? The Embargo vs. Internal Failures Debate**

All three guests push back against framing U.S. embargo responsibility and Cuban government responsibility as an either-or question. Guerra calls it "a very comfortable paradigm for both the Cuban government as well as its detractors." She also argues that the embargo has paradoxically generated "cottage industries" that give the Cuban military financial interests in keeping it in place — making the military a faction that now actually opposes lifting it. Torres argues that if Cuba had chosen economic liberalization after the Soviet collapse in 1991 instead of repression, it would not be in its current position; the government feared political change and sacrificed economic productivity to preserve centralized control, producing a system where "Cubans are more equally poor than they are equally rich." Viera closes with the view that what happens next will ultimately be determined by "the reaction of the Cuban people" — if the regime falls, it will be because Cubans take to the streets and "start a new revolution."
