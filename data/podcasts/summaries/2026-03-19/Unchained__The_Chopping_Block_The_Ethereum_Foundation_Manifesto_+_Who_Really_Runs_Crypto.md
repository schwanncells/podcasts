# Unchained — "The Chopping Block: The Ethereum Foundation Manifesto + Who Really Runs Crypto?"

**Host:** Haseeb Qureshi (head hype man, Dragonfly Capital). **Guests:** Tom Schmidt (DeFi maven, master of memes); Tarun Chitra (giga brain, Gauntlet); Taylor Monahan (security expert, formerly Metamask); David Hoffman (crypto connoisseur, Zara super state).

## TL;DR

- **The EF Mandate (March 13, 38-page document) reaffirms cypherpunk values via CROPS:** Censorship Resistance, Open Source, Privacy, Security. Claims the Ethereum Foundation should measure success by becoming "increasingly invisible" — a "walkaway test" where Ethereum functions even if the Foundation disappears.
- **Two opposing reactions split the Ethereum community:** Cypherpunks and EF insiders praise it as a true reaffirmation of Ethereum's soul; pragmatists (including Haseeb and David) read it as "backsliding" — Ethereum prioritizing sanctuary tech over growth and adoption during a time when competitors like Solana aggressively court builders.
- **The crux: EF isn't saying what it *will* do, it's saying what it *won't* do.** The document signals Ethereum is optimized for financial censorship resistance and authoritarian edge cases (not an economic engine), positioning Ethereum as Galadriel's light in Lord of the Rings — not very bright, not many features, but unbreakable when you need it most.
- **Builder retention crisis:** New startups rarely choose Ethereum mainnet; they pick Base, Arbitrum, or Solana. Most major success categories (perps, prediction markets, stablecoins) are not on Ethereum L1. Builders cite better ecosystem support and active messaging from other foundations as differentiators.
- **EF communications paradox:** The Foundation has improved dramatically at comms and founder support over 2023–2025 (startup profiles, VCs at Frontier Tower, backing promising DeFi projects). Yet this document contradicts those efforts by reaffirming values-first, growth-agnostic positioning.
- **Solana's role as the shadow catalyst:** Solana's success over the past 18 months forced Ethereum's hand, but Ethereum community rarely acknowledges it. Ethereum lost ground to Solana and other ecosystems; strategic response is needed, not just reaffirmed ideology.
- **Stewardship vs. permissionlessness tension:** Some panelists (Tay) argue builders don't need EF support — Ethereum's permissionlessness is enough. Others (Haseeb, David) argue EF stewardship of growth and founder morale is a responsibility the Foundation shirks by publishing this document.

**The EF Mandate: Cypherpunk Reaffirmation or Strategic Retreat?**

On March 13, the Ethereum Foundation released a 38-page mandate document introducing the acronym CROPS (Censorship Resistance, Open Source, Privacy, Security) to describe its core mission. The document emphasizes the "walkaway test" — Ethereum must be functional even if the Foundation disappears — and positions the EF's role as increasingly invisible and unnecessary over time. This sparked immediate debate.

The pro-mandate faction (cypherpunk maximalists and EF insiders) welcomed it as a necessary reaffirmation that Ethereum is fundamentally about censorship resistance and neutrality, not become a financial engine to compete with traditional systems. Vitalik Buterin underscored the document, signaling his backing. The anti-mandate faction (Haseeb, David, Tom) read it differently: as a statement of what Ethereum *won't* be, deployed at precisely the wrong moment. The pragmatists note that a year ago the EF was messaging "Ethereum is all things to all people" (the Infinite Garden concept) and pushing startup adoption; now it's reaffirming values that were never in doubt, while the broader industry is asking Ethereum to "ship" and compete for builder mindshare.

David framed the document as a dog whistle: Ethereum is optimizing for sanctuary technology — Galadriel's light from Lord of the Rings, which is dim, has few features, but works perfectly when you need to escape oppression. This is incompatible with building an economic engine. Tom added that the document feels like "an empty mission statement" released on a "random Tuesday" without new concrete commitments, despite the EF having improved at comms significantly over the prior two years. Tarun observed an "ossification" pattern: enough early parties are satisfied with the status quo that meaningful innovation stalls; the document felt reminiscent of Bitcoin's hardening cycle from optimism to rigidity.

**Why This Matters: The Growth Question and Competitive Pressure**

Haseeb emphasized the real problem: Ethereum is losing the marginal builder. When he asked the panelists where startups are launching today, the consensus was Base, Arbitrum, or Solana — *not* Ethereum mainnet. If he sampled DeFi Alliance startups, most would choose somewhere other than L1. Meanwhile, institutions launching real-world assets (RWAs) and major capital deployments still prefer Ethereum L1, creating a bifurcation: suits on Layer 1, chaos elsewhere.

The document is being interpreted as an EF signal that growth is not their responsibility. Tom noted this is a structural problem: when the EF abdicates growth leadership, the market fragments into competing standards (like the rollup wars), each pushing its own agenda with no coherent direction. Haseeb compared it to a politician saying "I'm cutting taxes and you don't want poor people, right?" — conflating disagreement about strategy with disagreement about values.

**The Solana Unspoken Elephant**

Notably, no panelist mentioned Solana by name until late in the conversation, yet it loomed throughout. Haseeb pointed out that Solana's aggressive push one year ago forced Ethereum to confront its market position. Many users entering crypto in the past 18 months never touched Ethereum mainnet; they discovered Ether as a token to buy, not as a place to build. Ethereum leadership understands this (Tomas DiMaio, the prior EF leader, would explicitly say "we are learning from what Solana Foundation did well"). Yet the community avoids discussing it, defaulting instead to virtue-signaling about decentralization. Haseeb argued this is self-defeating: you don't get to advance your values for free — you advance them by winning. If Ethereum loses the market to Solana or other chains, the cypherpunk values don't matter because no one is using them.

**EF Internal Factions and Communication Failures**

Haseeb noted that the EF is itself decentralized; there is no single CEO and there are genuine disagreements inside the organization. Recent hires (under Tomas and the transition to Bastian) focused on growth, founder support, and go-to-market. Not everyone in the EF agrees with the mandate document; some view it as a mistake, others as necessary. Additionally, the EF has demonstrably improved at communications and founder engagement since 2023 — it now profiles startups, connects VCs, attends conferences to support builders, and hires people who pitch startup opportunities. This creates a contradiction: the organization is doing growth-oriented work while the leadership signals a values-first, growth-optional mission.

**The Pragmatist Counter: Support Builders Without Shilling Tokens**

Tom offered a concrete suggestion: the EF should tweet milestones from successful Ethereum projects (Morpho, Aave, Lido, Morpho) and contextualize them through CROPS language. For example: "$1 billion in censorship-resistant assets deposited into Morpho via Ethereum." This way, the EF affirms its cypherpunk values *and* supports builder success. The EF's Twitter has instead highlighted obscure projects with minimal users or has gone quiet, which has symbolic weight in an attention economy.

**The Tay Rebuttal: Permissionlessness is the Feature**

Tarun Chitra pushed back with a different framing: builders don't actually need the EF's blessing. The greatest Ethereum success stories — Compound, Uniswap, DeFi Summer, NFTs — happened *without* EF encouragement and sometimes despite EF skepticism. Ethereum's real strength is that it permits anyone to build anything and no authority can stop you. If the EF wants to do cypherpunk research, let them. Meanwhile, builders will go do their thing and Ethereum will continue to be used. The implication: Ethereum doesn't need EF cheerleading to survive.

Haseeb largely agreed with Tarun's point *in principle* but noted it's empirically weak: when Solana's foundation and other ecosystem teams actively support builders and Ethereum's does not, marginal startups choose Solana. The EF's disposition and culture *do* cascade through the community, even in a permissionless system. Five years ago, when Ethereum was the only smart contract game in town, you could afford to be indifferent; today, you cannot. Additionally, Haseeb argued that the problem with EF leadership abdicating growth is that it creates a vacuum filled by competing interests (each L2, each protocol builder, each VC) all pushing their own agenda, leading to fragmentation and reduced coherence around what helps Ethereum as a whole.

**The "Sanctuary Tech" vs. "Economic Engine" Dichotomy**

David crystallized the tension: Ethereum has two roles. The soul is Ethereum's commitment to CROPS values, censorship resistance, and remaining a backstop against authoritarianism. The body is the economic engine — Uniswap, Aave, Morpho, USDC, USDT, Lido — that pays the bills, generates MEV for staking incentives, and attracts capital. The EF document, David argued, is essentially saying: "We care about the soul and very little about the body." But the flywheel depends on both. When the EF signals that growth and adoption are not its concern, it's placing the burden of the body entirely on builders and market forces, which is a choice but comes with consequences.

**Industry Virtue Signaling and the Integrity Problem**

Haseeb took issue with the rhetorical judo employed by pro-mandate voices: "Are you against decentralization? Are you against privacy?" This conflates disagreement about implementation with disagreement about values. Everyone on the call came to crypto for the cypherpunk mission; the debate is about *how* to advance those values in a competitive market. The virtue-signaling response ("if you disagree, you don't believe in decentralization") shuts down legitimate strategic critique.

**The Long-Term Cycle and Ossification Risk**

Tarun observed a pattern: Bitcoin started optimistic and experimental, but by 2015 it had ossified into a defensive posture, focused on not breaking things. He worried Ethereum would follow the same trajectory — accelerated ossification where early participants become satisfied with the status quo and resist change. Once enough stakeholders are entrenched, innovation stalls. The EF Mandate felt to him like an early sign of that calcification, made worse by the document's abstract language (CROPS, sanctuary, walkaway test) without concrete shipping milestones.

**A Reminder: Builders Don't Compete for EF Approval**

Late in the conversation, Haseeb clarified an empirical point: when he talks to startup founders choosing where to build, they are not primarily shopping around for "who won't rug me." They are asking "who will help me?" and "where are my users?" Base and Arbitrum attract builders because Coinbase and Offchain Labs are actively pulling levers. Solana attracts builders because the Solana Foundation spent heavily on incentives, events, and public support. Ethereum mainnet no longer has that pull; builders are not leaving Ethereum out of fear of rug risk — they're leaving because the alternatives offer more momentum and visibility.

**Conclusion: The Fight Over Meaning and Signal**

The debate is not really about whether CROPS values matter (everyone agrees they do) or whether Ethereum should be a sanctuary (everyone wants that too). The fight is over whether the EF, as a steward of a 2+ trillion-dollar ecosystem, has a responsibility to communicate *and enable* growth alongside values preservation. The document is being read as a message that EF does not want that responsibility, which ripples through the market as a cultural signal that Ethereum is okay with slow adoption and marginal builder recruitment. Whether that reading is fair or accurate is beside the point; the signal is what builders and institutions receive, and signals shape behavior.

Haseeb's closing framing: "Words matter. Communication matters." The EF has shown it can improve (2023 to 2025 was a dramatic shift). The hope is that the internal factions advocating for growth and builder support will prevail, and the next communication will harmonize values affirmation with explicit growth commitment. If not, the market will continue its bifurcation, with Ethereum mainnet serving large institutional players and other chains capturing the long tail of experimental builders.
