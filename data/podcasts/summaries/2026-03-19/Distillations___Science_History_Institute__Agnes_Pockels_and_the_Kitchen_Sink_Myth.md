# Distillations | Science History Institute — "Agnes Pockels and the Kitchen Sink Myth"

**Host:** Alexis Pedrick. **Guests:** Brigitte van Tegelen (Director of International Affairs, Science History Institute; former research fellow; specializes in women and couples in science, including Agnes Pockels); Don Opitz (associate professor, DePaul University, with affiliations in the School of Continuing and Professional Studies, the History Department, and LGBTQ Studies; historian of science); Petra Mieschnick (retired professor, Technical University of Braunschweig).

## TL;DR

- **Agnes Pockels published a letter in Nature in 1891 at age 29** — forwarded by Lord Rayleigh (John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh, Nobel Prize in Physics 1904), who recognized her homemade instrument as superior to what he was using and asked her to build him one.
- **The "discovery while doing the dishes" story is almost certainly false** — historians note her family's class would have had domestic staff, and the story obscures the fact that Agnes was already a self-educated scientist who approached the kitchen sink with deliberate scientific intent.
- **Agnes built the Pockels trough from household materials** — including an old pharmacist's balance, a button as a measurement disc, and a scale; the instrument became the basis for the Langmuir-Blodgett trough, which helped Irving Langmuir win the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1932.
- **Agnes likely shaped her brother Fritz scientifically, not the other way around** — as the elder sibling by two to three years, she was the more formative influence during their teenage years; he shared university textbooks with her, but the intellectual exchange went both ways.
- **In 1893, Agnes was invited to use the University of Göttingen lab but declined** — her parents were chronically ill from malaria contracted in Venice, and as the daughter it was her social duty to stay; but she also would have lost freedom of inquiry at an institution where someone else sets the agenda.
- **Working outside an institution gave Agnes an advantage** — she spent a decade refining her instruments and experiments at her own pace, on her own topics, while her brother Fritz struggled under academic obligations and unfashionable research directions.
- **Agnes defined the Pockels Point** — the minimum area a single molecule can occupy in monomolecular films — and published 14 papers, receiving the Laura R. Leonard Prize from the Colloid Society in 1931.
- **The dishwashing trope is "damaging"** — van Tegelen argues it delivers the message that "the best female scientists were able to" do both housework and science, which undermines women in science; she calls for the story to be "washed away from the literature."

**Agnes Pockels and the Science of Surfaces**

Agnes Pockels worked in surface science — the study of the boundary where two phases of matter meet, such as liquid and gas. These interfaces are highly reactive and the outermost molecules behave in unique ways. Dish soap reduces the surface tension of water to reach grease; the same principles underlie catalytic converters, computer chips, water filtration, and medical implants. Agnes was particularly interested in how impurities like oil or soil affect water surfaces, and how measuring surface tension could reveal their behavior.

Her 1891 letter to Nature, forwarded by Lord Rayleigh with a cover note calling her "a German lady working with some homely appliances," described original experiments she had conducted at home. Rayleigh wrote that her results on contaminated water surfaces were valuable — and he meant it. He immediately asked if she could build a copy of her instrument and send it to him. She declined, explaining she had built it "petit à petit, step by step," but offered detailed specifications. Rayleigh ultimately adopted her methods in his own further research.

**The Kitchen Sink Myth**

Nearly every online source about Agnes tells the same story: she became interested in surface science by watching soapy water behave while doing the dishes. Don Opitz and Brigitte van Tegelen both push back on this. Opitz notes that the Pockels family home was a "pretty prominent looking, substantial looking house" whose class position would have been associated with domestic staff — calling into question how much dishwashing Agnes actually did. More fundamentally, the story frames her discovery as a passive eureka moment, like Isaac Newton being struck by a falling apple. Petra Mieschnick argues Agnes was already a careful, educated observer who "became aware that little things other people would overlook mean something" — making it far more likely she went to the sink with scientific intent, not that the sink accidentally gave her science.

**Agnes's Early Life and Self-Education**

Agnes Louise Wilhelmine Pockels was born in 1862 in Venice, then part of the Austrian Empire, where her father served in the army. When he contracted malaria, the family relocated in 1871 to Braunschweig in the newly formed German Empire. Agnes attended the Municipal High School for Girls, where the curriculum focused on languages, textile work, music, and literature — skills for managing a household and hosting educated conversation, not scientific training. She pursued science on her own initiative, driven by genuine curiosity.

Her younger brother Fritz, two to three years her junior, studied physics at the University of Göttingen and shared his textbooks with her. The conventional assumption is that Fritz opened the door to science for Agnes. Van Tegelen challenges this directly: as the elder sibling, Agnes was the more formative influence during their teenage years, and may have influenced Fritz's choice to pursue science in the first place. Mieschnick describes their relationship as "a very balanced and warm exchange" in which Fritz "fully respected her research and her knowledge."

**The Pockels Trough and Its Legacy**

By age 19, Agnes was planning her own experiments. She built an instrument — now called the Pockels trough — from materials around the house: an old pharmacist's balance, a metal bar to split the water surface into two sections, a button to measure the force required to pull it from the surface, and a scale. Mieschnick calls it "really a sophisticated tool" despite its humble origins. With it, Agnes discovered that when you compress a surface film of oil, it reaches a threshold at which it "behaves suddenly physically differently" — what is now known as the Pockels Point, the minimum area a single molecule can occupy in monomolecular films.

The instrument she built became the prototype for the Langmuir-Blodgett trough, the tool Katherine Burr Blodgett and Irving Langmuir used at General Electric. Langmuir's work in surface chemistry won him the Nobel Prize in 1932. The episode is produced as a joint installment with the Lost Women of Science podcast, whose new season focuses on Blodgett — making Agnes the origin point for that scientific lineage.

**Home Lab, Amateur Science, and Freedom of Inquiry**

Agnes conducted all of this work in her home, and the episode pushes back against the modern instinct to read that as a strike against her. In the 19th century, "amateur" science — pursued out of love rather than employment — was not a pejorative label. Lord Rayleigh himself was an amateur scientist and proud of it; he later retired early from his professorship at the Cavendish Laboratory to return to experiments in his own manor. When he described Agnes's work, he recognized a kindred spirit doing the same kind of domestic-scale, privately funded investigation he valued and advocated for.

Van Tegelen goes further, arguing that university labs are not automatically superior environments for science. They impose goals, pacing, and topic selection from above — and history includes many cases where "the boss said this is not interesting" and a real discovery was suppressed. By staying home, Agnes "was able to just live with her own topic" and "devote 10 years to improve her instruments," free from the academic obligations that burdened Fritz. She published 14 papers in total and was recognized in 1931 with the Laura R. Leonard Prize from the Colloid Society, shared with French botanist Henri Deveau.

**The Dutiful Daughter Decision**

In 1893, as German universities began opening to women, Agnes was invited to use the lab at Göttingen. She declined. Her parents — whose health had never recovered from their years in malaria-plagued Venice — were ill and needed care, and as the daughter, the social expectation was that she would stay. The episode resists framing this as straightforward oppression. Her parents did not simply forbid her from going; Agnes felt a sense of duty. Fritz was never expected to fill the same caretaking role.

The gendered dimension is real — "of course it applies," van Tegelen acknowledges — but Agnes also had examples in her own family of independent women's lives: an aunt who was a painter, lived in Paris and Berlin, and never married. Agnes tried at least once to find independent housing, which suggests she felt the tension. She also recorded bouts of vertigo and headaches in her diary and spent time in a sanatorium. Her niece and sister-in-law later described her as having stood "like a soldier" at her post until the end — a phrase that takes on added weight given that her father was a military man.

**Agnes's Legacy and the Problem With the Story We Tell**

The episode closes by asking what it means to succeed as a scientist. Agnes never held an official appointment, never won a Nobel, and never ran a university lab. But she invented an instrument that downstream enabled Nobel-winning work, defined a concept still bearing her name, and published 14 papers — all from home, in her spare hours around caregiving responsibilities.

Van Tegelen's sharpest critique is reserved for the dishwashing trope itself. Neither Lord Rayleigh nor Irving Langmuir has ever been imagined discovering surface science at the kitchen sink. The story is "a very gendered stereotype" and, worse, it carries the implicit message that women can — and should — do both: manage the household and produce science. "I think this is a very damaging message," van Tegelen says, adding that she "really wish that this dishwashing thing would be washed away from the literature sooner than later." Mieschnick describes Agnes as someone who "followed her own path," forged her own connections, and "was not afraid to act for herself" — without needing to be cast as a rebel or a victim to make the story worth telling.
