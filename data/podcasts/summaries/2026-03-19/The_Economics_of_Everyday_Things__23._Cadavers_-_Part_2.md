# The Economics of Everyday Things — "23. Cadavers - Part 2"

**Host:** Zachary Crockett. **Guest(s):** Philip Guyette (former body broker; ran a for-profit cadaver parts operation from the early 1990s until 2006, when he was convicted of wire fraud and sentenced to eight years in federal prison).

## TL;DR

- **No license required to buy, dissect, and sell human bodies** — Guyette had no medical degree, no funeral director's license, and no formal training of any kind when he entered the industry in 1993.
- **A single cadaver yields $5,000–$10,000 when split into parts** — individual prices: a severed head fetches $500, a foot $350, a spine $300, with additional revenue from organ tissue segments (brain lobes, heart, lung).
- **The biggest buyers are medical device companies** — 3M, Johnson & Johnson, and Stryker pay body brokers to supply surgical workshops at medical conventions, where surgeons test new tools on real human parts; Guyette was paid $500 per head and $5,000 per day to staff rhinoplasty workshops of 250 heads.
- **Free cremation serves as the primary donor incentive** — average U.S. cremation costs ~$2,000; body brokers absorb that cost in exchange for the right to dissect and sell the remains, a deal Guyette says is especially attractive to low-income families.
- **Consent forms are deliberately vague** — they specify "research use" but typically don't disclose that a body will be cut apart and sold to multiple buyers; families often imagine someone in a lab coat looking at a slide, not a head being removed with Home Depot tools.
- **Metal recycling from cadavers generated $350,000–$500,000 per year** — Guyette harvested cobalt and titanium from hip and knee implants left behind after cremation, plus $25,000–$50,000 per month from dental gold.
- **Guyette was convicted in 2006 after selling diseased tissue as healthy** — prosecutors said more than 120 patients received contaminated transplant tissue, exposing them to potentially deadly risks; he served eight years in federal prison.
- **Congress is considering a registration bill** — it would require body brokers to register with the federal government and follow rules on labeling, packaging, and disposal, but would not eliminate the industry.

**How Guyette Entered the Business**

In the early 1990s, Guyette was working as a land surveyor when a back injury forced him into less physically demanding work. An acquaintance connected him with a Southern California medical school that needed help sourcing cadavers for anatomy classes — the program had 250 students and needed around 80 bodies per year, but had few donors of its own. Guyette began visiting UCLA, USC, UC San Diego, and UC Irvine to collect excess cadavers from their storage. He noticed that some program directors at those schools drove Lexuses and wore three-piece suits despite holding the same title he did; they were quietly running side businesses, approaching universities with cash offers for individual body parts. Guyette learned that researchers and medical device companies would pay far more for specific parts than academic institutions spent on whole bodies.

In the early 2000s, Guyette left the university job and opened his own body donation firm in Las Vegas.

**The Market for Body Parts**

The regulatory landscape Guyette operated in has a sharp distinction: live organ and tissue donation for transplant is heavily regulated by federal agencies, with extensive health screening. But body parts used strictly for research face no equivalent oversight — there is little or no screening, and no vetting of buyers. Guyette found demand across a wide range of use cases: cadaver dog training courses requested tissue in various stages of decomposition; pharmaceutical and device companies needed whole limbs, joints, and spines; researchers ordered brain segments (parietal lobe, frontal lobe, occipital lobe), heart, and lung tissue.

The largest recurring buyers were medical equipment manufacturers staging workshops at medical conventions, where surgeons try out new instruments on real human anatomy. These events, hosted in hotel ballrooms at venues like Caesars Palace in Las Vegas, could require hundreds of specimens. Larger body donation companies eventually built their own auditoriums and teaching facilities to keep the workshops in-house.

**Finding Donors and Obtaining Consent**

To source bodies, Guyette ran ads in obituary sections of local newspapers and cultivated relationships with hospice workers and hospital nurses — the people who interact with families at the moment of death. He described offering gifts and running raffles to maintain those relationships. Some body brokers paid funeral homes referral fees for steering families toward donation.

The pitch to families centered on free cremation. Guyette acknowledged the tension inherent in approaching grieving, often financially stressed families: "Sometimes a family will say, oh God, this came at a terrible time. No one has money. My husband just lost the job. And then that kind of gives an opening." Consent forms, he noted, are broad enough to permit nearly anything — they mention research but don't specify dismemberment or the sale of parts to multiple commercial entities.

**The Work Itself**

Guyette had no medical training. He described being able to disassemble a body in 15 minutes, bag the parts, label them with ID numbers, and load them into chest freezers. The tools came from Home Depot. Sterility was not a concern for research-grade tissue — everything was double-bagged and frozen fresh, then shipped in hard-shell Costco or Sam's Club coolers packed with dry ice via FedEx or similar carriers.

The cremation process itself opened a secondary revenue stream. Hip and knee implants left behind after cremation are made of cobalt and titanium; funeral homes typically discarded them, but Guyette recycled the metal and earned $350,000–$500,000 per year doing so. Dental gold added another $25,000–$50,000 per month. His summary of the economics: "The whole human body, it just ends up being a freaking commodity."

**Scandals and the Industry's Pattern of Abuse**

Guyette describes his own case as one instance in a recurring pattern. In one high-profile recent case, the director of Harvard's body donation program was indicted for selling parts from cadavers donated to the university; one client allegedly purchased two dissected faces for $600 and used them to make dolls with human skin. A Phoenix body donation firm that misled families about how bodies would be used settled a civil lawsuit for $58 million. In Colorado, two funeral home directors were charged with selling tissue from people who had paid for cremation services — billing families for cremation, then dismembering and selling the body instead.

Guyette noted that the deception can extend to the ashes returned to families: when a body is heavily harvested, only a rib cage may remain, and families receive a pound or a pound and a half of remains. He said this creates pressure on operators to pad the ashes with filler.

**Guyette's Conviction and Aftermath**

In 2006, after relocating his operation to North Carolina, Guyette came home to seven news vans parked outside. He had crossed the line that separates legal body brokering from criminal conduct: he was selling tissue for live transplants — which is federally regulated — and had altered medical records to pass off diseased samples as healthy. Prosecutors said more than 120 patients received the contaminated tissue, with potentially fatal exposure risks. He was convicted of wire fraud and sentenced to eight years in federal prison.

Guyette does not minimize his culpability: "It's completely my fault for what I did." But he observes that his case has not deterred the industry — "everyone else just thinks they can do it better." He is now back to land surveying in California, having learned options trading from white-collar inmates during his sentence. He noted, with no apparent irony, that nothing legally prevents him from returning to the body parts trade today.
