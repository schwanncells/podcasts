# The Indicator from Planet Money — "How Iran is wasting American resources"

**Host:** Wailonn Wong, Darian Woods. **Guest:** Jerry McGinn (director, Center for the Industrial Base, Center for Strategic and International Studies; expert on military supply).

## TL;DR

- **Iran's drone-swarm strategy vs. U.S. expensive missiles:** Iran fires thousands of cheap drones (costing $4,000–$50,000 each) to force the U.S. to shoot them down with multi-million dollar Tomahawk and Patriot missiles, depleting U.S. munitions supplies.
- **U.S. drained 10% of its Tomahawk inventory in the first three days** — forcing the Pentagon to cannibalize missile defense systems from South Korea and the Indo-Pacific to resupply the Middle East.
- **America's bias toward "exquisite weapons"** — high-precision, handmade systems that limit collateral damage but take years to produce at scale; the U.S. military loves them because they're effective and don't risk friendly forces.
- **Iran has weaponized economics:** Its distributed drone factories (with two backup sites each) and export network (supplying Russia, Sudan) create sustained production capacity; the U.S. is struggling to match volume.
- **The Pentagon is learning:** deploying anti-drone cannons instead of expensive missiles, building a $35,000 "Lucas drone" modeled on Iran's Shahed 136, and planning to buy 200,000 drones by 2027.
- **McGinn's prescription:** shift from ultra-high-end weapons to mass-produced low-cost munitions; allow Congress to fund production over multiple years (currently only permitted for high-value weapons); invest in scale and capacity now as "insurance" for future conflicts.

---

**The Asymmetric Economics of Modern Warfare**

The war between the U.S., Israel, and Iran has exposed a stark economic imbalance. Iran fires waves of unmanned aircraft—Shahed 136 drones, roughly go-kart sized with wide wings and propellers—that cost thousands to tens of thousands of dollars to manufacture. The U.S. responds with Tomahawk cruise missiles and Patriot air-defense systems costing millions per unit. McGinn explains that this disparity is partly by design: Iran has weaponized its low-cost production capability, deliberately launching large drone barrages to force the U.S. to expend its far more expensive inventory.

The strategy is working. In just the first three days of the current conflict, the U.S. depleted roughly 10% of its total Tomahawk stockpile. The shortage has become so acute that the Pentagon has begun stripping missile-defense systems from South Korea and the Indo-Pacific to reinforce Middle Eastern deployments. This reveals a structural vulnerability: the U.S. military's decades-long preference for high-end, precision weapons—what McGinn calls "exquisite weapons"—has left it ill-equipped for a grinding war of attrition against low-cost drones.

**Why America Loves Expensive Weapons**

The U.S. bias toward exquisite weapons has deep roots in military culture and technology strategy. These systems are extraordinarily precise—a Tomahawk can hit a specific building, limiting collateral damage. They're devastatingly effective. And crucially, they don't put American soldiers or allies at personal risk. Presidents across administrations have favored them for these reasons.

But exquisite weapons are inherently slow to produce. At a Tomahawk factory, workers in white lab coats manually drill and assemble components. High-end jets and ships take years to prototype and manufacture. Ramping production is nearly impossible without massive capital investment and workforce expansion. McGinn notes that the manufacturing base for these weapons is simply not designed for speed—it prioritizes precision and reliability over volume. The bureaucratic structure of military procurement reinforces this: Congress can approve multi-year funding for high-value systems but not for cheaper, mass-produced munitions.

**The Pentagon's Emerging Response**

In response to the drone crisis, the U.S. military is beginning to shift tactics. Instead of using million-dollar missiles against every drone, the Pentagon has deployed anti-drone cannons—basically automated gatling guns that can shred drones at a fraction of the cost. These have proven remarkably effective; very few Iranian drones are getting through.

The Pentagon is also copying Iran's playbook. It announced the Lucas drone, a $35,000 unmanned aircraft modeled directly on the Iranian Shahed 136, and deployed it operationally in the current war. More ambitiously, the Pentagon announced a "drone dominance initiative" aimed at procuring 200,000 drones by 2027. This represents a fundamental shift away from the exquisite-weapons paradigm toward quantity and speed.

**Building Capacity for Future Conflicts**

McGinn argues that the U.S. needs a more radical restructuring of its munitions strategy. Rather than betting everything on a handful of ultra-high-end weapons, the Pentagon should invest in mass-producing affordable, effective munitions across multiple manufacturers. Congress should extend multi-year funding authority to cheaper systems, allowing private contractors to invest in production infrastructure and tooling without the risk of annual budget fluctuations. This approach would cost more in any given year but would function as insurance—building surge capacity so the country can scale up production rapidly in future conflicts.

The core insight is that modern warfare increasingly rewards volume and industrial capacity over individual weapon brilliance. Iran has built a distributed manufacturing network specifically designed to survive targeting and maintain output; the U.S. is starting from behind but beginning to invest in that mindset. Whether the Pentagon can overcome decades of institutional preference for exquisite weapons fast enough remains an open question.
