# Throughline — "The confederates who left the USA"

**Hosts:** Randa Abdel-Fattah and Ramtin Araблui. **Guests:** Luciana Brito (history professor, Federal University of Reconcavo de Bahia, Brazil); Sunny Dossi (retired professor of geography and director of the Institute for Latin American Studies, Auburn University, Alabama; co-author of *The Confederados: Old South Immigrants in Brazil*).

## TL;DR

- **Up to 10,000 Confederate emigres fled to Brazil after the Civil War** — seeking to preserve white supremacy and slavery after the South's 1865 defeat.
- **Brazil was the last country in the Western Hemisphere to abolish slavery** — ending it in 1888, over two decades after the U.S.; this made it the primary destination for Confederates unwilling to accept Reconstruction.
- **Brazil's emperor actively recruited white Americans** — offering subsidized land, paid travel tickets, and free hotel stays in Rio de Janeiro to attract settlers as part of a deliberate "whitening" campaign.
- **Doctor James McFadden Gaston's 1867 book *Hunting a Home in Brazil*** — published after six months of travel — promoted Brazil as a near-Garden of Eden where "the elements of society which have resulted from the mastery of the white man will never be erased entirely."
- **Race worked differently in Brazil, shocking the Confederados** — unlike the binary Black/white American system, Brazilian racial identity was shaped by physical appearance, wealth, and family, making it possible to move between racial categories.
- **Most Confederados returned to the U.S., but an enclave survived** — the town of Americana, São Paulo state, became their permanent settlement; by 1888, with slavery abolished, the remaining settlers assimilated, intermarried, and became Brazilians.
- **Today, some Americana residents reclaim the Confederate identity as heritage** — flying the Confederate flag not as advocacy for slavery, but as an "homage to their forefathers" who left in opposition to Reconstruction-era America.

**Why Confederates Left and Why Brazil**

After the Confederacy's surrender in 1865, the American South was devastated: farms overgrown, railroads destroyed, banks insolvent, and cotton prices falling. Nearly 4 million formerly enslaved people were now free, which Brito says created deep panic among white Southerners who feared retaliatory violence. The end of slavery shattered both the economic system and the social order they had built around white supremacy. Rather than adapt, many sought a country where that order still held. By the mid-1860s, slavery had been abolished across most of the Western Hemisphere — but not in Brazil, which held one of the largest enslaved populations in the Americas.

**Brazil's Imperial Welcome**

Brazil's emperor was simultaneously eager for white American settlers. The country was pursuing a deliberate policy of "whitening" its population — the emperor considered Brazil "too dark" and hoped European and American immigration would shift the racial balance. To attract settlers, he offered deeply discounted land prices, covered travel costs, and provided accommodation in Rio de Janeiro on arrival. Dossi notes the emperor also expected practical benefits: American agricultural technology was far more advanced, especially for cotton cultivation, and he hoped settlers would establish schools and introduce new techniques.

**Selling the Dream: Gaston's Book and Southern Press**

Word spread through several channels. Former Confederate soldiers who had scouted Brazil before the war published promotional accounts. The most influential was James McFadden Gaston, a Civil War doctor who spent six months in Brazil and published his findings in 1867. His book, *Hunting a Home in Brazil*, praised the soil's "dark reddish or brown color" as ideal for coffee, corn, beans, and cotton, and described the Brazilian empire as sharing the "character and sentiment" of the better class of Southern citizens. Similar accounts circulated in Southern newspapers. The settlers who went were not, Brito emphasizes, mainly wealthy plantation owners — most were ordinary farmers, tradespeople, and doctors who saw Brazil as a chance to acquire slaves and wealth for the first time.

**The Rude Awakening: Climate, Soil, and Race**

When the Confederados arrived, the promises fell apart. Dossi describes how the climate was hotter and more tropical than advertised, the soil poorer, and many crops failed to disease. The deeper shock was racial. The Confederados had come to preserve what they called "pure white blood," but Brazil's racial categories were entirely foreign to them. Portuguese colonization from the 1500s onward had produced extensive racial mixing between white settlers, indigenous peoples, and the large African enslaved population; by the 1800s, a "mestizo" category existed that the American binary system had no equivalent for. Brito notes that in Brazil, people with African ancestry could appear white and live as white, with race determined partly by phenotype but also by wealth and family connections. Confederado letters recorded their shock that the same family could contain "several shades of color" — something that violated the rigid racial logic they had emigrated to preserve.

**The Confederado Enclave and Americanа**

To maintain their identity, Confederados in Brazil spoke English at home, homeschooled their children in English, and maintained Protestant religious practice in a Catholic country. Many eventually gave up and returned to the United States. But a core community remained and founded what became the town of Americana in São Paulo state — named simply because it was where the Americans had settled. Dossi compares its cultural significance for Confederado descendants to Plymouth Rock for American Pilgrims. In 1888, Brazil abolished slavery, removing the central reason many had emigrated. Over subsequent generations, the remaining Confederados intermarried with Brazilians, adopted Portuguese, and were absorbed into Brazilian society.

**A Contested Legacy Today**

Some residents of Americana have recently reclaimed the Confederate identity, flying the Confederate flag and celebrating their ancestry. Brito describes this as a romanticized "fantasy about Confederate life." Dossi frames it differently: he says contemporary celebrants are not advocates for slavery but are honoring ancestors who left in protest against what the United States was becoming during Reconstruction — a distinction that reflects how thoroughly the Confederado story has been filtered through generations of assimilation and selective memory.
